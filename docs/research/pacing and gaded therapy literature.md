# Pacing and Graded Build-Up: Practitioner Craft Wisdom Meets the PEM Threshold

## TL;DR
- The OT/physio "craft" of pacing converges on one core idea — help patients find and live within a personal, fluctuating energy envelope using named tools (4/5 Ps, Spoon Theory, Energy Envelope, Managing Fatigue, Stop-Rest-Pace, Hours of Upright Activity, heart-rate/anaerobic-threshold pacing) — but most of these are accumulated clinical/practice wisdom and patient-derived experience, not RCT-proven interventions.
- The decisive line for a PEM app is **symptom-contingent vs time-contingent progression**: graded build-up that heals deconditioned, post-surgical, cardiac and post-ICU patients (progressive overload → upward adaptation) actively harms PEM patients, because in PEM the same repeated load *lowers* the functional threshold. Keller et al. (2014) measured day-1→day-2 decreases on a 2-day CPET of VO₂peak 13.8%, Work@peak 12.5%, and **Work@ventilatory-threshold 21.3%** (peak RER ≥1.1 confirming maximal effort), and Appelman/Wüst et al. (Nature Communications 2024) showed PEM "is associated with severe exercise-induced myopathy, local and systemic metabolic disturbances and infiltration of amyloid-containing deposits in skeletal muscles." NICE NG206 (2021) bars "fixed incremental increases" (GET) for exactly this reason.
- What transfers safely: envelope-finding, baselining below capacity, consistency/anti-boom-bust, proactive/pre-emptive rest, counting cognitive+emotional load, "change one thing and hold until stable," flexible up-or-down adjustment, and HR-threshold guardrails. What is actively dangerous to import: any fixed time-contingent increment, "push through," quota-regardless-of-symptoms logic, and age-predicted HR-max targets.

## Key Findings

**1. Pacing is a real, named clinical craft — but its evidence base is weak and contested.** A 2023 scoping review (Sanal-Hayes et al., *Journal of Translational Medicine*, DOI 10.1186/s12967-023-04587-5) found 17 included studies, of which only three were randomised controlled trials: "Eleven studies reported benefits of pacing, four studies reported no effect, and two studies reported a detrimental effect," concluding "the literature base is insufficient to inform treatment practises for people with ME/CFS and long COVID." So pacing is best characterised as clinical-practice consensus plus very strong patient preference, with limited formal trial support. This matters for honest app messaging.

**2. The frameworks cluster into "energy-conservation/symptom-contingent" (fatigue tradition) vs "operant/quota-contingent" (chronic-pain tradition) — and they are partly incompatible.** Energy-conservation pacing (Jason's Energy Envelope; OT fatigue management) aims to *reduce symptoms* by staying within perceived energy. Operant pacing (Fordyce 1976) aims to *increase function* by doing pre-set quotas *regardless* of symptoms. For PEM, the symptom-contingent envelope philosophy is correct and the quota-contingent philosophy is dangerous.

**3. The deconditioning-vs-PEM distinction is now well-evidenced and is the linchpin of the whole synthesis.** 2-day CPET, cerebral-blood-flow, and muscle-biopsy studies show PEM is a recovery/adaptation *failure*, not a fitness deficit — which is why progressive overload works in one population and harms the other.

## Details

### Part 1 — The major pacing/energy-management frameworks OTs and rehab practitioners actually use

**The "P" frameworks (4 Ps / 5 Ps / 3 Ps).** The most widely taught OT energy-conservation mnemonic. Commonly **Pacing, Planning, Prioritising, Positioning** (4 Ps), sometimes extended to 5 with **Pleasure/Permission** or **Pausing/Posture**. The UK Royal College of Occupational Therapists popularised a **3 Ps** version (Pace, Plan, Prioritise) in its COVID-era fatigue guidance, and the WHO has cited the 3 Ps in Long COVID patient materials. Origins trace to rheumatology and MS fatigue practice; clinicians themselves note they "can't find the original papers" — this is **accumulated craft wisdom**, face-valid and near-universal in handouts, not a trialled intervention. The naming is inconsistent across organisations.

**Energy Envelope Theory (Leonard Jason, DePaul University, late 1990s).** The most academically developed pacing model. Patients keep *expended* energy within *perceived available* energy; an "energy quotient" (expended ÷ perceived × 100) operationalises it. Jason's studies (2008/2009/2013) found over-exertion associated with worse fatigue, pain, disability and QoL, and that staying within the envelope correlates with better function. Importantly the envelope means avoiding **both over- and under-exertion** — "a comfortable range," not simply "do less." Evidence status: **observational/correlational support**, developed over a 15-year program; described by Jason himself as a rehabilitation-management strategy, not curative.

**Spoon Theory (Christine Miserandino, 2003).** Not a clinical invention — a lupus patient's diner metaphor (12 spoons = finite daily energy units; each task spends a spoon). It has been widely adopted as a **clinical teaching and communication tool** because it makes invisible energy limits legible to families and clinicians, and is now standard shorthand ("spoonies") in chronic-illness care. Status: communication aid / patient education, not a measurement system.

**Managing Fatigue / energy conservation course (Packer, Brink & Sauriol, 1995; Therapy Skill Builders).** The most rigorously studied OT energy-conservation program, developed substantially in **multiple sclerosis** fatigue. Originally a 6-week group course teaching ~14 energy-management strategies (changing positions, simplifying tasks, delegating, using adaptive equipment, planning/scheduling, strategic rest). Mathiowetz et al. (2001, 2005) RCTs in MS showed reduced fatigue impact and improved QoL; later adapted (teleconference, internet via REFRESH protocol) and applied to neuromuscular disease, TBI, Parkinson's. Status: **the strongest evidence-based OT fatigue program** — but its evidence is in MS-type fatigue, where graded activity is tolerated, NOT in PEM-dominant illness.

**Stop. Rest. Pace. (World Physiotherapy 2021; #MEAction #StopRestPace).** The core Long COVID safety message. World Physiotherapy Briefing Paper 9 (June 2021), led by Long COVID Physio board members (Darren Brown, Todd Davenport, Kelly O'Brien et al.), states that in the presence of post-exertional symptom exacerbation, "Stop. Rest. Pace," activity management, and heart-rate monitoring may be effective, and explicitly warns against graded exercise. #MEAction's campaign urges newly-ill post-viral patients to stop, rest and pace to prevent harm. Status: expert-consensus safety guidance.

**Hours of Upright Activity / HUA (Bateman Horne Center).** A practical tracking heuristic: total time per 24h with "feet on the floor" (sitting upright, standing, walking) vs feet elevated/horizontal. Drawn from clinical experience with over 1,000 ME/CFS patients (per Yellman et al., wearable HUA study): "Severely ill ME/CFS patients reported 0 to 4 h with their feet on the floor while moderately ill patients reported having their feet on the floor for 5 to 8 h," and <4 HUA correlated with significantly worse orthostatic intolerance and impaired standing/walking. BHC pairs this with "Pacing Upright Activity" (recumbent rest to manage orthostatic intolerance) and the "Crash Survival Guide" (the PEO acronym — Person, Environment, Occupation — for surviving a crash by maximising horizontal rest). Status: clinically-derived heuristic, increasingly validated (wearable HUA research underway).

**Heart-rate / anaerobic-threshold pacing (Workwell Foundation; Davenport, Stevens, Snell, VanNess).** Uses a heart-rate monitor with an alarm to keep activity below the **ventilatory/anaerobic threshold (V/AT)**, ideally measured on day 2 of a 2-day CPET. Rule of thumb when no CPET is available: avoid >2 minutes above V/AT; a common estimate is resting HR + ~15, or fractions of (220−age) — but Workwell explicitly **warns against age-predicted HR-max formulas** because chronotropic incompetence is common in ME/CFS, arguing for even lower thresholds. Status: objective, mechanistically-grounded pacing guardrail; CPET evidence strong, the HR-threshold pacing protocol itself is practice-based with emerging feasibility data (Clague-Baker et al. 2025).

**Activity diaries + activity analysis (core OT method).** The foundational data-gathering step: log activities by type/intensity/duration against symptoms (and sleep/rest) over 1–2 weeks to reveal the envelope and PEM triggers. NHS energy-management services structure the day around consistent anchors (fixed wake/sleep, meals, regular short rests) and classify activity as low/medium/high. Status: universal OT craft practice.

**Traffic-light / stoplight systems (Red-Amber-Green).** Activities colour-coded by energy cost: Green = restful/all-day; Amber = moderate, safe in moderation with breaks; Red = likely to trigger PEM. Some variants add Blue (energy-restoring). Widely used in NHS OT/physio and patient communities. Status: practice wisdom, no RCT base.

**Pacing apps.** Visible (2022) uses morning HRV/resting HR to set a daily energy "budget" and pairs with a wearable for real-time HR alerts; Bearable (symptom correlation), ME/CFS Pacing app (energy-currency logging), and others exist. Status: tools; efficacy not yet established in RCTs but Visible is feeding research (Imperial College London).

### Part 2 — How gradual build-up is actually structured (the rules of thumb)

Across traditions the practical heuristics are strikingly consistent:

- **Find the baseline first, and set it below capacity.** The baseline is the activity level sustainable on a *bad* day as well as a good one. Methods: the **50% rule** (when new to pacing or post-crash, cut activity roughly in half, stabilise, then probe upward) and the **80% rule** (once stable, habitually do ~80% of what you feel capable of, leaving a buffer for unanticipated stressors). Nijs's Activity Pacing Self-Management (APSM) similarly advises activity durations **25–50% below** self-estimated capacity, because patients systematically overestimate. The PACE trial's Adaptive Pacing Therapy used 70% of estimated capability.

- **Stabilise before progressing.** Universal rule: do not increase until symptoms are *consistently stable* at the current level for a sustained period (commonly framed as "a few days to a few weeks"; APSM requires a stabilisation phase of several weeks before any grading). Consistency on good AND bad days is the explicit anti-boom-bust mechanism. In Antcliff's APQ work, "activity consistency" emerged as the most beneficial of the five empirically-derived pacing themes.

- **Increase by small increments.** Common conventions: the **10% rule** (from exercise science/NASM — keep weekly increases ≤10%) and, in graded-activity/GET-derived programs, up to **20% per week**. NHS "building up your baseline" guidance uses ~10–20% increments (e.g., 5→6 minutes) and tests each new level over a **two-week period** before progressing, to catch *delayed* PEM.

- **"Change one thing and hold it."** Progress one variable at a time, wait several days to weeks to read the (delayed) symptom impact, and only then add another. World Physiotherapy frames the trigger for progression as "sustained stabilisation," not a calendar date. (Note: the specific "hold ~3 weeks" figure is common practice guidance but is not a NICE-numbered rule.)

- **Signals a step was too big.** The universal red flag is **PEM** — delayed (often 12–48h, sometimes days/weeks) symptom worsening and failure to return to baseline the next morning. Bateman Horne's benchmark: engage only in the amount of activity that doesn't induce PEM in 24–48h; the goal is to be "back to baseline the following morning." HR signal: exceeding the individual V/AT. HRV/resting-HR drift is also used (morning HR creeping >10–15 bpm above baseline signals over-exertion or impending crash).

- **Back off after a setback.** Return to (or below) the last stable level, rest aggressively, re-stabilise, then re-probe. NICE explicitly requires "recognising a flare-up or relapse early" and making "flexible adjustments (up or down)."

### Part 3 — The central tension: progressive overload vs envelope management

**Why build-up heals in deconditioning/post-surgical/cardiac/post-ICU contexts.** These rehab traditions rest on **progressive overload** — the SPORT/FITT principles, originating in DeLorme's WWII Progressive Resistance Exercise (1945–48; 3 sets of 10 reps at 10-rep-max) and codified by ACSM. They exploit the body's normal adaptive response (Selye's adaptation: apply a stress slightly above current capacity → recover → supercompensate → repeat at higher load). In **post-ICU syndrome (PICS)** and post-surgical reconditioning, deconditioning *is* the primary driver, so escalating load reverses it. Cardiac rehab (Phase III) titrates intensity by %heart-rate-reserve and Borg RPE; pulmonary rehab (COPD) breaks "the vicious circle of dyspnea–decreased activity–deconditioning" by starting at the highest tolerable level and slowly increasing duration/difficulty as endurance grows. In all of these, the second exposure reproduces or *improves* on the first — adaptation runs upward.

**Why the same logic harms PEM.** In ME/CFS and PEM-positive Long COVID the adaptive machinery itself is impaired, so escalating load runs *downward*:
- **2-day CPET evidence.** Healthy and merely-deconditioned people reproduce day-1 output on day 2; ME/CFS patients show a significant drop, especially at the ventilatory/anaerobic threshold. Keller et al. (2014, *J Transl Med* 12:104) found day-1→day-2 decreases of VO₂peak 13.8%, Work@peak 12.5%, and **Work@VT 21.3%**, with peak RER ≥1.1 on both days proving maximal effort; the same study found "a standard classification of functional impairment based on VO2peak or VO2@VT results in over-estimation of functional ability for 50% of ME/CFS participants" — the impairment is only revealed by the second test. Snell et al. (2013, *Physical Therapy*) found workload/VO₂ at VT discriminated ME/CFS from controls only on day 2.
- **Not deconditioning.** Van Campen, Rowe & Visser (2021) showed the abnormal orthostatic cerebral-blood-flow drop was present regardless of deconditioning severity (no difference between no/mild/severe deconditioning) — if deconditioning drove symptoms, the most deconditioned would be worst. Idiopathic chronic-fatigue patients *improved* on CPET2 (like controls), while ME/CFS deteriorated — the day-2 drop is specific.
- **Muscle-level damage.** Appelman, Wüst et al. (*Nature Communications* 2024, 15:17, "Muscle abnormalities worsen after post-exertional malaise in long COVID") biopsied Long COVID patients with PEM before/after maximal exercise and showed PEM "is associated with severe exercise-induced myopathy, local and systemic metabolic disturbances and infiltration of amyloid-containing deposits in skeletal muscles of patients with long COVID" — active exercise-induced injury, not disuse atrophy. The authors explicitly refute deconditioning: in their 2025 reply they state, "We refute that our findings are due to deconditioning… We demonstrated significant physiological differences in Long COVID patients with post-exertional malaise (PEM) compared to healthy controls, even at matched physical activity levels." A 2025 preprint comparing bed-rest controls found the muscle changes differ from those of deconditioning.

**How the field now draws the line.** NICE NG206 (29 October 2021) defines GET as "first establishing an individual's baseline of achievable exercise or physical activity, then making **fixed incremental increases** in the time spent being physically active" and recommends **not** offering it, nor any program based on "deconditioning and exercise avoidance theories." The decisive distinction is **who controls the increment**:
- **Time-/quota-contingent (GET, discredited for ME/CFS):** the *schedule* dictates the increase regardless of symptoms; symptoms are framed as a psychological barrier to push through.
- **Symptom-contingent / envelope-based (recommended):** the *body's symptom response* dictates whether/when/how much to change; activity is "guided by perceived symptom levels to avoid worsening symptoms" (World Physiotherapy 2021).

Crucially, NICE does **not** ban all increase — recommendation 1.11.13 permits a personalised program that establishes baseline, *reduces below it first*, "maintain[s] this successfully for a period of time before attempting to increase it," then makes "flexible adjustments… (up or down as needed)… while staying within their energy limits," with early flare-up recognition. This is "graded activity within the envelope," explicitly distinct from fixed-increment GET. It must be physiotherapist-overseen and is only for those who feel ready to progress.

**Contested / overturned.** GET and curative CBT were removed from NICE (2021) and GET from CDC (2017). The PACE trial (White et al., 2011) — which had reported CBT/GET superior to Adaptive Pacing Therapy — is heavily criticised (outcome-switching, recovery-threshold changes); patient surveys repeatedly report GET worsened symptoms. But note APT (the PACE pacing arm) is **not** the same as energy-envelope pacing — Goudsmit, Jason et al. argue APT was a flawed hybrid, which is why PACE found it ineffective while patients endorse envelope pacing. Jo Nijs's APSM occupies a contested middle ground: it includes a time-contingent grading phase and is rooted in operant conditioning, which many in the ME community reject for PEM.

### Part 4 — What transfers to a daily PEM pacing-tracker app (and what is dangerous)

**Safe and high-value to build in:**
- **Envelope/baseline finding** from a 1–2 week activity+symptom diary; set baseline *below* observed capacity (50%/80% logic) and to the bad day, not the good day.
- **Multi-domain activity logging.** Physical, **cognitive, emotional, and social** exertion all draw on the same budget and all trigger PEM — count screen time, conversation, reading, and stress as activity, not just steps. This is one of the most important and most-neglected heuristics.
- **Delayed-PEM detection.** Because PEM onset is delayed 12–48h+, correlate symptoms against activity from the *previous 1–3 days*, not the same day. Surface "back to baseline next morning?" as a key signal.
- **Consistency / anti-boom-bust nudges.** Flag good-day over-exertion ("feeling okay is not a signal to do more"); reward steady output over spikes.
- **Proactive/pre-emptive rest scheduling.** Prompt scheduled rest *before* fatigue appears and before known high-demand events; support "radical rest" (lying flat, dark, quiet) as distinct from screen-based "rest."
- **HR-threshold guardrails** (optional, if wearable): individualised V/AT or resting-HR-based ceiling with an alarm — explicitly NOT age-predicted HR-max. Morning resting-HR/HRV drift as an over-exertion early-warning.
- **"Change one thing, hold until stable, adjust up OR down."** Symptom-contingent progression logic with the default bias toward stability; make stepping *down* as easy and unstigmatised as stepping up.
- **Traffic-light tagging** of activities; **Hours of Upright Activity** as an orthostatic-load tracker.

**Actively harmful to import from progressive-overload traditions:**
- **Any fixed time-contingent increment** ("add 10% every week on schedule," streaks, escalating daily goals) — this is the GET mechanism that harms PEM.
- **"Push through" / "no pain no gain" framing**, step-count or exercise-ring targets, gamified escalation that rewards doing more regardless of symptoms.
- **Quota-contingent goals** that tell the user to complete a set amount irrespective of how they feel.
- **Age-predicted HR-max zones** (standard fitness-app default) — clinically wrong for this population.
- **Treating "good day" feeling as license to bank/spend more.**

The design north star: the app should help the user *stay within and gently map* a fluctuating ceiling, defaulting to stability and easy step-down — the opposite of a fitness app that defaults to progressive escalation.

## Recommendations

1. **Build the core loop around symptom-contingent envelope management, not progression.** Default the app to "find baseline → stabilise → maintain," with progression as an optional, user-initiated, *symptom-gated* feature that always offers step-down as prominently as step-up. Benchmark to change this stance: none — for PEM-positive users, never ship a default time-contingent escalation.

2. **Make delayed-PEM correlation the analytic centrepiece.** Implement rolling 1–3 day lag analysis between exertion (all domains) and symptoms, plus a "returned to baseline by next morning?" daily check. This is where a data analyst can add genuine value beyond existing apps.

3. **Count cognitive/emotional/social load as first-class activity.** Most trackers under-weight this; for PEM it is decisive.

4. **Offer HR-threshold pacing as an opt-in guardrail with the right defaults** — individualised (resting-HR-based or CPET-derived V/AT), never age-predicted max; surface morning resting-HR/HRV drift as an early-warning. Label clearly that CPET-derived thresholds are gold-standard and rules-of-thumb are estimates.

5. **Label evidence honestly in-app.** Distinguish (a) evidence-based (Managing Fatigue in MS; 2-day CPET as objective PEM measure; deconditioning-refutation studies), (b) practice wisdom (4/5 Ps, traffic lights, 50/80% rules, 2-week hold, Stop-Rest-Pace, HUA), and (c) contested/overturned (GET, fixed-increment progression, curative CBT, PACE-trial claims). Avoid promising that consistent pacing *expands* the envelope — Jason suggests it may over time, but this is not robustly proven, and over-promising recreates the GET harm of implying effort cures.

6. **Thresholds that would change the guidance:** if a user is *recovering* and a clinician confirms PEM has resolved (e.g., negative 2-day CPET pattern, no PEM after graded probes), cautious progressive build-up (cardiac-rehab style) becomes appropriate — but this is a clinical determination, not an app default, and should be gated behind explicit clinician input.

## Caveats
- **Pacing's own evidence base is thin** (Sanal-Hayes et al. 2023 scoping review: 17 studies, 3 RCTs, 11 benefit / 4 no effect / 2 harm, poor-fair quality, "insufficient to inform treatment practises"). Most of the "craft wisdom" here is exactly that — experience-based and strongly patient-endorsed, but not RCT-proven. This is what the user asked for, but it must be labelled as such.
- **Many numeric rules (10%, 20%, 50%, 80%, two-week hold) are practice conventions without head-to-head validation;** they are reasonable defaults, not established science.
- **The MS/cancer/cardiac/pulmonary/post-ICU literatures are only partly transferable.** Their energy-conservation *techniques* transfer; their progressive-overload *exercise* logic does not, because those populations largely lack the PEM threshold-lowering response.
- **Severity matters.** Per Solve M.E. and the 2015 National Academy of Medicine report, about 25% of people with ME/CFS are housebound or bedbound; for very severe patients even activity-management may be of limited use and aggressive rest dominates. An app should not assume a moderately-affected user.
- **The push-crash "cumulative sub-threshold erosion" pattern is clinically universal but never directly measured physiologically** — the day-2 CPET drop evidences single-effort threshold suppression, not multi-day accumulation. Treat the accumulation model as well-grounded clinical inference, not proven mechanism.
- Some sources here are grey literature (clinic handouts, advocacy orgs, commercial apps) rather than peer-reviewed; the Wüst 2025 bed-rest comparison is a preprint.
