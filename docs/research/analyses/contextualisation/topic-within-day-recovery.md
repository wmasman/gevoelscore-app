### Subclaim 1: "cardiovascular autonomic dysfunction is present in Long COVID populations relative to controls"

**External-consensus status: CONSENSUS-EXISTS** (multiple in-population Long COVID sources on cardiovascular autonomic dysfunction).

**Primary citation A (narrative-review anchor)**: [`marques_2023_lc_cardiovascular_autonomic_dysfunction.pdf`](../../literature/marques_2023_lc_cardiovascular_autonomic_dysfunction.pdf) (Marques, Quaresma & Falcão, 2023, *Front Cardiovasc Med* 10:1256512) — narrative review on cardiovascular autonomic dysfunction in Long COVID. From the Abstract: patients experience "a cardiovascular autonomic imbalance known as dysautonomia" with the underlying pathophysiological mechanisms candidate-linked to "inflammatory persistence" and immunological factors. HRV is framed as the primary autonomic-imbalance readout: "Heart rate variability can indicate autonomic imbalances in individuals suffering from Long COVID." Symptoms of cardiovascular autonomic dysfunction include "orthostatic intolerance, chest pain," and related cardiovascular symptoms. The review synthesises that Long-COVID cardiovascular autonomic dysfunction (dysautonomia) is a reproducible clinical entity linked mechanistically to persistent inflammation via HRV alterations and orthostatic-intolerance patterns.

**Primary citation B (24h Holter primary study anchor)**: [`mooren_2023_postcovid_hrv_autonomic_dysregulation.pdf`](../../literature/mooren_2023_postcovid_hrv_autonomic_dysregulation.pdf) (Mooren et al., 2023, *Sci Rep* 13:15814) — 24-hour Holter HRV in **103 post-COVID syndrome (PCS) patients vs 103 coronary-artery-disease (CAD) patients vs 90 healthy controls** (n = 296 total). Verbatim key findings from Abstract + Results: PCS patients showed significantly higher LF/HFpower + LF/HFnu + LF/HF ratio (24 h; p < 0.001) compared to controls, largely comparable to CAD patients but with **the highest sympathetic activation across all three groups**. Time-domain findings: SDNN and rMSSD were elevated in PCS patients compared to healthy controls (day period, p ≤ 0.003) and were comparable to CAD patients. Key mechanistic finding (Abstract verbatim): "PCS patients showed disturbed diurnal adjustment of HRV, with impaired parasympathetic activity at night." Missing increase of parasympathetic activity during the night (p < 0.001 vs controls). Sympathicus activation as reflected by LF/HF ratio and LFnu was elevated in PCS patients. Sub-cohort finding: Patients hospitalized during acute infection showed even more pronounced sympathetic overactivation vs non-hospitalized; long-term-symptom-duration PCS patients showed a significantly lower LF/HF ratio during the day (p = 0.014) with higher parasympathetic activation (p = 0.006). Discussion: "sustained impairment of sympathovagal balance ... sympathetic overstimulation and diminished parasympathetic response." **Group-level effect-size illustration** (from Results Table): Day SDNN 154.7 ± 39.9 (PCS) vs 146.9 ± 33.3 (controls), p ≤ 0.023; Day LF/HF 3.7 (PCS) vs 2.4 (controls), p < 0.001; Night SDNN 124.1 (PCS) vs 92.5 (controls), p < 0.001. Substantial group-level differences at clinical-grade 24-hour Holter resolution.

**Corroborating population-wearable anchor**: Radin et al. (2021, *JAMA Netw Open*) per [`pem_recovery_trajectory_review.md`](../../literature/reviews/pem_recovery_trajectory_review.md) §4a — post-acute COVID wearable-cohort tracking shows the autonomic channel (RHR) took ~79 days on average to return to baseline, versus ~24-32 days for behavioural channels (sleep quantity, step count) — a same-direction dissociation with subject's within-day slow-autonomic-recovery finding at population wearable resolution. ### Subclaim 2: "the autonomic dysregulation pattern in Long COVID overlaps substantially with the ME/CFS autonomic phenotype"

**External-consensus status: CONSENSUS-EXISTS** (LC and ME/CFS autonomic phenotypes converge on shared vagal-withdrawal + sympathetic-predominance + reduced HRV signature).

**Primary citation**: [`ryabkova_2024_mecfs_lc_dysautonomia_patterns.pdf`](../../literature/ryabkova_2024_mecfs_lc_dysautonomia_patterns.pdf) (Ryabkova et al., 2024, *Pathophysiology* 31:1-17) — controlled spiroarteriocardiorhythmography (SACR, Intox, Russia) of **34 ME/CFS + 29 post-COVID condition (PCC) + 32 healthy controls** (n = 95 total). Verbatim key findings from Abstract + Results: "the total power (TP) and high-frequency of RR interval variability at rest as well as baroreflex sensitivity were significantly lower both in PCC and ME/CFS patients compared to healthy controls." Baroreflex sensitivity Results (from Table 7): BRSup 4.42 (2.88-6.28) ms/mmHg (ME/CFS) vs 5.91 (3.54-7.92) (PCC) vs 7.40 (4.90-14.03) (HC), p = 0.001 for ME/CFS vs HC + p = 0.000 for ME/CFS vs HC step-wise; BRSmean 4.85 vs 5.24 vs 9.15, p = 0.000 vs HC (ME/CFS) + p = 0.003 vs HC (PCC). "The BRS was significantly lower both for up and down SBP ramps in the patients with ME/CFS compared to the HCs and significantly lower only for down SBP ramps in the patients with PCC." Differential finding (Abstract verbatim): "During slow breathing, the HRV parameters returned to normal in PCC but not in ME/CFS patients" — a graded severity signature: ME/CFS shows more entrenched dysautonomia than PCC at controlled-respiration challenge, but both differ from controls. Fatigue correlation (Results section): "The correlation analysis revealed a close relationship of HRV, BPV parameters and baroreflex sensitivity with fatigue, but not with HADS depressive/anxiety symptoms in the ME/CFS and PCC patients." Ryabkova ME/CFS fatigue-domain correlations: BRSup / BRSdown / BRSmean correlate negatively with general fatigue MFI-20 (-0.559, -0.394, -0.546). **Conclusion (Abstract verbatim)**: "A similar pattern of HRV and baroreflex failure with signs of a pathological acceleration of age-dependent dysautonomia was identified in the ME/CFS and PCC patients." Ryabkova's hypothesis: "the similarity in the chronic symptoms of patients with PCC and ME/CFS ... suggest that a subgroup of PCC and ME/CFS" share pathophysiological mechanism. **This is direct primary-source licensing for treating LC-era hypotheses as ME/CFS-aligned per the corpus's own methodological framing in [`wiggers_testable_hypotheses.md`](../../wiggers_testable_hypotheses.md).**

**Adjacent-condition anchor**: Nelson et al. (2019) meta-analysis of 64 case-control ME/CFS studies (per [`PEM_HRV_heart_rate_literature_review.md`](../../literature/reviews/PEM_HRV_heart_rate_literature_review.md) §5) reporting sympathetic-shift + elevated resting HR + reduced vagal HRV + exaggerated tilt HR response + lower maximal HR — the "wired but tired" phenotype consistent across ME/CFS studies. Framing paper: Komaroff & Lipkin (2023, *Front Med*) roadmap review documenting substantial LC + ME/CFS overlap in symptoms and biological abnormalities, including autonomic and cardiovascular dysfunction (per `PEM_HRV_heart_rate_literature_review.md` §2). Sherif et al. (2023) *eLife* mechanistic PASC review aligning LC with ME/CFS + POTS with dysautonomia among candidate drivers.

**Position-holders**: Ryabkova et al. (2024) (primary 3-group SACR comparison — the closest single primary source for LC-ME/CFS autonomic convergence at controlled-instrument resolution); Nelson et al. (2019) (meta-analytic ME/CFS anchor); Komaroff & Lipkin (2023) (LC-ME/CFS overlap roadmap review); Sherif et al. (2023) (mechanistic PASC review).

### Subclaim 3: "resting heart rate is elevated at baseline in ME/CFS and Long COVID populations relative to controls"

**External-consensus status: CONSENSUS-EXISTS** (baseline RHR elevation is a reproducible finding across ME/CFS + LC cohorts).

**Primary citation**: [`vancampen_2022_mecfs_rhr_elevated.pdf`](../../literature/vancampen_2022_mecfs_rhr_elevated.pdf) (Van Campen & Visser, 2022, *Med Res Arch* 10(6)) — supine HR vs stroke-volume-index study in **474 ME/CFS patients (314 with normal HR/BP + 160 with POTS during tilt-testing) vs 56 healthy controls** (n = 530 total). Verbatim key finding from Results (Table): **Supine HR = 62 (SD 10) bpm in healthy controls vs 71 (SD 11) bpm in ME/CFS, p < 0.0001** — a ~9 bpm baseline HR elevation at group level. From Abstract verbatim: "Using the upper limit of the 95% prediction interval for the heart rate of healthy controls, 46 (15%) of patients with a normal heart rate and blood pressure response had a resting heart rate above the upper limit ... in patients developing POTS the resting heart rate was above the upper limit [in 47/160 = 29%], and in 113 (71%) patients within the upper limit and lower limit." Conclusion (Abstract verbatim): "Patients and healthy controls showed a significant and inverse relation between the resting stroke volume index and resting heart rate ... in patients developing POTS during tilt-testing were higher compared to ... healthy controls." Interpretation: ME/CFS baseline RHR is elevated at group level via reduced stroke-volume-index (blood-volume + stroke-volume + peripheral-oxygen extraction reductions cited in the paper's Introduction section as mechanistic candidates). The POTS subset (160/474 = 34% of the ME/CFS sample) carries the strongest RHR signal, but the non-POTS subset (314/474 = 66%) still shows meaningful RHR elevation at group mean.

**Corroborating group-level population-wearable evidence**: Radin et al. (2021, *JAMA Netw Open*) per [`pem_recovery_trajectory_review.md`](../../literature/reviews/pem_recovery_trajectory_review.md) §4a — wearable-cohort post-acute COVID tracking (RHR took ~79 days to return to baseline vs behavioural channels ~24-32 days; biphasic dip-then-elevated course); Parthasarathy et al. (2025) RECOVER Fitbit-class cohort per [`wearable_validity_lc_mecfs_review.md`](../../literature/reviews/wearable_validity_lc_mecfs_review.md) §3 — reduced sleep HRV + higher resting heart rate independently associated with Long COVID after confounder adjustment. Acute-viral autonomic signature per [`acute_viral_infection_autonomic_signature_review.md`](../../literature/reviews/acute_viral_infection_autonomic_signature_review.md) §1 + §2 (Natarajan 2020 n = 2745 COVID cases: "Respiration rate and heart rate are typically elevated by illness, while HRV is decreased"; Mishra 2020 n = 32 COVID cases from ~5300-person cohort: elevated RHR relative to personal baseline in 81% of cases often at or before symptom onset) as adjacent-condition anchor establishing the direction of RHR-up under autonomic activation. Adjacent anchor: Nelson et al. (2019) meta-analysis reporting elevated resting HR as one component of the ME/CFS autonomic signature.

**Position-holders**: Van Campen & Visser (2022) (primary ME/CFS supine HR study); Radin et al. (2021, 2024) (wearable-cohort acute-COVID recovery); Parthasarathy et al. (2025) (RECOVER Fitbit cohort); Nelson et al. (2019) (meta-analytic anchor); Natarajan et al. (2020) (COVID wearable cohort, RHR-up direction).

### Subclaim 1 (cardiovascular autonomic dysfunction in LC)

**Named metrics in the sources**: HRV time-domain indices (SDNN, RMSSD), HRV frequency-domain indices (LF, HF, LF/HF ratio); resting heart rate; heart-rate response to orthostatic challenge.

**Source's operational definition**: Marques 2023 review synthesises across studies using **clinical-grade instrumentation** — supine ECG-derived HRV per Task-Force 1996 methods (Kubios-analysed short-term or Holter-derived), lab-measured resting supine HR, tilt-table or active-stand HR change. Mooren 2023 uses **24-hour Holter ECG** with Kubios-derived HRV indices (SDNN, RMSSD, LF/HF, diurnal circadian analysis).
