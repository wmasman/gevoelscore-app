# Has Anyone Directly Measured the Cumulative, Multi-Day "Push-Crash" Erosion of PEM? A Focused Literature Verdict

## TL;DR
- **The prior conclusion holds, with one nuance: there is NO direct physiological measurement of a progressive, cumulative day-over-day erosion of exertional threshold across 3+ consecutive days in any post-infectious fatigue condition, in any language.** The objective-physiology evidence base stops at the single-bout, 2-day (24-hour) CPET suppression paradigm, supplemented by symptom self-report and clinical inference for the multi-day "stacking" model.
- **The single closest near-miss — a 2025 Dutch multi-day wearable HRV study (Ruijgt/Wüst/Janssen) — DID collect 3–7 days of continuous physiological data, but analyzed it as single-bout→same-night suppression and explicitly averaged across days, deliberately NOT testing night-to-night accumulation.** So "never measured physiologically over multiple days" is too strong about data *collection*, but correct about demonstrated cumulative *erosion*.
- **No study anywhere formally operationalizes or tests "threshold-crossing" (single supra-threshold spike) versus "dose-accumulation" (cumulative sub-threshold load) as competing mechanisms with physiological endpoints.** The dose-accumulation/"total load" model exists only as clinical-consensus and patient-guidance language, plus one untested mechanistic hypothesis (Wirth/Scheibenbogen sodium-overload).

## Key Findings
1. **The 2-day CPET ceiling is universal.** Every published cardiopulmonary exercise protocol — Workwell lineage (Stevens, Snell, VanNess, Davenport, Keller), Norwegian lactate work (Lien 2019), NIRS muscle-oxygenation (Thomas 2026), epigenetic/proteomic exertion studies — caps at two maximal/submaximal bouts 24 hours apart. None extends to a third day or to repeated provocations across a week. This design demonstrates single-effort threshold suppression, not multi-day accumulation. The prior conclusion's central distinction is correct.
2. **The largest 2-day CPET study** (Keller et al., 2024, *Journal of Translational Medicine*; 84 ME/CFS vs 71 sedentary controls) found ~5.3% peak VO2 and ~6.8% VO2-at-ventilatory-threshold declines on day 2 in ME/CFS but not controls. Still only 2 days.
3. **Multi-day physiological data DOES exist in one study, but not multi-day erosion analysis.** The Dutch wearable HRV preprint recorded 3–7 consecutive days of clinical-grade ECG in 127 long COVID patients but compared each night to the patient's *best* night and averaged exercise-recovery across days — by construction it cannot show day-over-day stacking. The authors themselves call for controlled PEM-induction studies with continuous follow-up.
4. **Repeated-bout WITHIN-day or 24-hour metabolic abnormality is well documented** — the Newcastle 31P-MRS study (Jones et al., 2012, *European Journal of Clinical Investigation*) showed a CFS subgroup had increased intramuscular acidosis after each of three linked exercise bouts "with no apparent reduction in acidosis with repeat exercise" (unlike controls), roughly 50-fold higher cumulative post-exercise acid exposure (area-under-curve), and a "significant prolongation (almost 4-fold) of the time taken for pH to recover to baseline." This is the closest *metabolic* evidence of failure-to-recover-from-repeat-exertion, but it is intra-session (three 3-minute bouts), not multi-day.
5. **Recovery from a single bout is prolonged but measured by symptoms.** Moore, Keller, Stevens et al. (2023, *Medicina* 59(3):571; n=80 ME/CFS, 64 controls) found a "highly significant difference in judged recovery time (ME/CFS = 12.7 ± 1.2 d; CTL = 2.1 ± 0.2 d… Chi² = 90.1, p < 0.0001)," with ME/CFS recovery ranging 1–64 days and one patient unrecovered after one year — but the endpoint was a symptom-severity scale, not a physiological marker. This reinforces the "plus symptom inference" clause.
6. **Non-English literature adds no multi-day cumulative physiological study.** Chinese, Japanese, German, Dutch, French, Spanish, Italian, and Norwegian sources all either replicate/reference the 2-day paradigm, provide single-bout objective tools, or remain at symptom/theory level.

## Details

### Direct answer to the core question
Is there ANY direct physiological measurement of the cumulative multi-day erosion pattern? **No.** The evidence stops at single-bout (2-day CPET) threshold suppression plus symptom-level inference and clinical consensus. The prior conclusion — *"The push-crash 'cumulative sub-threshold erosion' pattern is clinically universal but never directly measured physiologically… Treat the accumulation model as well-grounded clinical inference, not proven mechanism"* — is **correct in substance.** The only refinement: one study (Dutch HRV) did *collect* multi-day physiological data, so the precise wording should be "no study has demonstrated progressive day-over-day physiological accumulation/erosion," rather than "no multi-day physiological data has ever been collected."

### Multi-day / repeated-bout physiological studies catalogued

**2-day CPET paradigm (single-bout suppression — does NOT demonstrate accumulation):**
- **Workwell Foundation lineage** (Stevens, Snell, VanNess, Davenport, Staci Stevens; methods paper Stevens et al. 2018, *Frontiers in Pediatrics*). Two maximal CPETs 24h apart. CPET-1 provokes PEM; CPET-2 measures impaired recovery (failure to reproduce VO2peak, work, and values at ventilatory/anaerobic threshold). **No protocol extends beyond 2 days** — confirmed across their entire published output. Their own pacing materials invoke a "vicious push-crash cycle" in which "even mild physical activity can further decrease the threshold," but this is asserted clinically, not serially measured.
- **Keller et al. 2014** (n=22) and **Keller et al. 2024** (n=84 ME/CFS + 71 controls, *J Transl Med*) — largest to date; ~5.3% peak VO2 and ~6.8% VO2VT decline day 2.
- **Lien et al. 2019** (Oslo, *Physiological Reports*) — n=18 female ME/CFS + 15 controls, repeated CPET 24h apart with arterial lactate every 30 seconds; day-2 lactate increased at gas-exchange threshold in patients, decreased in controls. 2 days only.
- **Thomas et al. 2026** (*Physiological Reports*) — n=46 long COVID + 10 controls, two submaximal CPETs 24h apart with continuous-wave NIRS on gastrocnemius; impaired day-2 function. 2 days only.
- **Hodges et al. 2018** (*Clin Physiol Funct Imaging*) — repeated exercise pilot in CFS, MS, and controls. 2 days.

**Within-session repeated-bout (same-day, not multi-day):**
- **Jones et al. 2012** (Newcastle, *European Journal of Clinical Investigation*) — 31P-MRS across three 3-minute exercise bouts at 35% MVC. CFS subgroup showed increased intramuscular acidosis after each bout with no reduction on repeat (unlike controls), ~50-fold higher cumulative acid exposure (area-under-curve), and ~4-fold prolonged pH recovery. Strong *metabolic* evidence of failure-to-recover from repeat exertion, but intra-session. (A companion design was applied to primary biliary cirrhosis with the same finding.)

**Single-bout muscle/biopsy (mechanistic, single provocation):**
- **Appelman, Charlton, Wüst et al. 2024** (Amsterdam UMC / VU, *Nature Communications* 15:17; NCT05225688; n=25 long COVID patients/controls) — longitudinal case-control; muscle biopsy + blood before and after a single maximal CPET. Verbatim: "severe exercise-induced myopathy and tissue infiltration of amyloid-containing deposits in skeletal muscles of patients with long COVID worsen after induction of post-exertional malaise." Single bout, two timepoints — not multi-day.

**Invasive CPET (single-session, cross-sectional):**
- **Systrom** iCPET work — signature "preload failure." Per Health Rising's report of his 2022 IACFS/ME keynote, "about 90% of the ME/CFS patients Systrom has examined exhibit preload failure. Of the 1,500 or so invasive CPETs Systrom has data on, 72% of the patients with preload failure… met the criteria for ME/CFS" (a related Joseph/Systrom analysis found preload failure in 92% of 66 ME/CFS patients assessed). Performed as a single session. No serial multi-day invasive CPET exists; Systrom has stated repeat-test significant change is essentially not seen, and frames iCPET as single-bout detection.

### Longitudinal wearable / actigraphy / EMA studies (flagging physiological vs self-report)

**Physiological endpoint, multi-day data, but NOT cumulative-erosion analysis:**
- **Ruijgt, Slaghekke, Ellens, Janssen, Wüst 2025** (medRxiv 2025.03.18.25320115, not peer-reviewed) — 127 long COVID + 21 controls, Firstbeat Bodyguard 3 single-channel ECG worn 3–7 consecutive days. Verbatim: "HRV remained lower for 24 hours after exercise below, at or above VT1 in patients, but not in healthy controls (p=0.010). Nighttime HRV decreased with intense exercise and longer durations in patients with long COVID (p=0.018)… VT1 can be practically interpreted as a PEM threshold." **Critically, methods averaged HRV across days and compared each night to the patient's best night — single-bout→same-night suppression replicated across days, NOT a test of night-to-night stacking.** Strongest near-miss; authors explicitly flag absence of PEM induction and call for controlled studies.

**Severity classification (cross-sectional, not crash causation):**
- **Bateman Horne Center "Hours of Upright Activity" (HUA) / UpTime** (Palombo et al. 2020, *J Transl Med*; 6-day case-control, n=15 IMU-based). Severe ME/CFS spend <20% of day upright; moderate 20–30%; healthy >30%. Stratifies severity; IMU more accurate than self-reported HUA (which patients over-estimate). Does NOT relate accumulated upright load to a measured physiological crash.
- **van Campen/Rowe/Visser (Stichting CardioZorg)** — extensive objective tilt-test cerebral blood flow work; ~90% of ME/CFS show abnormal CBF reduction during head-up tilt, and CBF "remains reduced after tilt" (delayed recovery). These are single-session orthostatic provocations, not multi-day accumulation, though the "incomplete recovery" finding is conceptually adjacent.

**Symptom self-report endpoints (NOT physiological):**
- **Leonard Jason energy-envelope studies** (DePaul) — longitudinal diary/questionnaire work showing staying within the "energy envelope" predicts better outcomes (e.g., 16.0% vs 2.7% improvement in fatigue severity for those inside vs outside the envelope at baseline), and overexertion associates with worse disability/fatigue. Explicitly symptom/self-report, NOT physiological measurement of threshold erosion.
- **EMA studies** (e.g., intensive longitudinal long COVID cohorts measuring symptom severity every 3 hours for up to 24 days) — characterize symptom fluctuation after physical/cognitive/social triggers; symptom endpoints.
- **Pacing RCTs** (e.g., "Pace Me" app + wearable activity tracker; WHO Borg CR-10 5-phase protocol) — PEM/PESE measured by DePaul Symptom Questionnaire-PEM or Leeds PESE Questionnaire; symptom endpoints; the "Pace Me" RCT (250 randomised) found no significant time-by-group DSQ-PEM difference.

### Non-English literature (language, source, quality, translated results)

- **Chinese (慢性疲劳综合征 = chronic fatigue syndrome; 长新冠 = Long COVID; 运动后不适 = post-exertional malaise; 心率变异性 = HRV):** No CNKI/SCI-indexed ME/CFS or Long COVID study with objective multi-day cumulative threshold erosion. Field dominated by TCM-theoretical/herbal/acupuncture work and reviews citing Western 2-day CPET. A multi-week HRV-decline literature exists but concerns *athlete overtraining syndrome*, a different population — must not be conflated. **Quality flag: low for this question; genuine null.**
- **Japanese (慢性疲労症候群 = CFS; 筋痛性脳脊髄炎 = myalgic encephalomyelitis; 労作後倦怠感 = post-exertional malaise):** Strong objective fatigue-biomarker tradition at RIKEN / Osaka City University (Watanabe, Kuratsune, Tanaka, Yamano, Nakatomi) — neuroinflammation PET (Nakatomi et al. 2014), TCA/urea-cycle metabolite markers (Yamano et al. 2016, *Sci Rep* 6:34990), deep phenotyping (Kitami/Fukuda et al. 2020, *Sci Rep*), autonomic/HRV mental-fatigue induction, and a rat chronic-fatigue model. All single-timepoint, cross-sectional, or short induction; the Japanese clinician materials reference the 2-day CPET. **No multi-day cumulative erosion study; null. Quality flag: high-quality groups, wrong design for this question.**
- **German (Charité / Scheibenbogen, Wirth; Belastungsintoleranz = exertion intolerance; postexertionelle Malaise):** Highest-quality mechanistic output. Repeat hand-grip strength (two series ~1 hour apart) shows decline correlated with PEM/fatigue/cognitive/autonomic severity in ME/CFS (Paffrath, Kim, Scheibenbogen, Sotzny et al.; Jäkel et al.) — intra-day, single session. Hand-grip also used as a longitudinal *prognostic* marker over months (observational; *Nat Commun* 2022). **Wirth & Scheibenbogen sodium/calcium-overload hypothesis** is the leading *theory* predicting a cumulative lowered re-induction threshold — but it is hypothesis, not multi-day measurement. 23Na-MRI muscle sodium (Petter et al. 2022, n=6) — single short bout.
- **Dutch:** Appelman/Wüst single-bout biopsy (above); van Campen/Visser tilt-CBF (above); Utrecht wearable HRV preprint (above, the near-miss). No true multi-day cumulative erosion study.
- **Norwegian:** Lien et al. 2019 arterial lactate (above) — 2-day. Canonical Norwegian metabolic contribution, still within the 2-day paradigm.
- **Spanish / French / Italian:** Clinical reviews and patient-guidance describing/recommending the 2-day CPET and pacing; one Spanish clinical service offers a 24h-repeat CPET ("R-Test"). No original multi-day cumulative physiology. Null.

### Animal models
- General "long-term exercise-induced fatigue" rodent models (treadmill/swimming over weeks) show cumulative neural, myocardial, bone, and skeletal-muscle damage with objective endpoints — but these are healthy-animal overtraining models, not post-viral/PEM models.
- A post-influenza convalescence fatigue mouse model (BALB/c, influenza A + diet restriction + weight-bearing swimming), a coxsackievirus B3 myocarditis exercise model, and a Gulf War Illness mouse model with repeated exercise-endurance testing (showing persistent exercise fatigue) all exist. None directly models cumulative multi-day sub-threshold erosion of an exertional threshold in a post-infectious PEM framework.

### Threshold-crossing vs dose-accumulation
No study formally operationalizes or tests these as competing mechanisms with physiological endpoints. The dominant implicit paradigm is **threshold-crossing** (VT1/VAT as "the PEM threshold"; a single supra-threshold bout provokes PEM). The **dose-accumulation/"total load"** model appears only in clinical-consensus and patient-guidance language (e.g., ME/CFS clinical sources stating the impact of triggers is cumulative and total load influences PEM) and in the untested German sodium-overload hypothesis. The Dutch study's finding that both exercise *duration* and *intensity* independently lower subsequent HRV hints at a dose component but was not framed or tested as accumulation-versus-threshold.

## Recommendations

**Stage 1 — Adopt the refined verdict now.** State in your work that the cumulative multi-day sub-threshold erosion model is **well-grounded clinical inference and an untested mechanistic hypothesis, not a physiologically demonstrated mechanism.** Use the precise phrasing: *"No study has demonstrated progressive day-over-day physiological erosion of exertional threshold; the only multi-day physiological dataset (Dutch HRV, 2025) analyzed single-bout same-night suppression and averaged across days rather than testing accumulation."* This is more defensible than a blanket "never measured physiologically."

**Stage 2 — Treat these as the evidence tiers:**
- *Well-established:* Single-bout 24-hour threshold suppression (2-day CPET); within-session failure to recover from repeat exertion (Newcastle 31P-MRS acid handling, Jones 2012).
- *Emerging:* Multi-day wearable HRV showing delayed/incomplete overnight autonomic recovery after a single bout (Dutch preprint — not yet peer-reviewed); single-bout muscle myopathy worsening with PEM (Appelman/Wüst 2024); prolonged multi-day symptom recovery from a single bout (Moore/Keller 2023, symptom endpoint).
- *Hypothesis only:* Cumulative day-over-day dose-accumulation erosion; the sodium/calcium-overload lowered-re-induction-threshold mechanism.

**Stage 3 — The study design that would settle it (and the thresholds that would change the verdict):** A prospective, controlled protocol applying **standardized sub-threshold (below-VT1) daily exertion for 5–7+ consecutive days** with **daily physiological endpoints** — serial submaximal threshold (VO2/VT1), morning resting HR/HRV, continuous wearable HRV, ambulatory lactate or NIRS muscle oxygenation, and ideally serial muscle/blood biomarkers — explicitly modeling **day-over-day drift** (mixed-effects models testing a monotonic downward trend across days) and **statistically separating accumulated load (area-under-curve of exertion) from threshold-crossing events** as predictors of a subsequently measured (not merely self-reported) crash. **The verdict flips from "hypothesis" to "demonstrated" if such a study shows a statistically significant progressive decline in an objective threshold across ≥3 consecutive days of sub-threshold load that is not explained by a single supra-threshold event.** The Dutch authors' own call for "controlled trials with PEM induction and continuous follow-up" is precisely this gap. An ambulatory-lactate-pacing proposal (Faghy et al. 2024) and a forthcoming augmented 2-day step-test protocol with portable VO2 and NIRS (NCT06933017) are steps toward, but not yet, this design.

## Caveats
- **Source quality:** Primary peer-reviewed sources (Keller 2024, Stevens 2018, Lien 2019, Jones 2012, Appelman 2024, Thomas 2026, Palombo 2020, Moore/Keller 2023) are reliable. The pivotal Dutch HRV study is a **medRxiv preprint, not yet peer-reviewed**. Some corroborating details on Systrom's serial-testing remarks and older Workwell figures come via secondary reporting (Health Rising, MEpedia) rather than primary papers.
- **Terminology trap:** Multi-day/multi-week HRV and lactate-threshold decline ARE documented in healthy-athlete *overtraining syndrome* — a different population and construct that must not be counted as PEM evidence.
- **"Multi-day" almost always means 2-day (24h) in this literature.** Readers and clinicians frequently conflate the 2-day CPET with multi-day accumulation; they are different claims.
- **Populations:** Evidence is overwhelmingly ME/CFS and Long COVID. No multi-day cumulative physiological studies were located for post-Q-fever, post-SARS, post-Ebola, post-Lyme/PTLDS, post-mononucleosis/EBV, or fibromyalgia specifically (fibromyalgia appears only in single-bout delayed-HR-recovery work).
- **The recovery-decay endpoint problem:** The strongest "delayed recovery that could stack" data (Moore/Keller 2023, 12.7-day recovery) uses a symptom-severity endpoint, not a physiological one — exactly the limitation the core question targets.
