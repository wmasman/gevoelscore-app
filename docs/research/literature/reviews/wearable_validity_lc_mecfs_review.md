# Does the Watch See Anything Real? Consumer-Wearable Autonomic Signals in Long COVID / ME-CFS / PAIS — An External-Validity Literature Check (R24)

*Evidence retrieved from PubMed (DOIs linked throughout). Purpose: this brief asks the foundation-chain question that sits underneath every felt-state↔Garmin analysis in this project — **has a consumer/wrist wearable's autonomic readout (HRV, resting HR, "stress"/body-battery, sleep) actually been shown to carry meaningful, in-population signal in Long COVID, ME/CFS, or other post-acute infection syndromes (PAIS)?** This is the "does the watch see anything real?" check. It is deliberately DISTINCT from the companion brief [`expected_shapes_autonomic_signals_review.md`](expected_shapes_autonomic_signals_review.md), which is about the expected SHAPE of an autonomic↔outcome curve, not about whether a wrist device validly carries the signal in this patient group.*

*Scope note: this is an n=1 instrument-test project. "Validated in-population" below means **a study in an actual LC/ME-CFS/PAIS sample showed the wearable signal tracks something clinical** — it does NOT mean validated for this body. The load-bearing distinction kept throughout is **"validated in THIS population" vs "plausible by analogy."* See [[findings-are-provisional]], [[site-is-medium-not-subject]].*

---

## 1. Reader-facing synthesis (one paragraph)

Based on articles retrieved from PubMed, the honest external evidence is **thin-to-moderate and heterogeneous, and it splits cleanly in two**. There IS in-population evidence that **clinically-graded autonomic abnormality is real and detectable in LC and ME/CFS** — reduced HRV, elevated resting HR, and a vagal-withdrawal/sympathetic-predominance phenotype show up repeatedly in cohort and case-control work, and at least one large consumer-wearable cohort (RECOVER, Fitbit-class) recovers the same reduced-sleep-HRV and raised-resting-HR signature. There is ALSO good general-population evidence that wrist devices measure HR well and sleep-vs-wake adequately (though sleep *staging* and ambulatory *HRV* are weaker). What is **largely missing** is the join of the two: a study that takes a *consumer wrist wearable*, in an *LC/ME-CFS sample*, and validates its autonomic output against a clinical instrument *and* against day-to-day symptom state. Most in-population autonomic studies use research-grade ECG/Kubios or tilt-table, not the watch; most consumer-wearable studies in this population measure activity/sleep/HR for feasibility or association, not validated autonomic inference; and the heart-rate-pacing literature that clinicians actually recommend rests heavily on a single survey plus CPET-anchored theory. So the watch almost certainly *sees something real* (the autonomic abnormality it would be reading is well-documented in-population), but the specific claim "this consumer device's stress/HRV/body-battery index is a validated readout of autonomic state in LC/ME-CFS" is **not cleanly established** — it is supported more by convergence and analogy than by direct in-population device validation.

## 2. What IS validated in-population (autonomic abnormality is real and measurable in LC/ME-CFS)

This strand answers "is there a real autonomic signal in this population for a device to detect?" — and the answer is a confident yes, though mostly via research-grade instruments, not consumer wearables.

- **LC cohorts show reduced HRV and a sympathetic shift on supine recording.** In 87 long-COVID patients vs 71 controls, vagally-mediated HRV indices (RMSSD, SDNN, HF, total power, SD1) were significantly *reduced* and the stress/SNS indices significantly *elevated*, with the autonomic deficit partially mediated by reduced cardiorespiratory fitness ([Oscoz-Ochandorena et al., 2024, *Curr Probl Cardiol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cpcardiol.2024.102732)). This is direct in-population evidence that the HRV signal carries group-separating information.
- **LC dysautonomia is detectable from a continuous physiological index, and tracks the fatigue phenotype.** In a 39-participant LC study, an HRV-derived autonomic index (NOL) dissociated over time specifically in the *fatigued* LC subgroup vs controls ([Barizien et al., 2021, *Sci Rep*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-021-93546-5)) — i.e. the autonomic readout separates not just disease but the symptom that matters here.
- **The autonomic abnormality is a recognised, reviewed feature of LC**, including HRV changes alongside orthostatic intolerance / POTS, establishing that "the watch would be reading a real disturbance" is mainstream ([Hira et al., 2023, *Curr Neurol Neurosci Rep*, review; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11910-023-01320-z)).
- **ME/CFS carries the same in-population HRV signature via mobile-health hardware.** In 77 ME/CFS patients vs 44 controls measured with mHealth instrumentation, HRV indices separated patients from controls and related to core fatigue symptoms, with the authors explicitly proposing HRV as an objective, non-invasive ME/CFS marker ([Capdevila et al., 2021, *Sensors*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3390/s21113746)). This is the closest existing thing to "wearable-grade autonomic signal validated against ME/CFS symptom state," though the sensor is a research mHealth chest/photoplethysmographic setup, not a commodity smartwatch's proprietary index.

**Strength of this strand: moderate-to-good, but the instrument is usually research-grade.** It establishes the *target signal* is real in-population; it does not, by itself, validate a Garmin/Fitbit "stress" or body-battery index.

## 3. What consumer wearables have actually shown IN these populations (association + feasibility, rarely validated autonomic inference)

- **Large consumer-wearable LC cohort recovers the autonomic signature — the strongest single piece of in-population device evidence.** In the RECOVER adult cohort, consumer-wearable (Fitbit-class) sleep recordings showed Long COVID independently associated with **reduced sleep HRV, higher resting heart rate**, lower sleep efficiency, and greater night-to-night variability in sleep duration/timing, after adjustment for confounders ([Parthasarathy et al., 2025, *Research Square* preprint; DOI](https://doi.org/10.21203/rs.3.rs-7422764/v1)). This is the closest published match to the project's premise — a commodity wrist device's HRV/RHR output carrying LC-relevant signal in a real cohort — but it is a **preprint (not yet peer-reviewed)** and is association-level, not a validation against a clinical autonomic instrument.
- **Wrist trackers are feasible and informative in post-COVID monitoring, but the studies stop at feasibility.** A prospective post-COVID rehab study had patients wear a Fitbit Charge 2; HR/sleep/activity were collectable and clinically plausible, but the paper characterises *feasibility*, not validated autonomic inference ([Wiebe et al., 2024, *PLOS Digit Health*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pdig.0000571)).
- **In ME/CFS, consumer activity trackers capture symptom-relevant behaviour, not autonomic validity.** A 6-month Fitbit Charge 3 study in 27 ME/CFS patients showed continuous monitoring was feasible and activity related to patient-reported outcomes ([Rekeland et al., 2022, *PLoS One*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0274472)); pediatric ME/CFS actigraphy similarly distinguished patients by activity pattern ([Loiacono et al., 2020, *Chronic Illn*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1177/1742395320949613)). These validate the *activity/actigraphy* channel in-population, not the HRV/stress channel.
- **A 2025 systematic review of wearable digital biomarkers of fatigue across chronic disease (incl. LC and CFS) is the right meta-anchor for "how strong is this overall."** It surveys exactly the device-signal↔fatigue relationship this project rests on across 13 condition categories ([Aboagye et al., 2025, *npj Digit Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-025-01939-x)) — the natural citation for the honest claim that the field is real but heterogeneous and immature.

**Strength of this strand: thin for the specific claim.** Consumer wearables are *feasible* and *associated* in-population; their proprietary autonomic indices are not directly *validated* against clinical autonomic measures in an LC/ME-CFS sample in the peer-reviewed literature found here.

## 4. The heart-rate-pacing literature (clinically recommended, evidentially light)

The Workwell/Bateman-Horne-style practice of pacing below a heart-rate threshold (anchored to the CPET ventilatory/anaerobic threshold) is the main clinical use-case for a wearable's *heart-rate* channel in this population.

- **The in-population evidence base for HR-monitor pacing is essentially one survey plus CPET theory.** An international survey of 488 people with ME/CFS using heart-rate-monitor pacing reported reduced PEM severity/duration and better activity management, while the authors explicitly note there is **"only one piece of research investigating the use of HRM"** despite multiple guidelines recommending it, and that the rationale rests on the reduced ventilatory anaerobic threshold seen on repeat CPET ([Clague-Baker et al., 2023, *Work*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3233/WOR-220512)). This is the honest ceiling of the pacing-with-HR evidence: recommended, self-reported-beneficial, but thinly validated.
- **The threshold rationale it leans on is real:** exercise above threshold reliably exacerbates symptoms in ME/CFS, supporting *why* a HR ceiling would help even though it doesn't validate any device's accuracy ([Sandler et al., 2016, *Med Sci Sports Exerc*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1249/MSS.0000000000000983)).

**Strength of this strand: thin (self-report + theory).** It supports the *use* of the HR channel for pacing; it is not device-validation evidence.

## 5. General-population device validation (the "plausible by analogy" leg — do NOT inflate)

These are NOT in-population studies. They license the expectation that a wrist device's HR is trustworthy and its sleep/wake adequate; they say nothing specific about LC/ME-CFS, and the HRV/staging caveats matter for any "stress"/body-battery claim.

- **Wrist HR is reasonably accurate; accuracy degrades as HR rises.** A laboratory + free-living validation of consumer wrist trackers against chest-strap ECG found moderate-to-strong agreement (Polar A370 ICC up to 0.83, MAPE ~6–7%), with errors growing at higher heart rates ([Müller et al., 2019, *JMIR Mhealth Uhealth*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.2196/14120)).
- **Sleep-vs-wake is valid across six major consumer devices (Apple/Garmin/Polar/Oura/Whoop); sleep STAGING and night-time HRV are weaker.** Against polysomnography + ECG, two-state sleep classification was good (κ up to 0.51) but multi-state staging was poor-to-fair ([Miller et al., 2022, *Sensors*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3390/s22166317)) — relevant because Garmin "stress"/body-battery is derived partly from sleep + HRV, the two channels this study finds weakest.
- **Ambulatory PPG HR can be made clinical-grade with motion-robust algorithms** ([Huang & Selvaraj, 2020, *Annu Int Conf IEEE EMBC*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1109/EMBC44109.2020.9175346)) — supports HR but not derived autonomic indices.

**Strength of this strand: good — but it is ANALOGY, not in-population validation.** It must not be cited as evidence that the watch carries valid autonomic signal *in LC/ME-CFS*; it only supports that the raw HR channel is trustworthy in general adults.

## 6. Honest-strength verdict

**On the headline claim — "consumer wearables carry meaningful autonomic signal in LC/ME-CFS": THIN-to-MODERATE, and it does not cleanly resolve.** Decomposed:

| Sub-claim | In-population? | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| Real autonomic abnormality (↓HRV, ↑RHR, vagal withdrawal) exists in LC/ME-CFS | Yes (research-grade ECG/Kubios/tilt) | **Moderate-good** |
| A *consumer wrist* device recovers that signature in a real LC cohort | Yes — but one preprint (RECOVER) | **Thin** (unreviewed, association-level) |
| Consumer wearable autonomic index *validated against a clinical autonomic measure* in LC/ME-CFS | **No study found** | **None** |
| HR-monitor pacing helps in-population | Survey + theory only | **Thin** |
| Wrist HR / sleep-wake accurate in general adults (analogy leg) | Not in-population | Good, but analogy only |

The defensible synthesis for the site: *the abnormality the watch would be reading is well-established in this population; the watch's raw HR channel is trustworthy in general; but the specific step "this device's proprietary HRV/stress/body-battery index is a validated autonomic readout in LC/ME-CFS" rests on convergence and analogy, not on a direct in-population device-validation study.* A "can't say cleanly — convergent but not directly validated" verdict is the honest and publishable outcome.

## 7. Honest limits

- **PubMed-only, English-language, purposive (not exhaustive) search**, run 2026-06-30; the consumer-wearable-in-LC field is moving fast and a directly-validating study may exist or appear.
- **The single best-matching source (RECOVER, Parthasarathy 2025) is a Research Square preprint** — not peer-reviewed; weight accordingly.
- **"Consumer wearable" was read broadly.** Several in-population HRV studies (Capdevila 2021; Oscoz-Ochandorena 2024) used research/mHealth instrumentation, not the commodity Garmin/Fitbit/Apple/Oura/Whoop class the project actually uses; the gap between "mHealth HRV works in ME/CFS" and "Garmin's Firstbeat stress index works in ME/CFS" is exactly the unvalidated step.
- **Garmin "stress"/body-battery are proprietary Firstbeat-derived indices**; none of the located studies validate *that specific transform* in this population — the closest device-channel validations (Miller 2022, Müller 2019) are general-population and flag HRV/staging as the weak links feeding those indices.
- **Overlap guard:** this brief does not re-derive curve shape (see [`expected_shapes_autonomic_signals_review.md`](expected_shapes_autonomic_signals_review.md)); existing repo PDFs (Suh 2023 LC-HRV review; Ryabkova 2024; Mooren 2023; Van Campen & Visser 2022; Aitken 2026 Visible) already cover LC/ME-CFS HRV *mechanism* and a Visible-app prediction cohort — this brief is specifically the *consumer-device-validity-in-population* question, which those do not directly answer.
