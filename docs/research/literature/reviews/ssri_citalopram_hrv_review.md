# SSRIs, Citalopram, HRV-derived "Stress", and Long COVID — A Cited Literature Review (Request R29)

*Evidence retrieved from PubMed/PMC. All cited articles include DOI links. This review answers three research questions for the citalopram page rebuild: (RQ1) whether SSRIs blunt HRV strongly enough that an HRV-derived watch "stress" score would read higher; (RQ2) the state of evidence for SSRIs in Long COVID / post-acute infection syndromes; (RQ3) Carla Rus's theory and exploratory study, plus emotional blunting as a documented SSRI effect.*

> **n=1 framing (binding).** This project independently **measured** a sized citalopram dose-response on watch-derived stress channels (`stress_mean_sleep`, `all_day_stress_avg`, `bb_lowest`). That measured result is local evidence in one body and stands on its own regardless of what follows. This review does **not** verify it and does **not** cite it as literature evidence. What is at stake here is only the **on-page mechanistic explanation** of that measured dose-response. The literature *contextualises*; it does not adjudicate the n=1 body. The user's lived benefit and emotional blunting are first-person self-report, bundled as context to write *to*, never cited as evidence.

## RQ1 mechanism verdict (load-bearing) — **MIXED, leaning weak-support**

**Claim under test:** "Citalopram raised the watch's measured stress because SSRIs blunt HRV, and the watch's stress/body-battery are HRV-derived."

**Verdict: MIXED.** The literature does **not** cleanly support the strong, mechanistic version of the on-page claim ("SSRIs blunt HRV" as an established fact that explains a higher stress reading), but it does **not** refute a weaker version either. The best current synthesis — a 2023 PRISMA/MOOSE meta-analysis of 30 studies — found SSRIs have **"no or inconclusive effects" on most autonomic outcomes**, with the direction of even the one significant HRV finding (RMSSD) **flipping with study design**: SSRIs were associated with *lower* RMSSD in randomized placebo-controlled trials (SMD −0.48) but *higher* RMSSD in pre-post studies (SMD +0.27) ([Fiani et al., 2023, *Eur Neuropsychopharmacol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.euroneuro.2023.03.013)). A large cross-sectional study is the strongest single signal *for* a lowering effect: SSRI users — explicitly **including citalopram** — showed lower HRV than non-users, though citalopram's effect was milder than paroxetine's and fluoxetine showed no effect at all ([Kemp et al., 2016, *Psychosom Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1097/PSY.0000000000000336)).

**Two strongest cites:** Fiani 2023 (meta-analysis, the headline "no/inconclusive, design-dependent" finding) and Kemp 2016 (the citalopram-specific "lower HRV in users" cross-sectional signal).

**What this means for the page (honest):** The **measured dose-response stays** — it was observed in this body. But the page's current one-line mechanism ("SSRIs blunt that variability") is **stated too strongly for the literature**. The page should be rewritten to say the HRV literature is *mixed*: there is a plausible, partially-supported direction (SSRIs, citalopram included, *can* be associated with somewhat lower HRV, especially in controlled trials and as a blunted autonomic *reactivity*), but it is **not** an established, uniform, dose-clean effect, and a consumer device's HRV→"stress" transform has **no published validation** under SSRIs. The dose-response is the primary local evidence; the HRV mechanism is offered as a *plausible but unverified* contextual explanation, explicitly flagged as mixed.

## RQ1 — full synthesis: do SSRIs (citalopram specifically) blunt HRV?

**Reader synthesis (one paragraph).** Ask "do SSRIs lower HRV?" and the honest answer is "sometimes, weakly, and it depends." The best meta-analysis finds SSRIs have no consistent effect on most heart-rate-variability measures, and the one measure that does move (RMSSD) points in *opposite* directions depending on whether you run a randomized trial or a before-after study. Older work is important context: depression itself lowers HRV, and SSRIs notably **fail to restore** that HRV even when mood recovers — so an SSRI is not an "HRV-raising" drug the way successful treatment might be assumed to be. The clearest mechanistic thread is that the tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs), which are strongly anticholinergic, *do* reliably lower HRV, whereas SSRIs are much gentler; citalopram specifically is associated with mildly lower HRV in some observational data but is cardiologically among the safer SSRIs. The net is a mixed, direction-unstable, mostly-small effect — enough to make "citalopram nudged an HRV-derived number" *plausible*, not enough to assert it as established mechanism.

**The meta-analytic evidence is the anchor.** According to PubMed, Fiani et al.'s 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis (30 meta-analyzable studies, RCTs and pre-post, any diagnosis) concluded that SSRIs **"reduce skin conductance response but have no or inconclusive effects on other ANS outcomes, depending on study design"**; the RMSSD effect was a *decrease* in RCTs (SMD −0.48) but an *increase* in pre-post designs (SMD +0.27), while **TCAs** reduced parasympathetic markers and agomelatine *increased* high-frequency power ([Fiani et al., 2023, *Eur Neuropsychopharmacol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.euroneuro.2023.03.013)). This is the single most load-bearing source: it makes the SSRI-vs-TCA distinction explicit and shows the SSRI direction is design-contingent rather than robust.

**The strongest single signal *for* a lowering effect — and it names citalopram.** A large propensity-score-controlled cross-sectional study (n=10,466 non-users plus users of five SSRIs) found that **users of all SSRIs except fluoxetine displayed lower HRV than non-users**, with citalopram users among the lowered group, though paroxetine showed the largest decrement and citalopram a milder one ([Kemp et al., 2016, *Psychosom Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1097/PSY.0000000000000336)). The authors stress the effect is drug-specific, not a uniform SSRI-class property — directly relevant to a citalopram-only story. Limitation: cross-sectional, non-randomized, confounding-by-indication possible.

**The "SSRIs don't fix depression's HRV deficit" thread.** An earlier Kemp meta-analysis (18 studies, 673 depressed, 407 controls) established that depression *itself* lowers HRV and that **SSRIs, mirtazapine and nefazodone did not significantly change HRV despite symptom response**, whereas TCAs decreased it ([Kemp et al., 2010, *Biol Psychiatry*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biopsych.2009.12.012)). This matters for the page's logic: an SSRI is not reliably an HRV-*mover* in either direction in depressed populations; the cleaner pharmacological HRV-lowering belongs to the anticholinergic TCAs.

**Escitalopram (the citalopram enantiomer) and blunted autonomic *reactivity*.** A double-blind placebo-controlled crossover in 30 healthy men found long-term escitalopram **blunted autonomic reactivity** to a pharmacological panic challenge — under escitalopram, subjects showed smaller HR rises and smaller HRV swings to the stressor than under placebo ([Agorastos et al., 2014, *Int J Neuropsychopharmacol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1093/ijnp/pyu053)). This supports a "flattening of autonomic *responsiveness*" reading rather than a simple resting-HRV drop. A rat study adds mechanistic colour: chronic escitalopram produced **parasympathetic predominance** (lower sympathetic, higher parasympathetic HRV components) alongside a small bradycardia ([Veríssimo et al., 2018, *Eur J Pharmacol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ejphar.2018.02.003)) — notably a direction that, if it held in humans, could read as *less* arousal, complicating a clean "reads higher stress" story.

**Citalopram's cardiac profile is mild.** A large prospective+retrospective safety analysis (>6,000 ECGs, 1,789 citalopram patients) found the **only** consistent cardiac effect of citalopram was a small heart-rate reduction (≤8 bpm), with no effect on conduction intervals ([Rasmussen et al., 1999, *J Clin Psychopharmacol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1097/00004714-199910000-00004)). A small resting bradycardia is the most reproducible autonomic fingerprint of citalopram, not a dramatic HRV collapse.

**On the device-validation question — say it plainly.** No retrieved study validates a Garmin-class consumer "stress" or "body-battery" transform under SSRI treatment. The mechanism-level chain (SSRI → some autonomic change → HRV shift → derived stress index) is reasoned, not measured, for these devices. **The page must say device-specific validation does not exist.**

## RQ2 — SSRIs / antidepressants for Long COVID and PAIS

**Reader synthesis (one paragraph).** There is a genuine, recently-energized hypothesis that serotonin is depleted in Long COVID and that SSRIs might therefore help — but the evidence is preliminary and contested. The headline mechanistic paper (Wong et al., *Cell* 2023) proposed that viral inflammation lowers serotonin and thereby impairs vagal signalling and memory; it generated real excitement but also a serious published methodological rebuttal arguing its core serotonin measurements are unreliable. For *acute* COVID, fluvoxamine (an SSRI and sigma-1 receptor agonist) has the best evidence: the TOGETHER trial showed it cut hospitalization, and meta-analysis supports a benefit at adequate dose started early — but this is about preventing acute deterioration, not treating established Long COVID. For *Long COVID treatment* specifically, the evidence thins to one supportive observational signal and scoping reviews; no adequately-powered randomized trial of an SSRI as a Long COVID *treatment* has reported a clean positive. Honest bottom line as of 2026: a biologically plausible hypothesis with one strong acute-illness trial, real mechanistic controversy, and only weak/observational support for the chronic-treatment use that this project's intervention actually represents.

**The serotonin-depletion hypothesis (the mechanistic spark).** According to PubMed, Wong et al. proposed that in post-acute sequelae of viral infection, **type-I-interferon-driven inflammation reduces serotonin** via reduced tryptophan absorption, platelet changes, and increased MAO turnover, and that **peripheral serotonin reduction impairs vagus-nerve activity** and hippocampal/memory function — explicitly tying together viral persistence, inflammation, hypercoagulability and autonomic dysfunction ([Wong et al., 2023, *Cell*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2023.09.013)). This is the "Wong/Rabi-type" work the brief names; it is mechanistically ambitious and influential.

**...but it has a serious published rebuttal — cite both.** A commentary argues the *Cell* paper's blood-centrifugation procedure would have produced platelet-depleted plasma, making its reported plasma-serotonin values **"30 to 60 times too high"** and unreliable, and notes that **no data were shown demonstrating SSRIs actually restore circulating serotonin** (indeed SSRIs would be expected to *lower* platelet serotonin) ([Anderson et al., 2024, *J Inflamm Res*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.2147/JIR.S456000)). Honesty demands the page treat the serotonin-depletion → "give an SSRI" logic as a contested hypothesis, not settled mechanism.

**Fluvoxamine for *acute* COVID — the strongest trial evidence (but not Long COVID treatment).** The TOGETHER randomized platform trial (741 fluvoxamine vs 756 placebo, high-risk symptomatic Brazilian outpatients, 100 mg twice daily for 10 days) found fluvoxamine **reduced the composite hospitalization endpoint** (RR 0.68, 95% BCI 0.52–0.88; 99.8% probability of superiority) ([Reis et al., 2021, *Lancet Glob Health*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2214-109X(21)00448-4)). A 2024 systematic review/meta-analysis of 14 fluvoxamine studies (7,153 patients) confirmed reduced clinical deterioration, with **best outcomes at ≥200 mg/day started early**, and frames the benefit through the **sigma-1 receptor / anti-inflammatory** mechanism rather than serotonin reuptake ([Prasanth et al., 2024, *Sci Rep*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-024-64260-9)). Crucial caveat: these are *acute-infection, prevent-deterioration* results — they do **not** establish SSRIs as a treatment for *established* Long COVID.

**SSRIs and Long COVID *risk/treatment* — thin and observational.** A large EHR study (N3C, n=302,626 with pre-existing depression, restricting to one indication to limit confounding) found SSRI use during acute COVID was associated with **modestly lower 12-month Long COVID risk** (adjusted causal RR 0.92, 95% CI 0.86–0.99) ([Butzin-Dozier et al., 2024, *BMC Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12916-024-03655-x)) — supportive but observational, prevention-flavoured, and a small effect. A scoping review of antidepressants in COVID concluded the best "anti-COVID" evidence exists for **fluvoxamine and, to a lesser extent, fluoxetine**, while explicitly noting that controlled studies in **post/long-COVID populations are still warranted** ([Bonnet & Juckel, 2022, *J Clin Psychopharmacol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1097/JCP.0000000000001543)). The honest bottom line: for the *chronic-treatment* use this project represents, randomized evidence is essentially absent.

## RQ3 — Carla Rus's theory and study, and emotional blunting

**Reader synthesis (one paragraph).** Carla Rus's work is sourceable and peer-reviewed: a 2023 *Scientific Reports* paper reporting that 95 post-COVID patients treated with SSRIs mostly improved, with brain fog and sensory overload improving most. It is, however, **exploratory by its own description** — a questionnaire study with no control group, reliant on self-report and a treated-and-surveyed sample — so it is a hypothesis-generating signal, not proof, and the user's lived experience ("less brain fog, fewer headaches") is *consistent with what Rus reports* without either verifying the other. Her proposed mechanism centres on the kynurenine/tryptophan pathway and serotonin in the context of neuroinflammation. Separately, **emotional blunting is a well-documented SSRI effect**, more associated with serotonergic than non-serotonergic antidepressants and reported with citalopram specifically, and the clinical literature supports **gradual, flexible tapering** as the sound response to discontinuation — both directly relevant to the user's arc of feeling toned-down and choosing a slow taper.

**Carla Rus's study — sourceable and peer-reviewed.** According to PubMed, Rus et al. treated **95 post-COVID-syndrome patients with SSRIs** and, using an exploratory questionnaire, found **two-thirds had a reasonably good to strong response, ~a quarter moderate, ~10% none**, with **brain fog and sensory overload decreasing most**, followed by chronic fatigue and dysautonomia; they propose seven candidate neurobiological mechanisms centred on the **kynurenine pathway** competing with serotonin for tryptophan under inflammation ([Rus et al., 2023, *Sci Rep*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-023-45072-9)). **Limitations are explicit and severe for inference:** exploratory design, **no control group**, self-report questionnaire outcomes, a sample that was both selected for and surveyed after treatment, and authors listed as independent researchers — it is hypothesis-generating, not confirmatory. (The brief's recollection of the paper as *Scientific Reports*, ~95 patients, is accurate; the recollection of citalopram/fluvoxamine/fluoxetine/escitalopram/venlafaxine as her named agents and a sigma-1-agonist framing is **partly** consistent with the literature but the agent list and dose-titration protocol are not all detailed in this primary paper's abstract — those specifics likely live in her Dutch-language grey-literature protocol pages, **not** independently verified here.)

**Is the user's lived account consistent with the literature?** "Less brain fog, fewer headaches on an SSRI for Long COVID" is **consistent with what Rus reports** (brain fog improved most in her cohort) and broadly coherent with the serotonin/anti-inflammatory hypothesis — while remaining, on both sides, unproven for any individual. This is the right register for the page: the literature *speaks to* the lived account without verifying it.

**Emotional blunting is a documented SSRI effect — citalopram included.** A qualitative/quantitative analysis of patient-reported experiences across nine antidepressants found **emotional blunting was reported more frequently by users of serotonergic agents than non-serotonergic ones**, and was among the side effects most inversely associated with overall satisfaction; citalopram was one of the agents analyzed ([Camino et al., 2022, *Psychol Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1017/S0033291722000678)). This documents that the user's experience — feeling emotions "toned down" while not depressed or anxious — is a recognized, serotonergic-specific phenomenon, not idiosyncratic.

**Slow tapering is the clinically sound instinct.** A qualitative interview study of SSRI withdrawal found participants emphasized that **gradual and flexible tapering** (with attention to timing) enabled a manageable withdrawal, and described the lifting of **emotional blunting and cognitive suppression** as part of discontinuation ([Mahmood et al., 2024, *Health Expect*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/hex.13966)). This supports the user's instinct toward a slow, deliberate taper as aligned with the lived-experience evidence base. (The actual *stop* sits outside this research's window, per the brief.)

## What changes for the citalopram page

- **The measured dose-response stays.** It is local n=1 evidence observed in this body and is untouched by this review.
- **The mechanism explanation changes.** The current unsourced line "SSRIs blunt that variability" is **too strong for the literature** and should be rewritten to state the HRV evidence is **mixed** (RQ1 verdict): a plausible, partially-supported direction (citalopram/SSRIs *can* associate with mildly lower HRV or blunted autonomic reactivity, especially in controlled designs) but **not** an established uniform effect, with **no consumer-device validation** for the HRV→"stress" transform under SSRIs.
- **The page gains a sourced "why".** RQ2/RQ3 let the intervention read as a *reasoned bet on a real hypothesis* (serotonin-depletion / Rus), with honest strength labels (plausible-but-contested mechanism; one strong *acute* trial; thin chronic-treatment evidence).
- **The lived strand is now backed as a recognized phenomenon, not verified as fact.** Emotional blunting (Camino) and slow-taper soundness (Mahmood) contextualise the first-person account without elevating it to a finding.
