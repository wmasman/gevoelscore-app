# How Much Does RESTING Heart Rate Change With Age in Midlife? A Focused External Literature Review of the Aging Slope (bpm per Year) for a ~40-to-45-Year-Old

*Evidence retrieved from PubMed/PMC and web literature search (DOIs linked throughout). Purpose: to put a defensible numeric bound on how much RESTING heart rate (RHR) changes per year and per decade due to normal aging in a healthy middle-aged adult (~40 to 50 years), so that the maximum plausible aging contribution to an observed multi-year RHR rise can be quantified and, if small, set aside as a minor bounded term rather than treated as a material confounder.*

**No-project-data line.** This review reads NO project n=1 data. Every number below is from published population/physiology literature. It is an external prior about the aging axis specifically, not evidence from this body. It is a focused companion to the broader confounder catalog ([`longterm_rhr_confounders_catalog_review.md`](longterm_rhr_confounders_catalog_review.md), which lists aging as row 1 and marks it a weak trend term); this review does that one row in depth.

---

## The critical distinction, stated first so it cannot be conflated

There are two different "heart rate vs age" facts, and only one of them is relevant here.

- **MAXIMAL heart rate declines strongly and reliably with age.** The classic relationships (the old 220 minus age, and the better-calibrated Tanaka HRmax = 208 minus 0.7 times age) give a decline of roughly **0.7 to 1.0 bpm per year** in the age-predicted MAXIMUM. This is large, monotonic, and well established. **It is NOT the quantity we want, and it must not leak into the resting-HR estimate.** Any number in the region of ~0.7 to 1 bpm/yr belongs to MAX HR, not resting HR.

- **RESTING heart rate is a different quantity with a much flatter age relationship.** Across healthy adulthood, RHR is roughly stable, with only a small age-related change whose direction is **mixed** across the literature (several large studies find it slightly LOWER at older ages cross-sectionally; some longitudinal / within-person work finds small rises driven mostly by lifestyle rather than age). A physiology synthesis states the contrast plainly: with chronological age, maximal HR decreases while **resting HR is relatively stable with age in healthy individuals**, even as heart-rate variability declines ([autonomic-aging reference dataset, Schumann & Bar, 2022, *Sci Data*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41597-022-01202-y)). The resting-HR aging slope is therefore an order of magnitude smaller than the max-HR slope, and this review quantifies it.

Everything below concerns RESTING HR only.

---

## Two key deliverables (stated up front)

1. **Resting-HR aging per-year estimate + upper bound.** Best central estimate for a healthy 40-to-45-year-old: **essentially flat, on the order of |0.1| to 0.2 bpm per year** in absolute value, and in several of the largest cross-sections the sign is NEGATIVE (RHR slightly lower at older ages, i.e. ~ -0.15 bpm/yr). A defensible **UPPER bound on the UPWARD aging contribution is ~0.3 bpm per year** (equivalently ~3 bpm per decade); values above that reflect max-HR physiology, lifestyle change, or disease, not resting-HR aging.
2. **Confounder verdict:** for a multi-year resting-HR trend of order **~1 bpm per year**, aging is a **MINOR, bounded term, NOT a material confounder.** At the upper bound it explains at most ~a quarter to a third of a 1 bpm/yr slope, and the central estimate explains a fraction of that (or works in the wrong direction). Aging cannot manufacture a ~1 bpm/yr rise.

---

## 1. Cross-sectional large-cohort resting HR by age: flat to slightly falling across the 40s

The largest consumer-scale cross-sections do not show resting HR climbing through midlife; if anything they show it modestly LOWER at older ages once other factors are held constant.

- **Health eHeart Study (N = 66,788; ~3.1 million PPG heart-rate measurements).** In multivariable linear regression, **increasing age was associated with a REDUCED heart rate** (while more medical conditions, female sex, and higher BMI raised it) ([Avram et al., 2019, *npj Digit Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-019-0134-9)). The reported adult age gradient is small, on the order of **~1 to 1.5 bpm LOWER per decade** across the adult range, i.e. roughly -0.1 to -0.15 bpm/yr. Direction: DOWN with age, not up.

- **Wearable cohort of 92,457 adults (~33 million daily RHR values).** This retrospective longitudinal wearable study characterized daily RHR by age, sex, sleep, BMI, and season, and found only **modest age associations** relative to the within- and between-person variability that dominates the signal; age was not a strong upward driver of RHR ([Quer et al., 2020, *PLoS One*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0227709)). The load-bearing sources of variance were BMI, sleep, sex, and season, not an aging trend.

- **Life-course cohort, same individuals age 6 to 69 (MRC NSHD, N = 4,779, ~26,000 observations).** RHR **decreases with age from childhood and PLATEAUS in adulthood**: the steep fall is in childhood/adolescence, and by mid-adulthood the trajectory is near-flat ([O'Hare et al., 2018, *JAMA Pediatr*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamapediatrics.2017.5525)). For the 40s specifically this means the age term is close to zero slope.

- **Midlife anchor value.** In a focused midlife women's cohort (N = 479, Australian Longitudinal Study on Women's Health), mean RHR at **age 41 to 46 was 75 bpm (SD 11)** ([Mielke et al., 2023, *J Phys Act Health*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1123/jpah.2023-0082)), consistent with population midlife means and with the point that RHR at 45 is not systematically elevated versus 40 by aging.

**Read for the 40s decade:** the cross-sectional resting-HR difference across the 40s is small and, in the biggest datasets, in the LOWER direction with older age (roughly **1 to 1.5 bpm per decade lower**, i.e. ~ -0.1 to -0.15 bpm/yr), with life-course data showing a near-flat plateau. There is **no large cohort showing resting HR rising by anything like ~1 bpm/yr through the 40s from aging.** The literature is genuinely mixed in SIGN but consistent in MAGNITUDE: the effect is small either way.

---

## 2. Longitudinal / within-person resting HR change in middle age: change is real, but driven by lifestyle and disease, not by aging

The most relevant design is repeated measures of the same people through their 40s. Here the key finding is that within-person RHR DOES move over years, but the movers are modifiable factors and clinical state, not chronological age.

- **CARDIA (N = 4,343; mean baseline age ~30; median 26-year follow-up into midlife).** Temporal within-person changes in resting HR from young adulthood into middle age were driven by **smoking (beta = 1.58), higher alcohol consumption, lower physical activity, sex, and worse cardiac diastolic function** ([Nwabuo et al., 2020, *Am J Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.amjmed.2019.12.035)). Chronological aging is not the driver; the longitudinal RHR change tracks lifestyle and emerging cardiac dysfunction. This is direct evidence that a within-person multi-year RHR rise, where it occurs, points to modifiable/clinical causes, not to an aging clock.

- **DCCT/EDIC (N = 1,402; annual ECGs over 22 years; linear mixed models; baseline mean age 33).** Longitudinal resting HR over two decades was modeled with mixed models; the persistent between-group difference was explained by **glycemic control (HbA1c)**, and higher HR predicted cardiovascular events. The dominant longitudinal signal is metabolic/treatment state, not an age slope ([Keshavarzi et al., 2021, *Diabetes Care*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.2337/dc20-2387)). (Type 1 diabetes cohort, so the absolute level is not a healthy-population norm, but the design isolates that within-person RHR over 22 years is governed by modifiable state, not age.)

- **Change-in-HR-over-years across three classic cohorts (Paris Prospective I, Whitehall I, Framingham).** Increases in resting HR over a **5-year (Paris) and 8-year (Framingham)** window were associated with higher mortality (Paris HR 1.20 per 10 bpm increase over 5 years; Framingham HR 1.13 men / 1.09 women per 10 bpm over 8 years) ([Gaye et al., 2024, *Sci Rep*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-024-70806-8)). The decision-relevant point: a within-person rise of ~10 bpm over 5 to 8 years is treated in this literature as a **pathological / prognostic signal, not as expected normal aging.** Normal aging does not book that rise; when RHR climbs materially over a few years it is flagged as risk, which is the opposite of "explained away by getting older."

**Read for a within-person 40-to-45 window:** repeated-measures evidence gives **no positive per-year aging slope** for resting HR in midlife. Within-person RHR change over a few years is attributable to weight, fitness/activity, smoking, alcohol, glycemic/metabolic state, medication, and cardiac disease (the confounders catalogued in the sister review), with chronological age contributing near zero. A CI-bearing "aging bpm/yr" from a clean healthy-midlife repeated-measures design is thin in the literature precisely because the effect is small and swamped by these other terms; this thinness is flagged, not papered over.

---

## 3. Direction and magnitude consensus for a 40-to-45-year-old

Pulling the strands together:

- **Central best estimate:** the aging-only resting-HR slope at age ~40 to 45 is **near zero, |0.1| to 0.2 bpm/yr in absolute value**, and in the largest cross-sections the SIGN is negative (older age -> slightly lower RHR, ~ -0.1 to -0.15 bpm/yr from Health eHeart and the life-course plateau).
- **Plausible UPPER bound on an UPWARD aging effect:** **~0.3 bpm/yr (~3 bpm/decade).** This is a deliberately generous ceiling; no retrieved large study supports a healthy-aging resting-HR RISE steeper than this in midlife, and most support flat-to-falling.
- **Mixed-evidence flag (honest):** the SIGN is not unanimous. Cross-sectional big data lean slightly negative; some within-person work shows small rises but attributes them to lifestyle/disease, not age; the life-course gold-standard shows a plateau. The one thing every strand agrees on is that the MAGNITUDE of the pure-aging component is small (well under half a bpm/yr).
- **What it is NOT:** it is NOT the ~0.7 to 1 bpm/yr max-HR decline. That number is excluded here by construction.

---

## 4. The bound we need: maximum total resting-HR rise from aging over a 4-to-5-year window (age 40 to 45)

Taking the generous upper bound of **~0.3 bpm/yr** and applying it over the 4-to-5-year window from age ~40 to ~45:

- **Maximum total resting-HR rise attributable to normal aging alone: ~1.2 to 1.5 bpm over the full 4-to-5-year span** (0.3 bpm/yr times 4 to 5 years). Call it **~1.5 bpm as a rounded upper bound over five years.**
- **Central expectation: ~0 to +1 bpm total over five years, and plausibly NEGATIVE** (a slight fall), given that the largest cross-sections and the life-course plateau point flat-to-down.
- **Citation basis:** the ~3 bpm/decade ceiling is anchored by the cross-sectional adult gradients (Health eHeart ~1 to 1.5 bpm/decade, lower with age; [Avram et al., 2019; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-019-0134-9)) and the life-course plateau ([O'Hare et al., 2018; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamapediatrics.2017.5525)); the upper bound is set above the largest observed adult gradient to be conservative, and the near-zero central value is corroborated by the physiology synthesis that resting HR is "relatively stable with age in healthy individuals" ([Schumann & Bar, 2022, *Sci Data*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41597-022-01202-y)).

**One-line bound:** over age 40 to 45, normal aging can contribute **at most ~1.5 bpm total** of resting-HR RISE (and quite plausibly zero or a slight fall); it cannot contribute more.

---

## 5. Confounder verdict: MINOR, not material, for a ~1 bpm/yr trend

**Verdict: for a multi-year resting-HR trend of order ~1 bpm per year in a 40-to-45-year-old, aging is a MINOR, bounded term, NOT a material confounder.**

Reasoning, stated plainly:

- A **~1 bpm/yr** trend implies **~4 to 5 bpm over the window.** The MAXIMUM aging contribution is **~0.3 bpm/yr / ~1.5 bpm total** at the generous upper bound, i.e. **at most ~a quarter to a third** of a 1 bpm/yr slope, and the CENTRAL estimate is near zero or negative.
- Therefore aging **cannot manufacture** a ~1 bpm/yr rise, and even at its ceiling it accounts for only a small, bounded fraction of one. It is a low-weight covariate to acknowledge, not a driver to model as the explanation.
- The direction caveat only strengthens this: if the true midlife aging effect is slightly NEGATIVE (as the biggest cross-sections suggest), aging would work AGAINST an observed rise, meaning the non-aging drivers would have to be even larger to produce the observed trend.
- The material confounders for a multi-year RHR rise live elsewhere (body-weight change, fitness/detraining, medication step-changes such as citalopram, device/firmware artifact, thyroid/anemia, and Long COVID itself) and are handled in the sister reviews; aging is explicitly not one of them at this magnitude.

**Bottom line:** aging is a real but small term. Bound it at ~0.3 bpm/yr (~1.5 bpm over five years), carry it as a minor acknowledged covariate, and do NOT let it absorb a ~1 bpm/yr trend, which is roughly 3-plus times larger than aging can plausibly supply.

---

## Honest flags where the evidence is thin or mixed

- **The SIGN of the midlife resting-HR aging slope is genuinely mixed** (cross-sectional big data lean slightly negative; some within-person data show small positive drift attributed to lifestyle). Only the small MAGNITUDE is unanimous. Do not assert a confident positive aging slope.
- **A clean, CI-bearing "resting-HR bpm/yr from aging alone" in healthy 40-to-50-year-olds is under-reported** precisely because the effect is small and confounded by weight/fitness/smoking/alcohol/metabolic state; the best within-person cohorts (CARDIA, DCCT/EDIC) start younger than 40 or are disease cohorts, so the midlife healthy-only slope is inferred, not directly tabulated. This is a real gap.
- **The ~0.3 bpm/yr upper bound is a conservative ceiling, not a point estimate.** It is set deliberately above the largest observed adult cross-sectional gradient so the confounder verdict is robust; the expected value is lower (near zero or negative).
- **Max-HR numbers were held out by design.** The ~0.7 to 1 bpm/yr figure is max-HR physiology and does not enter the resting-HR bound. Any future reader tempted to import it should be stopped: it is the wrong quantity.
- Purposive (not systematic) PubMed/PMC + web search, run 2026-06-30 to 2026-07-02. Landmark-and-representative papers, not an exhaustive review.
