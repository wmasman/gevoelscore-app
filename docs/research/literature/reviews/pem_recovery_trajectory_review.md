# Recovery Curves After a Crash: What Does the Literature Say a Post-Exertional-Malaise Recovery Trajectory Looks Like? A Cited External Anchor for the "Felt-State Snaps Back, Autonomic Settles Slowly" Finding

*Evidence retrieved from PubMed and web search (DOIs and source links throughout). Purpose: this brief grounds one specific project finding in published literature ONLY. The finding: after a crash (a post-exertional-malaise / PEM episode, operationalised by a low self-rated felt-state), the participant's self-rated felt-state recovers in a sharp V, from nadir to near-baseline in roughly 2 to 3 days, then plateaus at a constrained ceiling, while an overnight autonomic-load proxy (Garmin HRV-derived overnight stress) settles far more slowly, over roughly 2 weeks. This review asks the external question: what do published PEM recovery-trajectory studies say the shape and timescale of post-crash recovery should be, and is a fast symptom rebound paired with a slow autonomic settle consistent with, or divergent from, that literature?*

*No-project-data line: this review reads NO project data. It cites no n=1 numbers as evidence. It supplies the INDEPENDENT literature prior the participant's descriptive finding is to be compared against, so the comparison stays honest rather than circular. See [[findings-are-provisional]], [[site-is-medium-not-subject]].*

*Distinct from the sister reviews already in this folder. [`PEM_HRV_heart_rate_literature_review.md`](PEM_HRV_heart_rate_literature_review.md) asks whether an HRV/HR signal tracks a crash at all (detection/prediction). [`expected_shapes_autonomic_signals_review.md`](expected_shapes_autonomic_signals_review.md) asks what SHAPE the felt-state to stress relationship should take (the non-linear cross-sectional curve). [`acute_viral_infection_autonomic_signature_review.md`](acute_viral_infection_autonomic_signature_review.md) covers the ACUTE infection signature. That temporal-dissociation question is not answered by any of the three.*

## 1. Two verdicts, stated up front (load-bearing)

**Verdict A: PEM recovery shape and timescale (from the literature).** According to PubMed, the published, quantified PEM recovery signature is a **PROLONGED recovery measured in days to weeks, not a fast rebound**, and it is the prolongation, not the speed, that the operational definitions treat as the hallmark. The single best quantification (Moore et al., 2023, ME/CFS) reports a **mean recovery time of about 12.7 days after a standardised exertional stressor (range 1 to 64 days)**, versus about 2.1 days in sedentary controls, with a modelled decay that is "extremely prolonged" ([Moore et al., 2023, *Medicina*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3390/medicina59030571)). So the population/physiology prior for the autonomic-load recovery being on a **roughly two-week** timescale is well-supported. A **2 to 3 day** recovery, by contrast, is at the fast tail of the distribution, closer to the control-range recovery than to the ME/CFS-typical prolonged one.

**Verdict B: does the symptom-fast / autonomic-slow dissociation match the literature?** **Partly, and in the direction the finding claims, but the exact pairing is under-studied.** The strongest matching evidence is at population scale in wearables, not from within-episode PEM studies: after acute COVID-19, an OBJECTIVE autonomic channel (resting heart rate) took on average about **79 days** to return to baseline, while BEHAVIOURAL channels (step count, sleep quantity) returned in about **32 and 24 days** respectively ([Radin et al., 2021, *JAMA Netw Open*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2021.15959)). That is a real, published, same-direction dissociation: the heart-rate/autonomic channel is the slowest to settle, lagging the more behavioural/subjective channels. The direction of the participant's finding (felt-state fast, autonomic slow) is therefore **concordant** with the best available evidence. The honest caveat is that no retrieved study measured a self-rated felt-state and an overnight HRV-derived channel through the SAME spontaneous crash, so the precise 2-to-3-day vs 2-week pairing is an extrapolation from adjacent literatures, not a directly replicated result. PEM recovery-trajectory quantification is genuinely thin (Section 7).

## 2. PEM recovery timescale and shape (deliverable 1)

**The definitions make prolonged recovery a defining criterion, not an incidental feature.** PEM is operationally characterised by two temporal signatures: **delayed onset** and **prolonged recovery**. The DePaul Symptom Questionnaire PEM module (DSQ-PEM), the instrument most PEM studies use, scores PEM partly on a **recovery duration exceeding 24 hours** ([Jason & Brown, 2014, *Fatigue*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1080/21641846.2014.928014); applied as the >24h recovery criterion in e.g. [Twomey et al., 2020, *J Pain Symptom Manage*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jpainsymman.2020.02.012)). The Institute of Medicine 2015 report (*Beyond ME/CFS: Redefining an Illness*) similarly frames PEM around exacerbation after minimal exertion with a recovery period that is disproportionately long. Factor-analytic work on 2308 patients confirms PEM is a distinct, stable symptom domain across case definitions ([Conroy et al., 2022, *Disabil Rehabil*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1080/09638288.2022.2043462)). So "how long is recovery" is not a peripheral question, it is baked into what qualifies as PEM at all: a same-day bounce-back does not meet the standard PEM recovery-duration threshold.

**The quantified shape: a prolonged, slowly-decaying tail.** The most direct quantification of PEM recovery duration and shape is Moore et al. (2023). Eighty ME/CFS patients and 64 controls underwent a two-day cardiopulmonary exercise test (2-day CPET) and then self-rated a nine-symptom severity scale repeatedly for at least 10 days. According to PubMed, judged recovery time was **12.7 (SE 1.2) days in ME/CFS versus 2.1 (SE 0.2) days in controls** (highly significant), the ME/CFS range ran **1 to 64 days**, and pharmacokinetic-style models showed an **"extremely prolonged decay" of the PEM response** ([Moore et al., 2023, *Medicina*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3390/medicina59030571)). Read as a shape, that is not a symmetric fast-in-fast-out spike: it is a drop followed by a slow, long-tailed return, with a subset taking three weeks or more (though the authors note fewer than 10% of ME/CFS patients took more than three weeks). This is the load-bearing "expected shape" for a felt-state or symptom recovery curve after a provoked crash, and its central tendency is close to two weeks.

**How the participant's 2-to-3-day felt-state rebound sits against this.** A 2-to-3-day self-rated return to near-baseline is **faster than the ME/CFS-typical ~12.7-day recovery**, sitting nearer the control-range recovery (~2 days). Three non-exclusive readings, all supportable from the literature: (i) the participant's crashes are, by this metric, on the milder / faster-recovering end of the PEM severity spectrum; (ii) the day-resolution self-report captures the "can function again" threshold rather than full physiological normalisation, so it reads fast even while physiology is still perturbed (Section 3 and 4); (iii) the plateau at a "constrained ceiling" after the sharp V is itself consistent with an incomplete recovery, i.e. the felt-state returns to a capped baseline rather than to a pre-illness normal, which is exactly the chronic-illness pattern rather than a clean full rebound.

## 3. The delayed-onset plus prolonged-duration hallmark, and what it does to a day-resolution curve (deliverable 2)

**Delayed onset is documented and variable.** In a patient-centred survey of 150 ME/CFS patients, Chu et al. quantified the temporal structure of PEM directly: **84% endured PEM for 24 hours or more**, and **11% reported a consistent delay of at least 24 hours between the trigger and PEM onset** ([Chu et al., 2018, *PLoS One*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0197811)). Onset commonly lags the trigger by hours to a day or more, and recovery then extends over an "extended period." The practical upshot for a day-resolution recovery curve is important: **the nadir a daily tracker records may not be the day of the trigger, and the trigger-to-nadir and nadir-to-baseline legs are separate clocks.** A sharp V measured from the recorded nadir can coexist with a slower underlying perturbation that began before the nadir and persists after the felt-state has visibly returned.

**Would a self-rated recovery look fast even while physiology is still perturbed? The literature says yes, plausibly.** Molecular work speaks directly to this: longitudinal plasma proteomics across a 2-day CPET found the **most pronounced molecular dysregulation in ME/CFS during the RECOVERY phase, coinciding with the onset of PEM**, with persistent dysregulation of immune, metabolic and neuromuscular pathways ([Germain et al., 2025, *Mol Cell Proteomics*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mcpro.2025.101467)). In other words, the biology is measurably most disturbed during the window a patient is subjectively recovering. A felt-state that has bounced back to near-baseline is therefore not strong evidence that the underlying physiological state has normalised; the two clocks can and do run at different speeds. This is the mechanistic warrant for expecting an autonomic channel to lag a symptom channel, and it directly supports the participant's finding rather than contradicting it.

## 4. Autonomic / physiological recovery lagging symptom recovery: the key comparison (deliverable 2, continued)

This is the crux of the finding. The evidence is real and points the claimed way, but it is assembled from several adjacent literatures rather than one clean within-crash study.

**(a) Population wearable evidence: the objective channel is the slowest to settle.** The cleanest same-direction evidence is Radin et al. (2021). Tracking wearable data through acute COVID-19, COVID-positive individuals showed an initial **transient bradycardia** (a brief drop in resting heart rate roughly 9 to 15 days post-onset) **followed by a prolonged relative tachycardia that did not return to baseline until on average 79 days after symptom onset**, whereas **step count and sleep quantity returned to baseline much sooner, at about 32 and 24 days** ([Radin et al., 2021, *JAMA Netw Open*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2021.15959)). A subset (about 13.7%) stayed more than 5 bpm above baseline for more than 133 days. This is precisely the shape of the participant's dissociation at population scale: the **autonomic/cardiac channel is the last to settle, lagging behavioural channels by weeks**, and it even shows a biphasic (dip-then-elevated) course rather than a clean monotone return. The Long COVID follow-up from the same group found RHR and activity trajectories diverging from controls **for up to a year** in people with Long COVID ([Radin et al., 2024, *npj Digit Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-024-01238-x)), and a UK citizen-science cohort found resting heart rate **still significantly elevated in the post-COVID phase (12 to 16 weeks)** after diagnosis ([Stewart et al., 2024, *Lancet Digit Health*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2589-7500(24)00140-7)). A US military cohort likewise detected persistent physiological change (most commonly **elevated nightly heart rate and reduced HRV**) beyond the acute phase ([Borhani et al., 2025, *Sci Rep*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-025-15208-0)). Across these, the autonomic channel carries the long tail.

**(b) The 2-day CPET literature: a physiological deficit is still present 24 hours later, when symptoms may be easing.** The 2-day CPET paradigm is the closest objective analogue to a provoked crash. In the largest study (84 ME/CFS, 71 controls), ME/CFS patients failed to reproduce day-1 performance on day 2, with significant declines at peak effort in work, oxygen uptake, **heart rate and O2 pulse**, interpreted as autonomic dysregulation of blood flow and oxygen delivery, and not explained by deconditioning ([Keller et al., 2024, *J Transl Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12967-024-05410-5)). The point for THIS review is temporal: an objective physiological impairment is demonstrably still present a full day after the exertion. Note the honest tension flagged in the sister review: the same 2-day paradigm did **not** reproduce a day-2 deficit in Long COVID despite 80% reporting PEM ([Gattoni et al., 2024, *Respir Physiol Neurobiol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.resp.2024.104362)), so "objective deficit persists at 24h" is robust for ME/CFS and unsettled for Long COVID.

**(c) Sports-science precedent: perceptual and physiological channels genuinely recover on different clocks.** Outside the illness literature, the general phenomenon that a subjective/perceptual channel can recover ahead of, or on a different timeline from, an objective physiological channel is established. In a controlled altitude camp, wellness/perceptual measures and physical-performance measures acclimatised on **different, channel-specific time courses**, and the authors explicitly conclude the measures "should be used in combination" because they track different aspects of recovery ([Buchheit et al., 2013, *Br J Sports Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2013-092749)). Separately, cardiac autonomic (HRV) adaptations to training show a **different time course at rest versus in the immediate post-exercise window** ([Yamamoto et al., 2001, *Med Sci Sports Exerc*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1097/00005768-200109000-00012)). These do not study PEM, but they establish that a felt-fast / autonomic-slow split is a normal, expected property of multi-channel recovery data, not an artefact.

**Net for deliverable 2:** the direction of the participant's dissociation (subjective fast, autonomic slow) is **concordant with published evidence**, strongly so at the population-wearable level (Radin) and mechanistically (Germain), and supported by the general principle that channels recover on their own clocks (Buchheit). What is NOT directly published is the exact within-crash pairing of a daily felt-state V against an overnight HRV-derived 2-week settle in one individual, which is where the participant's data adds rather than merely confirms.

## 5. Minor dips versus major crashes: does recovery time scale with severity? (deliverable 3)

**Yes, recovery time and PEM severity scale with the magnitude of the episode and the disease, though the within-vs-between distinction matters.** The 2-day CPET severity study showed the day-2 physiological deterioration was **largest in severe ME/CFS** and scaled with disease severity grade ([van Campen et al., 2020, *Healthcare*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3390/healthcare8030192)). Epidemiologically, PEM severity itself varies systematically with patient factors (older age at onset, recurrent infections, gastrointestinal infectious precipitant), i.e. PEM is not a single fixed-size event ([Ghali et al., 2020, *J Transl Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12967-020-02419-4)). And the wide recovery range in Moore et al. (1 to 64 days from a single standardised stressor) itself implies that episode/patient severity, not just the trigger, sets recovery length ([Moore et al., 2023; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3390/medicina59030571)). One caveat from Moore: within their sample, recovery time did NOT differ by the level of pre-test symptoms before the CPET, so the scaling is more about episode/disease severity than about the baseline symptom level going in. The reasonable literature-anchored expectation for the project: **a minor dip should recover faster than a major crash, and recovery length is a severity signal**: which supports treating the depth of the felt-state nadir and the length of the autonomic tail as related-but-separate severity readouts.

## 6. Wearable-detected recovery signatures (deliverable 4)

The consumer-wearable / actigraphy characterisation of the post-PEM recovery trajectory specifically is **thin but growing, and consistent with a slow autonomic tail**. The Radin population studies (Section 4a) are the strongest signal: RHR is the slowest channel to normalise, with a biphasic dip-then-elevated course ([Radin et al., 2021; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2021.15959); [Radin et al., 2024; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-024-01238-x)). Sleep architecture is also disturbed for an extended period post-COVID (reduced deep and light sleep versus matched controls) on wearable data ([Mekhael et al., 2022, *J Med Internet Res*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.2196/38000)). A 2025 scoping review of wearables for Long COVID management confirms the field is real but immature: 11 synthesizable studies, heart-rate variability / temperature / sleep / activity the common metrics, and a clear call for more validation ([Kukreti et al., 2025, *J Med Internet Res*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.2196/69506)). Critically, **no retrieved wearable study characterises the recovery trajectory of a single, spontaneous, self-defined PEM crash at day resolution with a paired subjective channel**: the closest analogues track the acute-infection recovery (Radin) rather than a discrete free-living crash. So wearable-detected recovery signatures exist for post-COVID recovery broadly and point to a slow autonomic tail, but the specific per-crash recovery-curve signature the project is describing remains largely uncharted, echoing the gap the sister PEM_HRV review already flagged for detection/prediction.

## 7. Honest read on evidence quality

- **PEM recovery-trajectory quantification is genuinely under-studied.** The number of studies that actually put a number on PEM recovery duration and shape is small; Moore et al. (2023) is close to being the singular direct quantification, and it uses a provoked lab stressor (2-day CPET), not a spontaneous crash. Treating one well-done study as "the" recovery-shape prior is a real limitation, honestly stated.
- **The dissociation evidence is assembled, not replicated within-crash.** The symptom-fast / autonomic-slow pairing is supported by combining a population wearable study (Radin), a molecular timing study (Germain), CPET persistence (Keller), and a general multi-channel-recovery principle (Buchheit). No single paper measured a daily felt-state and an overnight HRV-derived index through the same crash. The direction is well-supported; the exact pairing is an inference.
- **Condition-specificity is unresolved.** Much of the strongest recovery-duration and CPET evidence is ME/CFS; the 2-day CPET deficit did not reproduce in Long COVID (Gattoni), so mapping ME/CFS recovery shapes onto a Long COVID participant carries a known transfer risk.
- **Garmin's overnight "stress" is a proprietary HRV-derived index**, not a validated PEM-recovery instrument; the "autonomic settles over ~2 weeks" channel inherits both HRV's information and its black-box processing. The literature validates the underlying HRV/RHR channels as slow-to-settle (Radin, Borhani), not the specific Firstbeat transform.
- **Day resolution can understate a longer physiological recovery.** This is the central methodological flag: because the molecular/autonomic perturbation is measurably ongoing during subjective recovery (Germain; Radin's 79-day RHR tail), a day-resolution self-report V should be read as "felt-state functional threshold recrossed," NOT as "the episode is physiologically over." The project's own finding, that the autonomic channel keeps settling for ~2 weeks after the felt-state has plateaued, is exactly the correction the literature predicts is needed.
- **PubMed + web-search, English-language, purposive (not systematic) search**, run 2026-06-30/07-03. Landmark-paper core, not an exhaustive review.

## 8. Reader synthesis (one paragraph)

Based on articles retrieved from PubMed and web search, the published PEM recovery signature is a **prolonged, slowly-decaying recovery on a days-to-weeks timescale** (about 12.7 days central tendency after a provoked crash in ME/CFS, range 1 to 64 days, with an "extremely prolonged decay"), and the operational definitions make that prolongation, not any fast rebound, the hallmark ([Moore 2023](https://doi.org/10.3390/medicina59030571); [Chu 2018](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0197811); [Jason & Brown 2014](https://doi.org/10.1080/21641846.2014.928014)). Against that prior, a **2-to-3-day self-rated felt-state rebound is fast** (nearer the control range), most plausibly because a day-resolution self-report captures the functional-threshold recrossing while the underlying physiology is still perturbed, exactly the window where molecular dysregulation peaks ([Germain 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mcpro.2025.101467)) and where an objective autonomic channel is documented to lag: after acute COVID, resting heart rate took ~79 days to normalise while behavioural channels settled in ~24 to 32 days ([Radin 2021](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2021.15959)), RHR stayed elevated at 12 to 16 weeks in a UK cohort ([Stewart 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2589-7500(24)00140-7)), and objective deficits persist at 24 hours post-exertion in ME/CFS CPET ([Keller 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12967-024-05410-5)). The participant's finding: felt-state V in 2 to 3 days, autonomic settle over ~2 weeks: is therefore **concordant in direction with the literature** (subjective/behavioural fast, autonomic/cardiac slow) and its slow-tail timescale sits comfortably inside the published PEM recovery range; the honest divergence is only that the felt-state leg is fast relative to the ME/CFS norm and that no published study has paired these two exact channels through one spontaneous crash. Net: the dissociation matches the best available evidence, the ~2-week autonomic tail is exactly what the wearable literature predicts, and the day-resolution self-report should be read as understating, not contradicting, a longer physiological recovery.
