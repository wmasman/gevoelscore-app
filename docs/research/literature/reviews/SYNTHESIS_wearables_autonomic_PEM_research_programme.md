# Wearables, Autonomic Signals, and Post-Exertional Malaise in Post-Infectious Illness: A Synthesis of Five Reviews and the Research Programme They Point To

*A master document integrating five state-of-the-art literature reviews produced in this project. Primary evidence throughout was retrieved from PubMed (DOI links retained below); guideline context from NICE/CDC where noted. This synthesis does not introduce new searches — it threads the five completed strands into one narrative and states the single, fundable research programme they collectively imply.*

**The five component reviews:**
1. Consumer wrist-worn wearables for sleep and HRV monitoring in chronic-illness self-management
2. Heart-rate and HRV signals before, during and after a PEM crash (ME/CFS, Long COVID, QFS, GWI)
3. Autonomic signals beyond HRV — orthostatic intolerance and cerebral blood flow (folded into review 2)
4. Pacing / energy management — practices, effects on PEM, and mechanism
5. Measuring PEM — instruments, provocation protocols, and the ground-truth problem

---

## 1. The single question underneath all five

Every strand turned out to be a facet of one applied question:

> **Can a wearable device detect, anticipate, or guide the management of post-exertional malaise in people with post-infectious illness — and if so, how would we know it works?**

Read in sequence, the five reviews are not five topics but one argument with a missing keystone. Reviews 1–3 ask what we could *measure*; review 4 asks what we could *do* with it; review 5 exposes why none of it can be validated yet. The synthesis below shows how they lock together and where the one decisive gap sits.

---

## 2. What each strand established (the honest one-line each)

- **Wearables (R1):** Consumer devices reliably capture heart rate, activity and gross sleep in patients, and wearable-derived sleep *regularity* already links to hard outcomes (e.g. depression relapse, [Matcham 2024; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jad.2024.07.136)) — but sleep staging, SpO₂ and especially **HRV are under-validated in disease populations**, Garmin is under-studied relative to Fitbit, and the self-management loop is rarely closed. Validation in healthy adults is doing too much of the work (e.g. Garmin Forerunner 245, [Miller 2022; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3390/s22166317)).

- **HRV/HR around crashes (R2):** A free-living wearable HRV signal tracking a real crash is **largely unstudied**. The strongest objective evidence is the two-day CPET, which shows a reproducible post-exertional decline in **ME/CFS** ([Keller 2024; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12967-024-05410-5)) — but **not in Long COVID** ([Gattoni 2024; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.resp.2024.104362)) — and the one direct attempt to *predict* PEM from acute heart-rate responses **failed** ([Boruch 2021; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.lifs.2021.119701)).

- **Autonomic signals beyond HRV (R3):** Autonomic dysfunction is a **robust, reproducible baseline feature** of ME/CFS — meta-analytic HR/HRV abnormalities ([Nelson 2019; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1097/MD.0000000000017600)) and abnormal cerebral blood-flow reduction on tilt even without POTS ([van Campen 2023; DOI](https://doi.org/10.14814/phy2.15639)) — but this characterises the *phenotype* and the *provoked* state, not a spontaneous crash.

- **Pacing (R4):** The patient-preferred, now guideline-endorsed strategy. It most clearly **reduces PEM episode frequency, duration and severity** ([Parker 2023; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1002/jmv.28373); [Godfrey 2024; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3390/jcm14010097)), less clearly changes disease trajectory, and rests on low-certainty, heterogeneous evidence (scoping review: 11 benefit / 4 null / 2 harm, [Sanal-Hayes 2023; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12967-023-04587-5)). Its own stated next step is **RCTs with objectively quantified pacing and validated PEM outcomes.**

- **Measuring PEM (R5):** Patient-reported instruments are mature (DSQ-PEM; [Brown & Jason 2014; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1080/21641846.2014.928014); [Murdock 2016; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11136-016-1406-3)) but capture *recalled, trait-level* PEM; the two-day CPET is objective but burdensome, snapshot, and non-generalising; and **real-time, ecological, wearable timestamping of a spontaneous episode is essentially absent.**

---

## 3. The through-lines that connect them

Four themes recur across all five strands and constitute the actual findings of this synthesis.

**(a) A timescale mismatch runs through everything.** PEM is delayed (peak often 8–72 h post-exertion, [Barhorst 2022; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1093/pm/pnab308)), fluctuating, and multi-day — yet every available tool is either retrospective (questionnaires), single-snapshot (CPET), or continuous-but-uninterpreted (raw wearable streams). Nothing currently samples at the timescale on which PEM actually unfolds. This single mismatch explains why detection, prediction, and validated pacing trials are all stalled.

**(b) Descriptive is strong, predictive is empty.** Across R2 and R3, the autonomic abnormality of these illnesses is well-described at baseline and under provocation, but every attempt to move from *description* to *forecasting an impending crash* has either failed or never been tried. The field has a robust portrait of the patient and almost no model of the event.

**(c) Condition-specificity must be designed in, not assumed.** The ME/CFS-vs-Long-COVID CPET divergence (R2) is the warning shot: pooling post-infectious conditions risks averaging away real effects. QFS, meanwhile, is a near-total blank across *all five* strands — no autonomic, no wearable, no validated PEM measurement work.

**(d) The loop is never closed because the label is missing.** R1 couldn't show wearables improve management; R4 couldn't prove pacing mechanistically prevents crashes; R2 couldn't test prediction — and in every case the proximate reason is the same: **there is no per-episode, time-stamped, validated PEM ground-truth.** Review 5 names the keystone the other four were missing.

---

## 4. The convergent gap, stated once

> **There is no validated method to label, in free-living conditions, that a specific PEM episode began at a specific time — and without that label, no wearable signal can be trained, no prediction model can be tested, and no pacing intervention can be objectively evaluated.**

Everything else is, in effect, waiting on this. It is simultaneously the cheapest gap to attack (it needs measurement design, not new hardware) and the highest-leverage (it unblocks all four other strands at once).

---

## 5. The research programme the five reviews jointly imply

A single, stageable programme follows directly from the synthesis. It is deliberately sequenced so each phase de-risks the next.

**Phase 0 — Establish the ground-truth label (the keystone; from R5).**
Build and validate an **EMA-anchored, multi-day PEM-capture protocol**: high-frequency patient-confirmed symptom reports (capturing onset, the delayed peak, and recovery, informed by the 8–72 h window and the measurable prodrome/"warning signals" of [Ghali 2021; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3390/jcm10112517)), scored against an explicit, pre-registered case definition (DSQ-PEM thresholds). Output: a per-episode, time-stamped PEM label. *Nothing downstream is valid without this.*

**Phase 1 — Layer continuous physiology onto the label (from R1–R3).**
Run a wrist-worn (or chest-strap) wearable continuously alongside Phase 0, capturing resting/overnight HRV, HR-relative-to-threshold, sleep regularity, and — where feasible — orthostatic/perfusion proxies. Pre-register which device and validate **HRV in the disease population**, not by extrapolation from healthy cohorts (the R1 caution).

**Phase 2 — Test detection, then prediction (from R2).**
With labels and signals aligned: first ask whether the wearable can *detect* a crash concurrently (the easier, still-unmet question), then whether multi-day trajectories *predict* onset (the question that has only ever been tried with failed in-task signals). Model **per-condition and per-person** — do not pool ME/CFS, Long COVID and QFS (the R2/R3 condition-specificity rule).

**Phase 3 — Close the management loop (from R4).**
Only once a signal is validated, run the RCT the pacing field has explicitly asked for: **objectively quantified, wearable-guided pacing vs usual pacing**, with the Phase 0 PEM label as the primary outcome — converting pacing from a behavioural guardrail into a measured, mechanism-linked intervention.

**Cross-cutting design commitments** (the recurring cautions, made into rules): pre-specify the case definition and instrument for every PEM label; report adherence and equity (device ownership is low in older/sicker patients); treat provocation as ethically capped and prefer naturalistic capture; and include QFS as a deliberately-sampled arm to begin filling the blank.

---

## 6. Honest assessment of the programme

- **What makes it tractable:** the keystone (Phase 0) is a measurement-design problem, not a hardware or biology breakthrough; the wearables already exist; the instruments already exist; the patient community is highly engaged.
- **What makes it hard:** label quality depends on patient burden tolerance (severe patients may not sustain frequent EMA — the very patients with the clearest signal); HRV validity in-population is unproven; severe/ bed-bound patients and orthostatic capture are technically awkward; and the field's controversy (PACE/NICE) means outcome choices will be scrutinised — pre-registration is non-negotiable.
- **What would falsify the optimistic case:** if, with a clean per-episode label and continuous high-quality physiology, **no** wearable signal detects or precedes crashes, that too is a publishable, field-advancing result — it would redirect effort from physiological signals toward behavioural/EMA-only prediction. The programme is designed to be informative whether the signal exists or not.

---

## 7. Bottom line

The five reviews are one argument. We have a **good portrait of the patient** (robust baseline autonomic dysfunction), a **plausible management strategy** (pacing reduces PEM episodes), and **capable hardware** (wearables that measure HR, sleep and — pending validation — HRV). What we lack, and what every strand independently runs aground on, is a **time-stamped, validated, free-living PEM label.** Supplying that label is the keystone; once it exists, the detection, prediction and wearable-guided-pacing questions all become answerable in sequence. The single most valuable next step in this entire domain is therefore not a new sensor or a new drug, but a **rigorously validated, EMA-anchored, condition-specific method for measuring when a PEM crash actually happens** — the foundation on which the rest of the programme, and arguably the field, can finally be built.
