# Over a Four-Year Post-Cessation Window, How Much of a Rising Resting-HR Baseline Is Deconditioning Versus Long-COVID Disease? A Cited External Anchor on Multi-Year Detraining and the Deconditioning-vs-Disease Debate

*Evidence retrieved from PubMed/PMC and web search (DOIs linked throughout). Purpose: this brief supplies an INDEPENDENT physiological and clinical prior for a multi-year hypothesis. The hypothesis to be anchored: a formerly highly-trained endurance athlete caught COVID in March 2022, developed Long COVID (LC), and essentially STOPPED training from that point; over the ~4 years since, wearable resting heart rate (RHR) and HRV-derived autonomic markers have drifted, and a large part of that slow multi-year drift may be DECONDITIONING (the gradual loss of the athletic base after training cessation) rather than LC disease per se, with the deconditioning possibly plateauing now, ~4 years in. This review grounds (a) the physiology and TIMESCALE of multi-year detraining of resting HR, (b) the residual "athlete's heart" question, and (c) the genuinely contested deconditioning-vs-disease debate in ME/CFS and LC, so the prior is external and the separability question is adjudicated honestly.*

**No-project-data line.** This review reads NO project n=1 data. Every number below is from published population/physiology/clinical literature. This is the external prior against which the project's (separately observed) multi-year RHR trajectory is interpreted, not evidence from this body. Sister to [`training_bradycardia_detraining_rhr_review.md`](training_bradycardia_detraining_rhr_review.md) (which fixes the SHORT-term, ~14-day detraining rebound for the peri-COVID window) and [`wearable_validity_lc_mecfs_review.md`](wearable_validity_lc_mecfs_review.md) (device validity in-population). THIS review asks the LONG-run question: over months-to-years, how far and how fast does a highly-trained RHR revert, does it plateau by ~4 years, and can a slow RHR trajectory be attributed to deconditioning rather than LC disease.

---

## Two-line deliverable (stated up top)

Based on articles retrieved from PubMed and web search:

1. **Multi-year deconditioning RHR shape + timescale.** Detraining reverses the training-induced RHR suppression with a **fast phase over the first ~2-4 weeks** and a **slower tail over weeks-to-months**, reaching a **new near-untrained equilibrium within roughly the first year** (the cardiovascular determinants of RHR, blood/plasma volume, stroke volume, and intrinsic HR, reverse within weeks-to-months, not years). So of a multi-year rise, **most of the pure-deconditioning contribution is front-loaded into year 1 and would be expected to have PLATEAUED well before year 4.** The residual structural remodelling ("athlete's heart") regresses largely but not always completely, so the plateau may sit at, or a touch below, full-untrained rather than above it.

2. **Separability adjudication (deconditioning vs LC disease).** The literature does **NOT** cleanly separate deconditioning from post-viral disease on a resting-HR value alone: both lower fitness and raise resting/orthostatic HR, and they are mechanistically entangled (disease enforces inactivity, which deconditions, which worsens the disease phenotype). What the literature DOES offer are **dissociating features in the trajectory and in provocation**, not in the static number: (a) deconditioning is a **smooth, monotonic, self-limiting decay that asymptotes**, whereas LC/ME-CFS autonomic dysfunction is characteristically **episodic, post-exertional, orthostatic, and does not resolve with the passage of time or track fitness**; (b) the **two-day CPET** and **repeat-exertion** paradigm is the field's main tool for showing a disease-specific drop that deconditioning does not reproduce, and it is contested; (c) POTS/orthostatic intolerance that **fails to normalise with reconditioning** is the disease signature. Net for the n=1 case: a slow, smooth, asymptoting RHR rise over year 1-2 is **compatible with, and largely attributable to, deconditioning**; a rise that is still climbing at year 4, or is episodic/orthostatic/post-exertional, or fails to fall with any return of activity, points at **disease (or aging), not deconditioning**. The honest limit: on the RHR level alone at a single time, they are **not separable**.

## 1. Multi-year detraining trajectory of resting HR: shape and time constant

**Verdict: the cardiovascular substrate of a low trained RHR (blood/plasma volume, stroke volume, intrinsic HR, and vagal tone) reverses on a WEEKS-TO-MONTHS timescale, not years. The rise is biphasic, a fast early phase then a slower tail, and reaches a new near-untrained equilibrium within roughly the first post-cessation year. There is no good evidence for a resting-HR determinant that keeps drifting purely from detraining out to year 4. Evidence is strong for the short-to-medium term and thinner (necessarily inferential) for the "does it keep moving at year 4" question.**

- **The landmark detraining reviews frame the shape.** In the short-term review (<4 weeks of cessation), highly-trained athletes show a **rapid** decline in VO2max and blood volume, with exercise HR rising to partially offset the fallen stroke volume ([Mujika & Padilla, 2000, *Sports Med* Part I; DOI](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200030020-00002)). In the long-term review (>4 weeks), VO2max declines markedly but stays above sedentary control values, driven by reduced blood volume, cardiac dimensions and ventilatory efficiency, lower stroke volume and cardiac output "despite increased heart rates" ([Mujika & Padilla, 2000, *Sports Med* Part II; DOI](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200030030-00001)). Read for our case: the quantities that hold a trained RHR down (blood/plasma volume, stroke volume) fall within **days-to-weeks**, and the residual fitness that lingers is an *exercise-capacity* residual, not a persistently-suppressed *resting* HR. This sets the expectation that the RHR rebound is largely a weeks-to-months event.

- **The direct RHR mechanism-and-timing study (rat, decisive on the intrinsic-HR tail).** Evangelista et al. trained rats then detrained for 1 and 2 weeks, using autonomic blockade to separate mechanisms. Trained and 1-week-detrained rats still had low resting HR tied to a **decreased intrinsic HR**; by **2 weeks** of detraining resting HR had reverted **near the pre-training level**, and this reversal was driven by intrinsic HR rising back, not by autonomic tone ([Evangelista et al., 2005, *Braz J Med Biol Res*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1590/s0100-879x2005000700018)). This is the cleanest statement that the RHR-specific reversal, including its non-autonomic (intrinsic) component, completes over **weeks**, not years, in a controlled model. (Species caveat: rat timescale is compressed relative to human, but the mechanism, intrinsic-HR reversal, is the point.)

- **Corroborating molecular/functional reversibility, all within ~4 weeks.** Endurance-training benefits to cardiomyocyte contractility and calcium handling in rats **returned to control values within 4 weeks of detraining** ([Carneiro-Junior et al., 2013, *J Mol Cell Cardiol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yjmcc.2013.01.013)); training-induced cutaneous microvascular and NO-mediated adaptations were reversed after 8 weeks of detraining ([Wang, 2004, *Eur J Appl Physiol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-004-1176-4)); and even training-induced brain dendritic remodelling in cardiorespiratory nuclei was fully reversed after ~50 days of detraining ([Nelson & Iwamoto, 2006, *J Appl Physiol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1152/japplphysiol.00483.2006)). The consistent message across substrates: training adaptations relevant to autonomic/cardiac control **de-adapt on a weeks-to-a-few-months clock.**

- **Bed-rest / immobilisation as the accelerated-deconditioning analogue.** In 60-day head-down-tilt bed rest (the strongest human model of pure deconditioning), heart rate **increased and parasympathetic tone decreased from ~day 28 onward** in the non-exercising group, and a 10-day recovery did NOT fully compensate the autonomic deconditioning ([Maggioni et al., 2018, *Front Physiol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2018.01553)). Classic bed-rest work established that inactivity produces a rise in HR and orthostatic intolerance with a defined temporal course and recovery ([Carlson, 1967, *Life Sci Space Res*; PMID 11973849](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11973849/)). Read for our case: deconditioning drives RHR **up** and the change is well-developed within **weeks**, reinforcing that the bulk of a deconditioning RHR rise is an early, not a year-4, phenomenon.

- **Time-constant synthesis (honest inference).** No retrieved study directly tracks a human elite athlete's overnight RHR month-by-month across 4 years of cessation, so the "when exactly does it plateau" answer is **inferred**, not measured. The convergent inference: the RHR rise has a **fast component (first ~2-4 weeks)** and a **slower tail (weeks-to-a-few-months)**, with the cardiovascular determinants (plasma volume, stroke volume, intrinsic HR) settling to a new equilibrium **within roughly the first year**. A pure-deconditioning RHR rise should therefore be **largely complete, and plateaued, by year 1-2, and essentially flat by year 4.**

## 2. Persistent "athlete's heart" / residual adaptation: does RHR revert fully or plateau partway?

**Verdict: structural cardiac remodelling REGRESSES substantially after training cessation, most of it within the first months-to-year, but a minority of highly-trained athletes retain a persistent residual cavity dilatation for many years. Wall-thickness (hypertrophy) reverses reliably; chamber (cavity) enlargement can persist in ~1-in-5. For RHR this means the plateau most likely sits AT or SLIGHTLY BELOW full-untrained, not above it, and the residual is not a driver of an ongoing RHR rise. Evidence is moderate-to-strong (a landmark long-term deconditioning cohort plus mechanism studies).**

- **The definitive long-term deconditioning cohort.** Pelliccia et al. followed 40 elite male athletes with marked LV remodelling from peak training through **1 to 13 years (mean 5.6 years) of deconditioning**. Wall thickness regressed 15% and **normalised in every athlete**; LV mass fell 28%; but LV cavity dimension fell only 7% and **persistent substantial cavity dilatation (>=60 mm) remained in 9 of 40 (22%)** years after stopping ([Pelliccia et al., 2002, *Circulation*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1161/hc0802.104534), [PMID 11864923](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11864923/)). Read for our case: the *hypertrophic* component of athlete's heart, the part most tied to a low intrinsic HR, reverses; a *chamber* residual can persist in a minority. A persistent large chamber tends, if anything, to support a larger stroke volume and thus a *lower* (not higher) RHR, so it does not explain an ongoing RHR *rise*, it would bias the plateau slightly downward in the minority who retain it.

- **The intrinsic-HR component reverses (Section 1).** Because a meaningful slice of trained bradycardia is non-autonomic / intrinsic ([Evangelista 2005; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1590/s0100-879x2005000700018)), and that intrinsic component reverts over weeks, the structural floor under a low RHR lifts on the same weeks-to-months clock as the rest of the deconditioning cascade. There is no retrieved evidence for a structural remodelling process that keeps RHR climbing across multiple years post-cessation.

- **Lifelong-trained masters athletes (the opposite bookend).** Masters athletes who *continue* training into old age retain enhanced cardiac baroreflex function versus sedentary peers ([Aengevaeren et al., 2012, *J Appl Physiol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1152/japplphysiol.00402.2012)), showing that the trained autonomic phenotype is **maintained by ongoing training** and, by implication, is not self-sustaining once training stops. This bounds the claim: the residual after *cessation* is structural (chamber) not autonomic; the autonomic advantage needs continued training to persist.

- **Read for the plateau height.** Combining Sections 1-2: after ~4 years of cessation the expected resting-HR plateau sits at, or marginally below, an age-appropriate untrained value, with the "below" coming from any retained chamber dilatation. The residual does not manufacture a continuing upward RHR drift; it sets where the (already-reached) plateau lands.

## 3. The deconditioning-vs-disease debate in ME/CFS: is the autonomic/exercise phenotype disease-intrinsic or deconditioning?

**Verdict: GENUINELY CONTESTED, and both sides have real evidence. The strongest disease-side argument is the two-day / repeat-CPET finding of a reproducible drop in oxygen consumption and workload on the second exertion that deconditioning does not reproduce; the strongest deconditioning-side argument is that single-test abnormalities, orthostatic HR, and low HRV are also fully consistent with low fitness, and that the two are hard to disentangle because disease enforces inactivity. The field's centre of gravity has moved AWAY from "just deconditioning" as a sufficient explanation, but the deconditioning contribution is not zero and the debate is unresolved. Represent both sides.**

- **Disease-side: the two-day CPET / Workwell paradigm.** The core observation is that ME/CFS patients, unlike healthy sedentary controls, cannot **reproduce** their exercise performance on a second CPET 24 h later. VanNess, Snell & Stevens reported diminished cardiopulmonary capacity during post-exertional malaise on a second-day test ([VanNess, Snell & Stevens, 2007, *J Chronic Fatigue Syndr*, 14:77-85](https://scholarlycommons.pacific.edu/cop-facarticles/439/)). The larger discriminative-validity study found **no significant group differences on test 1** (which "would appear to support a deconditioning hypothesis") but on **test 2** the CFS group achieved significantly lower VO2 and workload at peak and at the ventilatory/anaerobic threshold, classifying CFS vs sedentary controls with 95.1% accuracy ([Snell, Stevens, Davenport & Van Ness, 2013, *Phys Ther*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.2522/ptj.20110368)). A monozygotic-twin case discordant for ME/CFS showed the ill twin alone had an abnormal 13% fall in ventilatory-threshold VO2 on day 2 ([Giloteaux, Hanson & Keller, 2016, *Am J Case Rep*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.12659/ajcr.900314)). The logical force: **deconditioning lowers day-1 capacity but is reproducible day-2; the day-2 DROP is the candidate disease-specific, non-deconditioning signature.**

- **Deconditioning-side / critique.** The same 2013 study is explicit that day-1 results "would appear to support a deconditioning hypothesis," and the two-day effect, while replicated by the Workwell group, rests on small, mostly female, self-selected samples able to complete two maximal tests, limiting generalisation and inviting selection concerns ([Snell 2013; DOI](https://doi.org/10.2522/ptj.20110368)). Crucially, a **Long-COVID** two-day CPET study found **no day-1-to-day-2 difference** despite 80% of patients reporting PEM, with day-1 responses consistent with aerobic deconditioning in 40% ([Gattoni et al., 2024, *Respir Physiol Neurobiol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.resp.2024.104362)), i.e. the repeat-CPET signature does not always appear even in a PEM-positive post-viral population, which the authors read as PEM not being explained by impaired between-day recovery of exercise capacity. So the disease-specific marker is not universally reproducible.

- **The GET/PACE controversy (kept high-level, both sides).** If ME/CFS exercise intolerance were mainly deconditioning, graded exercise therapy (GET) should reliably help. The PACE trial reported benefit from GET and CBT, but its reanalysis controversy ("PACE-gate") argued the effects shrank or vanished on objective outcomes after outcome thresholds were changed post hoc ([Geraghty, 2016, *J Health Psychol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1177/1359105316675213); [Stouten, 2017, *J Health Psychol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1177/1359105317707531)). The counter-view holds the critiques reflect differing causal beliefs and that rehabilitative treatment remains defensible and does not imply the illness is "not real" ([Petrie & Weinman, 2017, *J Health Psychol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1177/1359105317703789); [Sharpe, Chalder & White, 2021, *J Gen Intern Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11606-021-07188-4)). NICE's 2021 guideline withdrew GET as a treatment and downgraded CBT, itself contested by a large multidisciplinary group as deviating from usual evidence standards ([White et al., 2023, *J Neurol Neurosurg Psychiatry*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1136/jnnp-2022-330463)). Read for our case: **the field has not settled whether the phenotype is reversible by reconditioning**, which is exactly the deconditioning-vs-disease question, and the safest external prior is that deconditioning is a *contributor* that does not fully account for the disease phenotype.

- **Separability conclusion for ME/CFS.** The consensus that can be defended: a *single* low-fitness measurement (low VO2max, high submaximal HR, low HRV) is **not separable** from deconditioning; the *repeat-exertion drop* and *non-recovery* are the field's attempt to find a disease-specific feature deconditioning lacks, and that attempt is real but contested and not universally reproducible.

## 4. Long COVID specifically: autonomic dysfunction, and how much is attributed to deconditioning vs disease

**Verdict: LC autonomic dysfunction (POTS, inappropriate/orthostatic tachycardia, low HRV, orthostatic intolerance) is REAL and often persistent, and the field explicitly argues it is NOT fully explained by deconditioning, while acknowledging deconditioning is a genuine and common contributor to reduced exercise capacity. The evidence base is larger than for the RHR-trajectory question but is heterogeneous and biased toward symptomatic cohorts.**

- **LC-POTS is a recognised entity.** The American Autonomic Society issued a statement recognising Long-COVID postural tachycardia syndrome and calling for dedicated research and resources ([Raj et al., 2021, *Clin Auton Res*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10286-021-00798-2)); dysautonomia including POTS is now a described sequela of both acute and long COVID in autonomic-neurology reviews ([Hovaguimian, 2022, *Neurol Clin*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ncl.2022.08.002)).

- **Persistent, measurable HRV alteration.** In 103 post-COVID-syndrome patients studied ~252 days after infection with 24-h Holter, frequency-domain HRV was disturbed (altered LF/HF, impaired night-time parasympathetic activity, higher sympathetic drive) versus healthy controls, and the pattern resembled that of coronary-artery-disease patients, suggesting a **sustained impairment of sympathovagal balance** that persisted at long-term follow-up ([Mooren et al., 2023, *Sci Rep*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-023-42615-y)). This is direct evidence that LC autonomic dysfunction **persists** and does not simply track a recovering fitness level.

- **The exercise-physiology adjudication in LC.** The CPET meta-analysis and reviews are the key balanced sources. A systematic review/meta-analysis of 38 CPET studies (2160 individuals) found peak VO2 ~4.9 mL/kg/min lower in symptomatic LC, with **deconditioning and peripheral (oxygen-extraction) limitation common, BUT also chronotropic incompetence and dysfunctional breathing**, and it explicitly listed altered autonomic function, endothelial dysfunction and muscular/mitochondrial pathology as mechanisms beyond deconditioning (low certainty, high heterogeneity) ([Durstenfeld et al., 2022, *JAMA Netw Open*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2022.36057)). A narrative CPET review of 32 studies concluded that low post-COVID VO2 is multifactorial and that its "findings do NOT support deconditioning as the primary mechanism" ([Schwendinger et al., 2022, *Sports Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-022-01751-7)). Read for our case: the LC field's own verdict is that deconditioning is present and common but **not the primary/sole** driver, and that chronotropic and autonomic abnormalities dissociate from pure fitness loss.

- **Dissociation from fitness (the key point for separability).** Chronotropic incompetence (blunted HR rise to exertion) and orthostatic tachycardia are **not** what pure deconditioning produces, deconditioning produces a HIGH exercise HR from low stroke volume, not a blunted one, so their presence in LC is evidence of an autonomic lesion distinct from deconditioning ([Durstenfeld 2022; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2022.36057); [Schwendinger 2022; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-022-01751-7)). That said, the two coexist: LC patients are frequently both deconditioned and autonomically affected, and the literature cannot cleanly partition the two within an individual.

## 5. Can a resting-HR TRAJECTORY distinguish deconditioning from disease? (deliverable 2, the key methodological output)

**The question:** what, if anything, in a multi-year RHR series lets you attribute the slow component to deconditioning vs LC disease, and where does the literature say they are NOT separable?

**Adjudication:**

1. **On the STATIC RHR value at one time: not separable.** Both deconditioning (Sections 1-2) and LC/ME-CFS autonomic dysfunction (Sections 3-4) push resting/orthostatic HR **up**. A single elevated RHR, or a single low HRV, is fully consistent with either, and the two are mechanistically entangled because disease enforces the inactivity that deconditions ([Mujika & Padilla 2000 I/II; DOI](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200030020-00002)/[DOI](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200030030-00001); [Durstenfeld 2022; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2022.36057)). This is the honest core: **the level does not adjudicate.**

2. **On the SHAPE of the trajectory: partially separable.** The literature supports several dissociating features:
   - **Self-limiting asymptote vs persistence.** Deconditioning is a **smooth, monotonic decay that reaches a new equilibrium within ~1 year** and then plateaus (Sections 1-2). A slow RHR rise that flattens by year 1-2 fits deconditioning; a rise **still climbing at year 4**, or one that never plateaus, is **not** what pure deconditioning predicts and points to disease progression or aging.
   - **Episodic / post-exertional / orthostatic structure.** LC and ME-CFS autonomic dysfunction is characteristically **episodic, orthostatic, and post-exertional** ([Raj 2021; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10286-021-00798-2); [Mooren 2023; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-023-42615-y)), whereas deconditioning is a **smooth baseline shift** without crash/relapse structure. Spikes, orthostatic surges, and PEM-locked step-changes in an RHR/HRV series are disease-flavoured, not deconditioning-flavoured. (This aligns with the project-side interest in spike-detecting rather than daily-average metrics, though that is a methods note, not evidence from this review.)
   - **Response to intervention (provocation).** The cleanest experimental separator in the literature: **reconditioning.** In POTS, which is partly a cardiac-atrophy/hypovolaemia deconditioning phenotype, structured exercise training raises stroke volume, lowers HR at any given VO2, and improves capacity ([Shibata et al., 2012, *J Physiol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1113/jphysiol.2012.233858); [Fu & Levine, 2018, *Auton Neurosci*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.autneu.2018.07.001)). If an elevated RHR **falls with reconditioning**, its deconditioning share is demonstrated; if it **fails to normalise despite reconditioning** (or reconditioning triggers PEM and cannot be sustained), the residual is disease. The two-day-CPET drop (Section 3) is the exertional analogue of the same idea.
   - **The repeat-exertion drop.** A reproducible day-2 fall in capacity/threshold is the disease-specific feature deconditioning lacks ([Snell 2013; DOI](https://doi.org/10.2522/ptj.20110368)), but it is contested and does not always appear in post-viral cohorts ([Gattoni 2024; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.resp.2024.104362)).

3. **Where the literature says NOT separable.** Beyond the static-value point: (a) because disease and deconditioning **co-occur and reinforce**, no study cleanly partitions their contributions within one patient's HR; (b) the repeat-CPET marker is **not universally reproducible** ([Gattoni 2024; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.resp.2024.104362)); (c) whether the phenotype is reconditioning-reversible is exactly what the unresolved GET/PACE debate is about ([Geraghty 2016; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1177/1359105316675213); [White 2023; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1136/jnnp-2022-330463)). So the **slow, smooth component** of an RHR series can be *attributed largely to deconditioning by shape*, but the *disease component cannot be excluded* and the two cannot be cleanly quantified apart.

**One-line deliverable:** A slow, smooth, monotonic RHR rise that **asymptotes within the first post-cessation year** is, by shape, largely attributable to deconditioning; disease reveals itself not in the resting level but in **persistence past the deconditioning plateau, episodic/orthostatic/post-exertional structure, and failure to fall with reconditioning**, and even then deconditioning and disease **cannot be cleanly separated on HR data alone** because they co-occur and reinforce.

## 6. Net read for the n=1 case (4-year post-cessation window)

**How much rising-RHR baseline does PURE deconditioning explain over 4 years, would it have plateaued by year 4, and how separable is it from LC disease and from aging?**

1. **Magnitude.** Losing a heavy endurance base restores resting HR from a suppressed athletic floor (commonly 5-12 bpm below untrained, more in elite; see sister review [`training_bradycardia_detraining_rhr_review.md`](training_bradycardia_detraining_rhr_review.md)) back toward an age-appropriate untrained value. So pure deconditioning can plausibly account for a **substantial slice** of a multi-year RHR rise, on the order of the training-bradycardia depth (a single-digit-to-low-double-digit bpm rise), no more, because the ceiling is "return to untrained," not "above untrained."

2. **Timing / plateau.** That deconditioning rise is **front-loaded**: fast in the first ~2-4 weeks, most of it done within months, and reaching a new equilibrium within **roughly the first year** (Sections 1-2). By **year 4 it should have plateaued** and be essentially flat. Therefore, if the project's RHR series is **still rising at year 4**, that late component is **NOT** well-explained by deconditioning and must be assigned to disease, aging, or other factors.

3. **Separability from LC disease.** On the **resting level**, not separable (Section 5.1). On the **trajectory shape**, partially: the smooth, asymptoting early rise is deconditioning-flavoured; persistence, episodic/orthostatic spikes, PEM-locked steps, or non-response to any reconditioning are disease-flavoured (Section 5.2). The two **co-occur and cannot be cleanly quantified apart** in one person.

4. **Separability from normal aging.** RHR drifts modestly with age independently of either process; over a 4-year window the aging contribution to RHR is **small but non-zero** and adds a slow upward baseline that is **confounded with the deconditioning plateau and any disease component**. The literature retrieved here does not quantify the aging slice for this individual, flagged as a limit.

5. **Bottom line.** Over 4 years, pure deconditioning can explain a meaningful, single-digit-to-low-double-digit bpm upward shift in RHR, but that shift is **almost certainly complete and plateaued before year 4**; any RHR movement that is still ongoing, episodic/orthostatic, post-exertional, or fails to reverse with activity is the **disease (LC) signal**, and on the resting value alone the deconditioning and disease contributions are **not separable**, only the trajectory shape and response to reconditioning give partial leverage.

## 7. Honest limits

- **PubMed/PMC + web-search, English-language, purposive (not systematic) search**, run 2026-06-30/07-02. This is a landmark-anchored brief (Mujika & Padilla detraining reviews; Pelliccia long-term deconditioning cohort; Snell/VanNess two-day CPET; Durstenfeld/Schwendinger LC-CPET; Fu/Levine POTS-reconditioning), not an exhaustive review.
- **No project n=1 data was read.** This is the external prior; the RHR trajectory it interprets was observed outside this document.
- **The multi-year human RHR time-course is INFERRED, not directly measured.** No retrieved study tracks an elite athlete's overnight RHR month-by-month across 4 years of cessation. The "plateaus by year 1-2, flat by year 4" claim is built from (a) weeks-to-months reversal of RHR determinants, (b) bed-rest analogues, and (c) the intrinsic-HR reversal timescale, not from a direct multi-year athlete RHR series. Treat the plateau timing as a well-grounded expectation, not a calibrated constant.
- **Species and model caveats.** The cleanest intrinsic-HR and reversibility timing come from rat detraining ([Evangelista 2005; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1590/s0100-879x2005000700018); [Carneiro-Junior 2013; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yjmcc.2013.01.013)); human timescales are longer, and bed-rest ([Maggioni 2018; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2018.01553)) adds a fluid-shift component absent in ordinary sedentary living.
- **The deconditioning-vs-disease debate is genuinely unresolved and contested.** The two-day-CPET marker is replicated by one group but small-sample, selection-prone, and not universally reproducible in post-viral cohorts ([Gattoni 2024; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.resp.2024.104362)); the GET/PACE controversy is live ([Geraghty 2016; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1177/1359105316675213); [White 2023; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1136/jnnp-2022-330463)). This review deliberately presents both sides and does not adopt the side that flatters the deconditioning hypothesis.
- **LC autonomic studies are biased toward symptomatic cohorts** and are heterogeneous in definition and method ([Durstenfeld 2022; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2022.36057)); persistence findings ([Mooren 2023; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-023-42615-y)) are cross-sectional at a single long-term timepoint, not individual longitudinal trajectories.
- **Aging is not quantified** for this individual; it is carried as a small, confounded upward contributor.

- Evangelista FS, Martuchi SED, Negrao CE, Brum PC (2005). "Loss of resting bradycardia with detraining is associated with intrinsic heart rate changes." Braz J Med Biol Res 38(7):1141-6. PMID 16007286, DOI 10.1590/s0100-879x2005000700018. Resting HR reverts near pre-training by 2 weeks of detraining via rising INTRINSIC HR, not autonomic tone. The RHR-specific reversal is weeks, and its non-autonomic part reverses too.
- Carneiro-Junior MA, et al. (2013). "The benefits of endurance training in cardiomyocyte function... are reversed within four weeks of detraining." J Mol Cell Cardiol 57:119-28. PMID 23376037, DOI 10.1016/j.yjmcc.2013.01.013. Cardiomyocyte contractility/Ca-handling adaptations return to control within 4 weeks. Corroborates weeks-scale cardiac de-adaptation.
- Pelliccia A, Maron BJ, De Luca R, et al. (2002). "Remodeling of Left Ventricular Hypertrophy in Elite Athletes After Long-Term Deconditioning." Circulation 105(8):944-9. PMID 11864923, DOI 10.1161/hc0802.104534. After 1-13 yr detraining, wall thickness normalises in all; LV cavity dilatation persists in 22%. Hypertrophy reverses, chamber residual can persist; residual biases plateau slightly LOW, not high.
- Aengevaeren VL, Claassen JAHR, Levine BD, Zhang R (2012). "Cardiac baroreflex function and dynamic cerebral autoregulation in elderly Masters athletes." J Appl Physiol 114(2):195-202. PMID 23139365, DOI 10.1152/japplphysiol.00402.2012. Lifelong-trained masters retain enhanced baroreflex; the trained autonomic phenotype is maintained by ONGOING training, so it is not self-sustaining after cessation.
- Maggioni MA, et al. (2018). "High-Intensity Exercise Mitigates Cardiovascular Deconditioning During Long-Duration Bed Rest." Front Physiol 9:1553. PMID 30510516, DOI 10.3389/fphys.2018.01553. 60-day head-down bed rest: HR up and parasympathetic tone down from ~day 28 in non-exercisers; 10-day recovery insufficient. Human pure-deconditioning analogue; deconditioning raises RHR within weeks.
- Carlson LD (1967). "Cardiovascular studies during and following simulation and weightlessness." Life Sci Space Res 5:51-4. PMID 11973849. Classic bed-rest deconditioning: HR rise, orthostatic intolerance, defined temporal course and recovery.
- VanNess JM, Snell CR, Stevens SR (2007). "Diminished Cardiopulmonary Capacity during Post-Exertional Malaise." J Chronic Fatigue Syndr 14:77-85. https://scholarlycommons.pacific.edu/cop-facarticles/439/ . Origin of the two-day-CPET disease-specific drop paradigm in ME/CFS.
- Snell CR, Stevens SR, Davenport TE, Van Ness JM (2013). "Discriminative validity of metabolic and workload measurements for identifying people with chronic fatigue syndrome." Phys Ther 93(11):1484-92. PMID 23813081, DOI 10.2522/ptj.20110368. Day-1 no group difference (supports deconditioning); day-2 CFS drop in VO2/workload classifies 95%. The central disease-vs-deconditioning CPET evidence, both sides in one study.
- Giloteaux L, Hanson MR, Keller BA (2016). "A Pair of Identical Twins Discordant for ME/CFS..." Am J Case Rep 17:720-9. PMID 27721367, DOI 10.12659/ajcr.900314. Ill twin alone shows abnormal 13% day-2 ventilatory-threshold VO2 fall; within-genotype support for a disease-specific repeat-exertion drop.
- Gattoni C, et al. (2024). "Two-day cardiopulmonary exercise testing in long COVID post-exertional malaise diagnosis." Respir Physiol Neurobiol 331:104362. PMID 39490617, DOI 10.1016/j.resp.2024.104362. In LC, NO day-1-to-day-2 difference despite 80% PEM; deconditioning pattern in 40%. The repeat-CPET marker is NOT universal in post-viral disease; counterweight to the ME/CFS studies.
- Durstenfeld MS, et al. (2022). "Use of Cardiopulmonary Exercise Testing to Evaluate Long COVID-19 Symptoms in Adults: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis." JAMA Netw Open 5(10):e2236057. PMID 36223120, DOI 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2022.36057. Peak VO2 ~4.9 mL/kg/min lower in LC; deconditioning common BUT chronotropic incompetence, dysfunctional breathing, autonomic/endothelial/mitochondrial mechanisms also present. LC is not purely deconditioning.
- Schwendinger F, Knaier R, Radtke T, Schmidt-Trucksass A (2022). "Low Cardiorespiratory Fitness Post-COVID-19: A Narrative Review." Sports Med 53(1):51-74. PMID 36115933, DOI 10.1007/s40279-022-01751-7. 32 CPET studies: findings "do not support deconditioning as the primary mechanism" of low post-COVID VO2.
- Mooren FC, et al. (2023). "Autonomic dysregulation in long-term patients suffering from Post-COVID-19 Syndrome assessed by heart rate variability." Sci Rep 13:15814. PMID 37739977, DOI 10.1038/s41598-023-42615-y. ~252 days post-infection: persistent HRV alteration, impaired night parasympathetic activity, sympathetic overactivation resembling CAD patients. LC autonomic dysfunction persists long-term.
- Raj SR, et al. (2021). "Long-COVID postural tachycardia syndrome: an American Autonomic Society statement." Clin Auton Res 31(3):365-8. PMID 33740207, DOI 10.1007/s10286-021-00798-2. Recognises LC-POTS as an entity needing dedicated research.
- Hovaguimian A (2022). "Dysautonomia: Diagnosis and Management." Neurol Clin 41(1):193-213. PMID 36400555, DOI 10.1016/j.ncl.2022.08.002. Reviews dysautonomia incl. acute/long-COVID; clinical framing of the LC autonomic phenotype.
- Shibata S, Fu Q, Bivens TB, Hastings JL, Wang W, Levine BD (2012). "Short-term exercise training improves the cardiovascular response to exercise in POTS." J Physiol 590(15):3495-505. PMID 22641777, DOI 10.1113/jphysiol.2012.233858. Reconditioning raises stroke volume and lowers HR-at-given-VO2 in POTS; the intervention-response separator between a reconditionable (deconditioning) and non-reconditionable (disease) HR elevation.
- Fu Q, Levine BD (2018). "Exercise and non-pharmacological treatment of POTS." Auton Neurosci 215:20-7. PMID 30001836, DOI 10.1016/j.autneu.2018.07.001. Cardiac atrophy/hypovolaemia (deconditioning) contributes to POTS; reconditioning is treatment. Frames deconditioning as a real, testable contributor to an autonomic phenotype.
- Geraghty KJ (2016). "'PACE-Gate': When clinical trial evidence meets open data access." J Health Psychol 22(9):1106-12. PMID 27807258, DOI 10.1177/1359105316675213. The GET/CBT reanalysis controversy; deconditioning-reversibility of ME/CFS is contested.
- Stouten B (2017). "PACE-GATE: An alternative view on a study with a poor trial protocol." J Health Psychol 22(9):1192-7. PMID 28805525, DOI 10.1177/1359105317707531. Effect sizes shrink on objective outcomes; critique side.
- Petrie KJ, Weinman J (2017). "The PACE trial: It's time to broaden perceptions and move on." J Health Psychol 22(9):1198-1200. PMID 28805523, DOI 10.1177/1359105317703789. Defence side; GET/CBT remain defensible.
- Sharpe M, Chalder T, White PD (2021). "Evidence-Based Care for People with Chronic Fatigue Syndrome and Myalgic Encephalomyelitis." J Gen Intern Med 37(2):449-52. PMID 34791590, DOI 10.1007/s11606-021-07188-4. Argues rehabilitative treatment (incl. GET) is effective and does not imply the illness is "not real"; the pro-reconditioning position.
- White P, et al. (2023). "Anomalies in the review process and interpretation of the evidence in the NICE guideline for CFS and ME." J Neurol Neurosurg Psychiatry 94(12):1056-63. PMID 37434321, DOI 10.1136/jnnp-2022-330463. Large multidisciplinary critique of NICE's withdrawal of GET; documents how live and contested the reconditioning question is.
