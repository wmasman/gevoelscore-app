# Does Heavy Endurance Training, and Its Cessation During Illness, Confound a Wearable RHR Rise on Infection? A Cited External Anchor for the Peri-Event COVID RHR Null

*Evidence retrieved from PubMed/PMC (DOIs linked throughout). Purpose: this brief supplies an INDEPENDENT physiological prior for a post-hoc observation in the project's peri-event COVID check. The observation to be anchored: during the participant's ~14-day acute COVID infection (2022-03), overnight/resting heart rate (RHR), which the acute-infection literature predicts should RISE, did NOT rise on the raw daily scale relative to a personal baseline built from a heavy pre-illness endurance-training block; it rose only after removing a downward pre-illness RHR trend. The candidate explanation: heavy pre-illness training suppressed baseline RHR (training-induced bradycardia), masking the infection-driven rise on the raw scale, and/or training cessation during illness introduced its own RHR dynamics. This review grounds that explanation in published sport-science and physiology literature so the prior is genuinely external, and adjudicates the decisive quantity: how much RHR rebound does detraining alone produce inside a 14-day window.*

**No-project-data line.** This review reads NO project n=1 data. Every number below is from published population/physiology literature. This is the external prior against which the project's (already-observed) raw-vs-detrended RHR pattern is interpreted, not evidence from this body. Sister to the acute-infection anchor [`acute_viral_infection_autonomic_signature_review.md`](acute_viral_infection_autonomic_signature_review.md), which fixes the predicted infection direction (RHR up, HRV down); THIS review asks whether the training story can plausibly blunt or mimic that RHR signal on the raw daily scale.

---

## Predicted direction / mechanism summary (deliverable 1, stated up top)

Based on articles retrieved from PubMed, the training story predicts a **low, and still-falling, baseline RHR at illness onset**, for two compounding reasons, and it predicts that this baseline is **slow to rebound** once training stops:

| Mechanism | Direction on RHR | Timescale | Strength of evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Endurance training-induced resting bradycardia | Baseline RHR **low** (roughly 5-12 bpm below untrained, more in elite) | Builds over **weeks to months** of training; a heavy block leaves a depressed, sometimes still-declining, baseline | Strong (multi-study, human + mechanistic) |
| Downward pre-illness RHR trend from an ongoing/peaking training block | RHR **still drifting down** into the illness window | Days-to-weeks trend | Moderate (taper/adaptation studies show continuing RHR decline) |
| Detraining rebound of RHR after cessation | RHR **rises back** toward untrained | The decisive quantity: **partial and slow within 14 days**; RHR is among the slower-reverting cardiac markers | Moderate (landmark detraining reviews + a direct cessation time-course study) |
| Acute inactivity / plasma-volume loss (bed-rest analogue for an ill, resting participant) | Tends to **raise** HR (hypovolaemia) but **lowers** parasympathetic drive | Plasma volume falls within days | Moderate (bed-rest/head-down-tilt studies) |

**Composite prediction (the anchor):** at infection onset the participant's baseline RHR sits **low and possibly still declining** because of the heavy training block. An acute infection then pushes RHR **up** from that low, moving floor. On the **raw** daily scale the infection rise is measured against a personal baseline that is itself depressed and drifting down, so the net raw movement can be small, flat, or even masked, exactly the observed pattern. Detrending (removing the downward pre-illness slope) removes the training-adaptation drift and lets the infection rise surface. So the literature predicts that **the raw null is the more training-explained artefact, and the detrended rise is the less-confounded estimate of the infection effect**, with the important caveat that detraining rebound is one further (smaller, slower) upward force layered on top.

## 1. Endurance training-induced resting bradycardia: how much, how fast, why

**Verdict: robust and large. Endurance training lowers resting HR by an order of ~5-12 bpm (more in elite endurance athletes, who commonly rest in the 40s-50s), built over weeks-to-months, via a mix of enhanced vagal tone AND a genuine reduction in intrinsic heart rate (electrical/morphological remodelling), not vagal tone alone.**

- **Magnitude and time course.** In a controlled 8-month endurance-training study, baseline HR fell from 66 to 57 bpm (a 9 bpm drop) alongside a 27% rise in VO2max and a 16% rise in blood volume ([Shi et al., 1995, *Med Sci Sports Exerc*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1249/00005768-199510000-00010) via [PMID 8531612](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8531612/)). A standardized 12-week running program lowered resting HR significantly in both younger and older recreational runners ([Carter et al., 2003, *Med Sci Sports Exerc*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1249/01.MSS.0000079046.01763.8F)). The point for our case: a resting bradycardia of this size accrues over the months of a heavy training block and defines a **depressed baseline**, which is exactly the personal-baseline the raw peri-event contrast is measured against.

- **Mechanism is mixed, not purely vagal (this matters for the detrending logic).** Two mechanisms coexist:
  1. *Enhanced vagal tone.* In the Shi 1995 blockade study, the training bradycardia was abolished by atropine but not metoprolol, i.e. it was vagally mediated, with no change in intrinsic HR in that cohort ([Shi 1995; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1249/00005768-199510000-00010)). Swimming-trained rats likewise showed parasympathetically-mediated resting bradycardia ([Medeiros et al., 2004, *Braz J Med Biol Res*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1590/s0100-879x2004001200018)).
  2. *Reduced intrinsic heart rate (non-autonomic).* In highly trained cyclists, intrinsic HR (after combined vagal + beta blockade) was ~13 bpm lower than untrained, a **non-autonomic** component attributed to cardiac enlargement/remodelling ([Lewis et al., 1980, *Acta Physiol Scand*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1748-1716.1980.tb06600.x)). A cross-sectional study of 172 runners found that apparent vagal (HRV) associations with training **disappeared** once heart rate and left-ventricular size were controlled, evidence that the low HR of trained hearts reflects morphological/electrical remodelling as much as autonomic tone ([Herzig et al., 2018, *Front Physiol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2018.00756)); a companion cross-sectional study attributed training bradycardia substantially to reduced intrinsic HR rather than enhanced vagal efferent activity ([Scott et al., 2004, *Auton Neurosci*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.autneu.2004.04.006)).
  - **Why the mixed mechanism matters here:** the *intrinsic-HR / remodelling* component does NOT reverse quickly on cessation (structural), whereas the *vagal* component can shift faster. A baseline built partly on remodelling is a slow-moving floor, reinforcing the "low, slow-to-rebound baseline" prediction.

- **Supporting hemodynamics.** Training raises blood/plasma volume and stroke volume (Shi: +16% blood volume), so a lower HR maintains cardiac output. This is the plasma-volume lever that detraining/inactivity later pulls in the opposite direction (Section 2, 4).

## 2. Detraining dynamics: the decisive quantity for a 14-day window

**Verdict: within ~14 days of cessation, detraining reverses SOME adaptations FAST (VO2max, blood/plasma volume, metabolic markers) but resting-HR / vagal-HRV rebound is PARTIAL and comparatively SLOW; full reversal of the cardiac-autonomic adaptation typically takes weeks, on the order of ~4-8 weeks, not 2. So detraining alone would not be expected to drive a large RHR rebound inside a 14-day illness window.**

The landmark reviews and a direct cessation time-course study:

- **Mujika & Padilla short-term detraining review (< 4 weeks of cessation).** In highly trained athletes, cessation causes a **rapid** decline in VO2max and blood volume, with plasma/blood-volume loss detectable within days; *exercise* HR rises to partially compensate for the fallen stroke volume ([Mujika & Padilla, 2000, *Sports Med* Part I; DOI](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200030020-00002); [Mujika & Padilla, 2001, *Med Sci Sports Exerc*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1097/00005768-200103000-00013)). Key nuance for our case: the fast-reverting quantities are **blood volume and maximal/exercise cardiovascular capacity**, and the rise they document is in *exercise* HR (compensating a smaller stroke volume), not primarily a large *resting* HR rebound over two weeks. Metabolic reversals (insulin sensitivity, GLUT-4, lipoprotein lipase) can appear within ~10 days; **resting** cardiac-autonomic markers are described as reverting more gradually.

- **Direct RHR/HRV cessation time-course (the most decisive single study).** Gamelin et al. followed HRV through 8 weeks of training then measured at 2, 4, and 8 weeks of training cessation. Vagal/total-power HRV indices that had risen with training decreased **moderately after 2 weeks (D2)** of cessation and then **stabilized**, and had returned toward baseline only by **8 weeks (D8)** ([Gamelin et al., 2007, *Int J Sports Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1055/s-2007-964861)). Read for our window: at the **2-week** mark, the autonomic reversal is present but **partial**, and it continues over the following weeks, i.e. two weeks captures only the early, incomplete part of the rebound. This is the cleanest external statement that 14 days is too short for full autonomic (and by extension resting-HR) de-adaptation.

- **Corroborating detraining-HRV studies.** An 8-week training / 4-week detraining study in Navy sailors showed the detrained group's resting HRV fell below the trained group only after the **4-week** detraining period ([Hansen et al., 2004, *Eur J Appl Physiol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-004-1208-0)). In professional footballers, one month of reduced (holiday) activity did NOT significantly change resting autonomic HRV control, partly because recreational activity persisted ([Rebelo et al., 1997, *Rev Port Cardiol*; PMID 9303607](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9303607/)). Both point the same way: meaningful resting-autonomic de-adaptation is a **multi-week** phenomenon, not a 2-week one.

**Net timescale read:** the resting-HR/vagal rebound from detraining is **slow relative to a 14-day window**: partial at 2 weeks, still incompleting toward baseline at 4-8 weeks. The fast-reverting detraining changes (plasma volume, VO2max, exercise-HR compensation) are real but act mostly on *exercise/maximal* cardiovascular performance, not on a large *resting* HR jump within two weeks. Caveat: this is population literature and the studies vary in training background and cessation completeness.

## 3. Taper and supercompensation: could a training-load transient perturb RHR on the 2-week scale?

**Verdict: a taper / load reduction tends to lower resting HR further and reactivate parasympathetic markers over days-to-weeks, i.e. it pushes RHR DOWN, not up. So a taper-like transient in the pre-illness period would DEEPEN the low, still-declining baseline, reinforcing (not opposing) the masking story. Evidence is directionally consistent but modest and metric-dependent.**

- **Taper lowers resting HR and can raise parasympathetic indices.** In professional soccer players across warm-up / intensive / tapering phases, supine resting HR fell by ~5 bpm across training and standing-position HRV total power rose further during tapering ([Ravé & Fortrat, 2016, *Eur J Appl Physiol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-016-3416-9)). A 12-week program with taper periods produced significant increases in resting total and high-frequency HRV power ([Carter et al., 2003; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1249/01.MSS.0000079046.01763.8F)). In an elite ultra-endurance swimmer case study, parasympathetic indices were **higher during heavy-workload periods** and the LF/HF ratio rose during taper, showing autonomic markers track load on a days-to-weeks scale ([Pla et al., 2019, *Int J Sports Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1055/a-0877-6981)).

- **Overreaching/overload transients also move autonomic markers on a 2-3 week scale.** A 2-week overload then 1-week taper shifted cardiac-autonomic and cognitive markers and then returned them toward baseline ([Dupuy et al., 2014, *Scand J Med Sci Sports*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1600-0838.2012.01465.x)). During a marathon overload-taper, vagal HRV (RMSSD) fell after 2 weeks of taper while performance improved, showing these markers can move **non-monotonically** on the timescale of a fortnight ([Hug et al., 2014, *Int J Sports Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1055/s-0033-1361184)). A 2-week intensive block measurably lowered nocturnal RMSSD ([Kaikkonen et al., 2020, *Int J Sports Physiol Perform*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1123/ijspp.2019-0531)).

- **Read for our case (with the honest hedge).** The dominant, better-evidenced taper effect is **RHR down / parasympathetic up**, which reinforces the "baseline is low and still falling into the illness window" story. But the overload/overreaching studies show autonomic markers can move in **either** direction on a 2-week scale depending on where in the load cycle the athlete sits. So a taper-or-peak transient is a plausible *additional* downward pull on the pre-illness RHR trend; it is not a clean, single-signed effect, and the literature here is mixed enough that it should be carried as a supporting, not decisive, mechanism.

## 4. The wearable-illness-detection confound: is a low, trained baseline documented to blunt the RHR-on-illness signal?

**Verdict: YES in mechanism, and directly supported. Wearable infection detectors are explicitly built on RHR ELEVATION RELATIVE TO A PERSONAL BASELINE; when that personal baseline is (a) depressed by training and (b) drifting because training load is changing, the elevation is measured against a moving, low floor, which blunts or hides the raw signal. Training cessation adds its own (slower) RHR dynamics that entangle with the illness window. The evidence is strong on the personal-baseline dependence and on athlete-specific detection; it is thinner on a head-to-head "trained vs untrained blunting" experiment, which I flag as a gap.**

- **The detectors are personal-baseline-relative by construction.** The COVID smartwatch detector of Mishra/Snyder fired on "extreme elevations in resting heart rate **relative to the individual baseline**" ([Mishra et al., 2020, *Nat Biomed Eng*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41551-020-00640-6)); Natarajan's cohort likewise reads elevation of HR against each person's own norm ([Natarajan et al., 2020, *npj Digit Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-020-00363-7)). If the personal baseline is itself sliding downward during a training block, a *fixed* infection increment produces a *smaller net* deviation on the raw scale, precisely the detrending problem. These two are cited in the sister acute-infection review; here they carry the specific corollary that a **non-stationary personal baseline weakens the raw contrast**.

- **Athletes do show the RHR-up/HRV-down illness signature, but detection sits close to normal training-induced fluctuation.** In NCAA Division I female athletes wearing WHOOP bands, COVID infection produced increased RHR, increased respiratory rate and decreased HRV, but the RHR/HRV changes were only clearly separable from baseline in the **last day or two** before/at a positive test, while respiratory rate moved earliest (~3 days prior) ([Rentería et al., 2023, *Sports Health*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1177/19417381231183709)). Read for our case: in trained individuals the RHR channel is a **later, narrower** discriminator, consistent with a low/variable baseline making the RHR rise harder to see against normal training noise, whereas non-cardiac channels (respiratory rate) can lead.

- **Training status itself moves the same cardiac metrics illness moves.** Detraining raises exercise HR and shifts HRV ([Mujika & Padilla, 2000/2001; DOI](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200030020-00002)); taper/overload move RHR and vagal HRV on a 2-week scale (Section 3). Because infection and training-load change perturb the **same** RHR/HRV channels in overlapping directions and timescales, a training-load change concurrent with illness is a genuine confounder, not just added noise. The participant stopping training *because* they were ill means the cessation and the infection are time-locked, so their RHR effects are entangled by design.

- **Inactivity / bed-rest analogue (the ill, resting participant).** An acutely ill participant who stops training also becomes acutely inactive. Short bed-rest / head-down-tilt studies show plasma volume falls ~9-10% within 4-7 days ([Millet et al., 2001, *Clin Physiol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1046/j.1365-2281.2001.00315.x); [Sigaudo et al., 1996, *Eur J Appl Physiol Occup Physiol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/BF00262806)) and parasympathetic indicators fall after 4-day head-down tilt (Sigaudo). Hypovolaemia tends to **raise** HR (to defend cardiac output), so acute inactivity during illness is a small *additional upward* nudge on RHR, partially in the same direction as the infection, and thus one more reason the "true" infection-only increment is hard to isolate on the raw scale.

- **Honest gap.** I did not find a clean experimental study that directly quantifies how much a low trained baseline *blunts* a given infectious RHR rise (a trained-vs-untrained infection-detection contrast). The confound is well-supported *mechanistically* (personal-baseline dependence + shared metrics) and *indirectly* (athlete detection studies), but the specific magnitude of blunting in trained bodies is under-quantified in the literature retrieved. Treat the confound as physiologically well-grounded but not precisely calibrated.

## 5. Confound adjudication (timescale): deliverable 2, the review's key output

**The question:** over a 14-day window, is detraining-driven RHR rebound LARGE (so it confounds an apparent infection rise) or SMALL/SLOW (so it does not much bite within 2 weeks)? And does the training story better explain the **raw RHR null**, **confound the detrended rise**, or **both**?

**Adjudication:**

1. **Detraining rebound within 14 days is SMALL/SLOW, not large.** The direct cessation time-course (Gamelin: partial autonomic reversal at 2 weeks, continuing to 8) and the landmark reviews (Mujika & Padilla: resting-autonomic reversal is the slower component; the fast reversals are plasma volume, VO2max, and *exercise* HR) converge: a 14-day cessation captures only the early, incomplete part of resting-HR/vagal rebound. So a **detraining-driven upward rebound is unlikely to be large enough, within 2 weeks, to manufacture a spurious detrended "infection rise" on its own.** This protects the detrended estimate: the detrended rise is not principally a detraining artefact, because detraining is too slow to contribute much over 14 days.

2. **The low, still-declining TRAINING BASELINE is the better explanation for the RAW NULL.** The heavy pre-illness block leaves RHR depressed (Section 1) and, if the participant was in a peaking/taper-like phase, still drifting down (Section 3). A personal baseline that is low and sloping downward means the raw peri-event contrast measures the infection increment against a moving, suppressed floor, blunting or masking it (Section 4). This is a **baseline-level and baseline-trend** effect, present from day 0 of the window, and it does not require detraining to have progressed at all. It is therefore the primary training-side explanation for why the RAW daily RHR did not rise.

3. **Therefore: the training story more likely explains the RAW NULL (via a low, downward-trending baseline) than it confounds the DETRENDED RISE (which detraining is too slow to fabricate in 14 days).** In the framing of the question, the answer is **primarily the first** ("explains the raw null"), with a **secondary, smaller** contribution to the detrended side: detraining and acute-inactivity effects do add a modest, slow upward pressure over the fortnight that is superimposed on the infection rise, so the detrended rise is a *slightly* mixed estimate (infection + a little detraining/hypovolaemia), not a purely clean infection effect. Net: detrending is the **better** estimator of the infection effect than the raw scale, but not a perfectly clean one.

**One-line deliverable:** Over 14 days, detraining RHR rebound is **slow and partial** (decisive: it does not much bite within 2 weeks), so the low-and-declining training baseline, not detraining rebound, is the dominant training-side reason the **raw** RHR failed to rise; removing that pre-illness downtrend (detrending) is the more defensible way to recover the infection-driven rise, with the caveat that a small, slow detraining/inactivity upward drift is layered onto that detrended estimate.

## 6. Reader synthesis (one paragraph)

Based on articles retrieved from PubMed, heavy endurance training lowers resting HR by roughly 5-12 bpm over weeks-to-months through a mix of enhanced vagal tone and genuine intrinsic-HR/structural remodelling ([Shi 1995](https://doi.org/10.1249/00005768-199510000-00010); [Lewis 1980](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1748-1716.1980.tb06600.x); [Herzig 2018](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2018.00756)), so at infection onset a heavily-trained participant sits on a **low, and quite possibly still-declining, baseline RHR** ([taper: Ravé 2016](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-016-3416-9); [Carter 2003](https://doi.org/10.1249/01.MSS.0000079046.01763.8F)). When training stops during the illness, the **resting-HR / vagal rebound from detraining is slow relative to a 14-day window**, partial at 2 weeks and still completing at 4-8 weeks ([Gamelin 2007](https://doi.org/10.1055/s-2007-964861); [Mujika & Padilla 2000](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200030020-00002)/[2001](https://doi.org/10.1097/00005768-200103000-00013); [Hansen 2004](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-004-1208-0)), while the *fast*-reverting detraining changes (plasma volume, VO2max, exercise-HR compensation) act mainly on maximal/exercise performance, not on a large resting-HR jump in two weeks. Because wearable infection detectors read RHR **elevation relative to a personal baseline** ([Mishra 2020](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41551-020-00640-6); [Natarajan 2020](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-020-00363-7)), a low and drifting baseline blunts the raw signal, and in athletes the RHR channel is a narrower, later illness discriminator than non-cardiac channels ([Rentería 2023](https://doi.org/10.1177/19417381231183709)). Adjudicating: the **low, downtrending training baseline is the dominant training-side explanation for the raw RHR null**, while **detraining is too slow to fabricate the detrended rise within 14 days**, so detrending is the more defensible estimator of the infection effect, though a small, slow detraining/hypovolaemia upward drift is superimposed on it. The honest limits are that this is population literature (not this participant), the specific magnitude of baseline-blunting in trained bodies is under-quantified, and the taper/overreaching evidence is mixed in sign.

## 7. Honest limits

- **PubMed/PMC-only, English-language (plus one Portuguese abstract), purposive (not exhaustive) search**, run 2026-06-30/07-02. This is the landmark-paper core (Mujika & Padilla detraining reviews; a direct cessation time-course; classic bradycardia-mechanism studies), not a systematic review.
- **No project n=1 data was read.** This review is the external prior; it does not prove what happened in this body. The raw-vs-detrended RHR pattern it interprets was observed outside this document.
- **The blunting magnitude is under-quantified.** The confound is strong *mechanistically* (personal-baseline dependence, shared RHR/HRV channels) and supported *indirectly* (athlete detection studies), but no retrieved study cleanly measures how many bpm a low trained baseline subtracts from an infectious RHR rise. The "raw null is training-explained" claim rests on baseline level + trend logic, not a calibrated blunting coefficient.
- **Detraining time-course studies vary** in training background, cessation completeness, and metric. Gamelin 2007 used young trained men and time/frequency HRV, not RHR per se; the resting-HR rebound timescale is inferred from the autonomic-marker timescale plus the reviews. Directionally consistent, not a perfect match to a Forerunner-245 overnight-RHR channel.
- **Taper/overreaching evidence is mixed in sign** on the 2-week scale (RHR/HRV can move either way depending on load phase); this is carried as a supporting, not decisive, mechanism.
- **Bed-rest/head-down-tilt is an imperfect analogue** for illness-driven inactivity (it adds a gravitational fluid shift), so the hypovolaemia-raises-HR nudge is directional, not quantitative, for this case.
