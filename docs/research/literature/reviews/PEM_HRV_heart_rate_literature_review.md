# Post-Exertional Malaise in Post-Infectious / Chronic Illness: Heart-Rate and HRV Signals Before, During and After a "Crash" — A State-of-the-Art Literature Review

*Evidence retrieved from PubMed. All cited articles include DOI links. Focus: ME/CFS, Long COVID, Q fever fatigue syndrome, and the related exertion-challenge literature (Gulf War Illness), with specific attention to whether heart-rate, HRV and broader autonomic signals are **descriptive**, **detect group differences**, or are **predictive** of PEM.*

---

## 1. The headline finding, stated plainly

The specific thing this brief is chasing — **a wearable HRV/heart-rate signal that tracks a free-living PEM crash before, during, and after it happens** — is, on current published evidence, **largely unstudied and certainly not established.** That is the most honest and most useful thing this review can tell you. The evidence that *does* exist clusters into three uneven tiers:

- **"During/after" detection — moderately strong, but lab-bound.** The two-day cardiopulmonary exercise test (CPET) is the closest thing to an objective physiological signature of PEM, and heart rate is one of its components. But it measures *provoked* exertion in a lab 24 hours apart, not a spontaneous crash, and the signal does **not** clearly generalise across conditions.
- **"Leading up to" / predictive — weak and mostly negative.** The one study that explicitly tried to predict PEM from acute exercise responses (including heart rate) **failed** to do so.
- **Free-living, wearable, continuous HRV around real crashes — essentially a gap.** No retrieved study continuously captured wearable HRV through a naturally occurring PEM episode. This is precisely where your research interest sits, which makes it an opportunity rather than a dead end.

Everything below substantiates and qualifies those three statements.

---

## 2. PEM and the conditions: a shared phenotype across triggers

PEM (also called post-exertional symptom exacerbation, PESE) is the disproportionate, often delayed worsening of symptoms after physical, cognitive or emotional exertion, with prolonged recovery. It is the cardinal feature of ME/CFS and is shared across a family of post-infectious and exertion-sensitive illnesses.

According to PubMed, a detailed road-map review argues that **Long COVID and ME/CFS share substantially overlapping symptoms and biological abnormalities** — including autonomic and cardiovascular dysfunction — and that both belong to a broader class of post-infectious fatigue syndromes ([Komaroff & Lipkin, 2023, *Front Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3389/fmed.2023.1187163)). A mechanistic review of post-acute sequelae of SARS-CoV-2 (PASC) explicitly aligns Long COVID with ME/CFS and POTS, and lists dysautonomia and dysfunctional brainstem/vagus signalling among the candidate drivers ([Sherif et al., 2023, *eLife*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.7554/eLife.86002)). On the ME/CFS side, a comprehensive mechanisms review documents autonomic and neurological dysfunction alongside immune, mitochondrial and metabolic disturbance as part of the underlying pathology ([Missailidis et al., 2019, *Diagnostics*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3390/diagnostics9030080)).

**Q fever fatigue syndrome (QFS)** belongs to the same family. A long-term (≥10 year) outcome study of 368 QFS patients found that **98.9% reported post-exertional malaise**, with low quality of life and high fatigue persisting more than a decade after infection — younger and middle-aged patients faring worse than older ones ([Spronk et al., 2023, *Epidemiol Infect*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1017/S0950268823001401)). The QFS mechanistic literature, however, has concentrated on **chronic inflammation rather than autonomic/HRV signals**: QFS patients show elevated *Coxiella*-stimulated IL-6, TNFα and IL-1β versus controls and even versus CFS patients ([Raijmakers et al., 2019, *J Infect*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jinf.2019.01.006)). Notably, I found **no QFS study examining HRV or heart-rate dynamics around exertion** — a specific gap within an already under-studied condition.

## 3. The "during / after" signal: the two-day CPET paradigm

This is the strongest objective evidence that exertion produces a measurable physiological state-change in these patients — and heart rate is part of that picture.

The largest study to date (84 ME/CFS patients, 71 controls) had participants perform two maximal CPETs 24 hours apart. According to PubMed, controls reproduced their performance on day 2, but **ME/CFS patients failed to reproduce it**, showing significant day-2 declines at peak effort in work, exercise time, oxygen uptake, **heart rate, O₂ pulse**, and blood pressure, plus declines at the ventilatory/anaerobic threshold. Critically, these abnormalities persisted even against controls matched for aerobic fitness — so this is **not deconditioning** — and the authors interpret the pattern as **autonomic dysregulation of blood flow and oxygen delivery** ([Keller et al., 2024, *J Transl Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12967-024-05410-5)). An earlier study established that this day-2 deterioration scales with disease severity, being largest in severe ME/CFS ([van Campen et al., 2020, *Healthcare*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3390/healthcare8030192)). The paradigm has been reproduced in CFS following humidifier-disinfectant exposure ([Leem et al., 2022, *Environ Anal Health Toxicol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.5620/eaht.2022033)) and even in a striking monozygotic-twin case discordant for ME/CFS, where only the ill twin showed an abnormal drop in anaerobic threshold on day 2 ([Giloteaux et al., 2016, *Am J Case Rep*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.12659/ajcr.900314)).

**But here is the tension you should not gloss over.** When the *same* two-day paradigm was applied to **Long COVID**, it did **not** reproduce the finding: despite 80% of patients reporting PEM on questionnaire, there were **no significant differences between day-1 and day-2 CPET responses**, leading the authors to conclude that PEM in Long COVID is *not* explained by impaired between-day recovery of exercise capacity ([Gattoni et al., 2024, *Respir Physiol Neurobiol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.resp.2024.104362)). 

The implication is important: **the CPET "signature" of PEM may be specific to ME/CFS and not a universal post-infectious marker.** Any wearable strategy built on the assumption that a single physiological signature underlies all PEM is therefore on shaky ground.

A practical caveat for your purposes: the two-day CPET captures the *consequence* of exertion 24 hours later as a snapshot. It tells us PEM has a physiological correlate; it does **not** tell us what heart rate or HRV is doing continuously in the hours leading into, during, or recovering from a real-world crash.

## 4. Autonomic / HRV evidence specifically

Several lines point to autonomic involvement, but the direct HRV-around-PEM evidence is thin and mostly indirect.

An editorial framing the field noted explicitly that **autonomic markers, HRV and post-exertion states** are an under-developed but plausible avenue in CFS — essentially flagging the question this brief asks rather than answering it ([Friedberg, 2019, *J Psychosom Res*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jpsychores.2019.109845)). Orthostatic/autonomic testing supports baseline dysautonomia: combining a 10-minute NASA lean test with circulating biomarkers, one cohort found endothelial-dysfunction and inflammatory markers distinguishing ME/CFS and Long COVID from controls, with POTS present in a subset (≈13% of ME/CFS) ([Domingo et al., 2024, *J Transl Med*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12967-024-05148-0)). On the central side, a neuroanatomical review links PEM to dysfunction of midbrain arousal-network nuclei governing autonomic control, threat assessment and orthostatic regulation ([Baraniuk, 2022, *Brain Sci*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3390/brainsci12020132)).

These establish that **autonomic dysfunction is part of the baseline phenotype**. None of them continuously tracks HRV through an exertion-triggered crash.

## 5. Autonomic signals beyond HRV: orthostatic intolerance and cerebral blood flow

If the lens widens from HRV to autonomic regulation as a whole — orthostatic/cardiovascular control, cerebral perfusion, sympathovagal balance — the evidence base is considerably larger and more consistent. This is one of the better-evidenced areas of the field, though it carries the same temporal caveat: it characterises the autonomic *phenotype* and the response to a *provoked* orthostatic stressor, not continuous signals around a spontaneous crash.

**Cardiac autonomic regulation (meta-analytic).** The strongest single synthesis is a systematic review and meta-analysis of 64 case-control studies. According to PubMed, ME/CFS patients showed a reproducible pattern of altered autonomic control versus controls: higher resting heart rate, an exaggerated heart-rate response to head-up tilt, higher orthostatic HR, and a higher LF/HF ratio (a sympathetic shift), together with *lower* maximal HR, lower HR at the anaerobic threshold, and lower high-frequency (vagal) HRV ([Nelson et al., 2019, *Medicine*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1097/MD.0000000000017600)). This is group-level *detection* of autonomic abnormality across many measures — not just HRV — and it ties the resting autonomic state to the blunted exercise responses seen in the CPET literature (Section 3).

**Orthostatic intolerance and cerebral blood flow.** The most distinctive strand goes beyond heart rate entirely, to brain perfusion. Work by the van Campen/Rowe/Visser group has shown that in ME/CFS the autonomic abnormality manifests as an abnormal reduction in cerebral blood flow (CBF) under orthostatic stress. In a large tilt series, the majority of patients had daily orthostatic intolerance and an abnormal CBF reduction during tilt — present **even in patients without POTS or classic orthostatic hypotension**, i.e. the perfusion deficit is not captured by heart-rate criteria alone ([van Campen et al., 2023, *Physiol Rep*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.14814/phy2.15639)). The finding reproduces in severely affected, largely bed-bound patients using a gentler 15-minute, 20-degree tilt (~27% mean CBF decline), comparable to the decline seen in less severe patients at a steeper tilt ([van Campen et al., 2020, *Healthcare*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3390/healthcare8020169)). This matters for PEM specifically because orthostatic stress is itself a recognised PEM trigger, and the signal here is haemodynamic/perfusion-based rather than a heart-rate metric.

**The sympathovagal-imbalance consensus and its mechanisms.** A recurring interpretation across reviews is sympathetic hyperactivity with reduced vagal tone — the "wired but tired" state. A recent review situates this within a broader neurotransmitter imbalance (reduced GABAergic tone, elevated glutamatergic/noradrenergic activity) and proposes it as a contributor to exertional intolerance and PEM ([Wirth & Scheibenbogen, 2026, *Int J Mol Sci*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms27094041)). Candidate mechanisms for the orthostatic/perfusion picture include dysregulated tetrahydrobiopterin (BH4) metabolism affecting cerebral vascular control ([Rahman et al., 2025, *Biomolecules*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3390/biom15010102)) and chronic neuroinflammation / glial dysfunction ([Chaves-Filho et al., 2023, *Brain Res Bull*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.brainresbull.2023.110702)).

**Clinical framing and measurement.** A broader dysautonomia literature places ME/CFS within the autonomic-disorder spectrum (alongside POTS, orthostatic hypotension and neurocardiogenic syncope) and details the standard autonomic test battery — the 10-minute active stand test, tilt-table testing, Valsalva, and deep-breathing RR-variability — a useful menu of validated autonomic readouts ([Blitshteyn, 2025, *Immunol Res*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12026-025-09661-2); [Rocha et al., 2021, *Arq Bras Cardiol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.36660/abc.20200420)).

**The same caveat, restated.** All of this is either baseline phenotyping or the response to a standardised orthostatic/exercise stressor measured cross-sectionally. "Autonomic dysfunction is a robust, reproducible feature of ME/CFS" is well-supported. "Autonomic signals track or predict an individual, free-living PEM crash" remains largely unestablished — and this strand is overwhelmingly ME/CFS, with Long COVID growing and QFS still essentially uncharacterised autonomically.

## 6. The predictive question — and a sobering result

The exertion-challenge literature in Gulf War Illness (GWI), a sister PEM condition, is where the predictive question has been most directly tested. A submaximal challenge (30 min cycling at 70% heart-rate reserve) provoked symptom exacerbation in a subset of GWI veterans 24 hours later, confirming PEM can be elicited and that not all patients show it ([Lindheimer et al., 2019, *Int J Psychophysiol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijpsychro.2019.11.008)).

The key study for your question then asked whether **acute exercise responses — including heart rate, ventilation and perceived exertion — could predict the subsequent PEM response.** They could not: the regression model explained essentially no variance in PEM (adjusted R² ≈ 0.03) ([Boruch et al., 2021, *Life Sci*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.lifs.2021.119701)). 

This is the single most relevant negative result in the corpus: at least with the in-task cardiorespiratory signals tested, **acute heart-rate behaviour did not forecast who would crash or how hard.** It does not rule out that *continuous, multi-day, free-living* HRV could do better — that simply hasn't been tried at scale — but it is a direct caution against assuming the predictive signal is sitting there waiting to be read off heart rate.

## 7. Where wearables actually appear: pacing and management, not crash-detection

Where consumer-grade physiological monitoring enters this literature, it is mostly as a **management tool** rather than a crash-detector or predictor.

A general review confirms wearable HRV is technically feasible and reflects autonomic function, while cautioning on accuracy limits ([Li et al., 2023, *Int J Environ Res Public Health*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijerph20247146)). Clinically, the dominant application is **pacing / staying under an exertion threshold**: a structured 6-week pacing protocol in Long COVID significantly reduced PESE episodes (from 3.4 to 1.1 per week) and improved health status ([Parker et al., 2023, *J Med Virol*; DOI](https://doi.org/10.1002/jmv.28373)). An ongoing telerehabilitation RCT in ME/CFS and Post-COVID Syndrome includes **HRV as a secondary outcome**, signalling that the field is beginning to instrument HRV in interventions — but as an outcome measure, not yet a real-time crash signal ([Fricke-Comellas et al., 2025, *Healthcare* protocol; DOI](https://doi.org/10.3390/healthcare13233062)).

The logic of heart-rate-based pacing (keeping daily HR below the ventilatory/anaerobic threshold to avoid triggering PEM) is widely used in practice and is *consistent* with the CPET findings in section 3 — but note that its evidence base is the pacing outcome, not a demonstration that the wearable detects or predicts the crash itself.

## 9. Critical synthesis against the three sub-questions

**(a) Descriptive — is there a characteristic autonomic / HR/HRV state in these patients?** Yes, and robustly so at baseline. Meta-analysis shows altered cardiac autonomic regulation in ME/CFS (higher resting and orthostatic HR, a sympathetic LF/HF shift, lower vagal HRV and lower HR at the anaerobic threshold); orthostatic intolerance affects the majority, with abnormal cerebral blood flow reductions on tilt even in the absence of POTS or orthostatic hypotension; and autonomic dysfunction is documented across ME/CFS, Long COVID and (by overlap) the post-infectious family. QFS specifically lacks autonomic/HRV characterisation.

**(b) Detection — can a signal distinguish PEM / a crashed state from baseline?** Partially, and condition-dependently. At the autonomic level, group differences are well-established for ME/CFS (meta-analytic HR/HRV abnormalities; abnormal cerebral blood flow on orthostatic challenge). For the post-exertional state specifically, the two-day CPET reliably detects impaired recovery in **ME/CFS** (blunted peak heart rate and reduced O₂ pulse on day 2), but the same protocol did **not** detect a difference in **Long COVID**. Crucially, all of these distinguish *patients from controls* or *provoked states from rest* — there is still no validated free-living/wearable signal that flags a spontaneous crash as it develops.

**(c) Predictive — can a signal forecast an impending crash?** This is the weakest area. The one direct test (in GWI) found acute heart-rate responses did not predict PEM. No prospective wearable study has demonstrated HRV forecasting of a free-living crash.

## 10. The gap — and what a study could target

The literature has a clear, exploitable hole at the intersection of your interest:

1. **Continuous, free-living HRV through spontaneous PEM episodes** — capturing the *lead-up* (the hours/days before a crash), the crash, and recovery — is essentially unpublished. The existing physiology is almost all provoked, lab-based, and snapshot.
2. **Condition-specificity must be designed in.** Given that the CPET signature appears in ME/CFS but not Long COVID, pooling conditions risks washing out real effects. QFS in particular is a near-blank slate for autonomic/wearable work.
3. **Predictive modelling has only been tried with in-task acute signals and failed.** Multi-day trajectories of resting HRV, overnight HRV, HR-relative-to-threshold during daily activity, and serial orthostatic measures (stand-test HR or wearable-proxy perfusion signals) — the kind of data a wrist wearable or chest strap yields continuously — have not been tested as crash predictors and are the obvious next move. The robust *baseline* autonomic abnormalities (Section 5) are candidate features, but their value as a *time-varying* crash signal is untested.
4. **Heart-rate-threshold pacing is accepted clinically but under-validated as a *detection/prediction* mechanism**, as opposed to a behavioural guardrail.

In short: descriptive autonomic abnormality is established; lab detection of the post-exertional state is established for ME/CFS only; and **predictive, wearable, free-living HRV signalling of PEM is an open research question, not a solved one.**

---

## 11. Bottom line

According to PubMed, exertion produces a measurable, autonomically-mediated physiological state-change in ME/CFS that includes blunted heart-rate and oxygen-pulse responses on repeat testing — but this signature is lab-provoked, snapshot, and does **not** clearly reproduce in Long COVID, and the one attempt to use acute heart-rate responses to *predict* PEM failed. Wearable HRV currently lives in the *pacing/management* corner of this field, not the detection-or-prediction corner. The continuous, free-living, condition-specific wearable HRV study of PEM crashes that this brief envisions has not yet been done — which is exactly why it would be worth doing.
