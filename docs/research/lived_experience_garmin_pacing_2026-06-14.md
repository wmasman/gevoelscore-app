## The braindump

### Garmin notes

Stress high whilst movement indicators say no or little
movement/steps are interesting. It's a body in overdrive, especially
when it shows this in the evening or at least after midday.

The up and down body battery in daytime: when up, it indicates a
restful nap; when quicker down than normal/standard, it indicates
extra exertion.

Sporting/exercise periods on a day always are worth a label because
of risk of PEM if not managed well.

### What data we have

We have data on healthy, illness, COVID, start of long COVID,
started with ergotherapie/pacing and learning to pace, the episode
where I was trying to recover and also work, the period when work
stopped (emotionally heavy), and the stabilisation period. And
during this, starting with citalopram and also stopping again, CPAP
machine, and earlier also when I tried to do some running etc. and
its effects.

### Pacing-quality question

In the period where I do pacing more correctly, can we see maybe
both less extreme exertion peaks, but also better resting/active
recovery behaviour after or before peaks?

### Timeline

Make a timeline with important events and interventions, like getting
ill, moments of emotional and physical stress, etc.

### How I used the Garmin for pacing

Describe how I used the Garmin for pacing: recognising high stress;
using BB score at the beginning of the day not to follow but as
indicator. More importantly, make sure you don't empty BB at end of
day; try to keep above 20/25, or at least 15. And actively rest or
go to bed earlier if battery is up for that day.

Recognise when body is not able to recover anymore in a day. Maybe
in the morning you can recover from stress; later in the day BB
continues to go down even when you lie down. On these days,
definitely don't push it.

### Crash fingerprint question

Do crashes have a distinct "fingerprint" that is recognisable in
Garmin data? And if yes, what is this? And if yes: would it be
possible to see this fingerprint also in other people?

### Multi-scale dynamics and the mediating narrative

We are dealing with multiple trends at once maybe — more
longer-term ones: swings that go over multiple weeks/months,
strings of crashes etc. Maybe periods as response to a previous
longer period of ineffective pacing? Illness?

Most clearly, at the onset of LC we had the acute illness, an
initial partial recovery in which I did not know what pacing was,
where I tried to apply normal things after normal illness, such as
exercise to "sweat it out" — not adaptive to an LC body, with
initial crash phase as the result (not measured with gevoelscore
etc.).

Then we got ergotherapie — not immediately effective of course;
learning to pace. Crashes from then on did also involve attempts
to pace in the period leading into the crash. Here we also tried
to re-integrate and there was the emotional phase with relationship
issues, all making it more complicated to pace sometimes.

Then we had the period where work ended at PwC and into the WIA.
Emotional and stressful at first, and later on the rest that I
needed to be able to pace myself. Here challenges lied in some
exertion days when going away with the kids to Efteling, sailing,
the period just before launching the TVO platform, and periods
where partner was away for work. But pacing is going better.

The above narrative is one we maybe can also detect in the data
(Garmin?) and it is a mediating story that is context for each
crash. When we want to study crashes in more detail (descriptive)
or compare crashes (on Garmin data etc.), this is important
context maybe to keep in mind. The 1-3 exertion scores may also
be helpful to give colour to exertion over our timeline leading
into or during crashes.

Then, when we are going to try to predict crashes from data, or
try to find things that are warning signs that may lead into a
crash, we can take the longer trends or circumstances into account
maybe. Or maybe not necessary because our research design controls
for that. Some intuitive ideas are that crash risk is increased in
a period with multiple crashes. Things we have to test empirically.

### Within-day vs daily aggregates

Once we go into the more detailed study of the fingerprint of days
leading into a crash, we probably should look at data within the
day, not at the aggregates that are averaged at daily level — that
is not granular enough.

### Resting heart rate after overdoing it

Wiggers quote (PDF lines 165-177):

> Sometimes your heart rate drops to your correct resting heart rate
> after a few minutes, but if you've pushed yourself much too far,
> it can take hours. I'm already unhappy with a resting heart rate
> that's 5–10 beats higher. I have really thoroughly overdone it
> when my resting heart rate is a 100 bpm instead of 60 for hours.

Implication for our testable hypotheses, once we are going to
operationalise this: we should be very aware of what is within
one day, and we need to fingerprint correctly on detailed enough
information so the signal doesn't disappear in aggregates. Then
we can label or score a day based on the underlying pattern in
that day for the multi-day analysis and patterns.
