# Two Types of Post-Exertional Malaise? A Literature Review of Acute vs. Cumulative PEM Across Post-Acute Infection Syndromes

## TL;DR
- **Partially supported, but not as a formal two-type taxonomy.** The peer-reviewed literature does NOT name or formally separate your two proposed PEM types ("shock-induced acute" vs. "cumulative push-crash"). However, the strongest qualitative study in the field (Stussman et al., 2020, *Frontiers in Neurology*) independently found exactly the empirical signature your hypothesis predicts: a single discrete maximal exertion (CPET) produces immediate, severe, longer-lasting PEM, whereas accumulated daily activity produces delayed (12–48h), "compounding" PEM. The "cumulative/rolling/push-crash" pattern is well-developed in clinical and patient literature (Workwell, Bateman Horne Center) but lacks a peer-reviewed operational definition.
- **The dominant taxonomies are about something else.** Existing PEM subtyping splits by symptom domain (physical vs. mental PEM) and by severity, not by temporal/trigger dynamics. So the field's formal taxonomies neither confirm nor refute your model — they simply have not asked your question.
- **Mechanistically, the evidence currently favors ONE core mechanism (impaired bioenergetic recovery / mitochondrial-metabolic failure) governed by a threshold that is itself dynamic and load-dependent** — which can generate BOTH an acute supra-threshold crash AND a cumulative push-crash without requiring two separate biological mechanisms. No mechanistic study maps distinct biology onto acute vs. cumulative triggers; this is a genuine, important open question.

## Key Findings

1. **No researcher has formally named or empirically separated "acute single-trigger PEM" vs. "cumulative push-crash PEM" as distinct entities in peer-reviewed work.** The concepts exist but are split across the literature: the immediate-vs-delayed onset distinction is empirical and peer-reviewed; the cumulative "rolling PEM" concept is clinical/patient-derived.

2. **Stussman et al. (2020, *Frontiers in Neurology* 11:1025) is the closest independent corroboration of your two-pattern intuition.** In 43 ME/CFS patients (NIH intramural study), more than three-quarters reported PEM onset immediately or within several hours after CPET (a single discrete maximal exertion), versus "nearly half reporting symptoms beginning 12–48 h after exertion" in daily life. CPET-induced PEM was also more severe and longer-lasting. Patients described "compounding"/baseline-exacerbation when daily exertions stacked.

3. **Chu et al. (2018, *PLoS One* 13(6):e0197811, n=150) confirms both immediate and delayed onset coexist within the same patients, and that the PEM threshold is cumulative-load-dependent.** Onset "varied" in ~42% (61/145), with immediate being the most common earliest time within that group; 11% reported a consistent ≥24h delay; 84% endured PEM ≥24h. "One hundred and twenty-nine subjects (90%) experienced PEM with both physical and cognitive exertion and emotional distress." Chu explicitly states the trigger threshold depends on baseline state and "how much total activity [the patient] engaged in this week so far" — direct peer-reviewed support for cumulative/dose dynamics.

4. **The formal PEM taxonomies that DO exist are orthogonal to your hypothesis.** Tuzzolino, Jason & Furst (2026, *Journal of Health Psychology*, doi:10.1177/13591053261420598) subtype 2,068 DSQ respondents into physical-PEM / mental-PEM / both / neither (1,535 in the "both severe" group). Severity-based gradients (van Campen, Rowe & Visser, 2020) show severe patients have the largest day-2 CPET workload decline and lower triggering thresholds. None address acute vs. cumulative temporal dynamics.

5. **The 2-day CPET is the field's objective anchor and models the ACUTE single-bout trigger.** ME/CFS patients uniquely fail to reproduce workload/VO₂ at the ventilatory/anaerobic threshold on day 2 — objective evidence of impaired recovery from a single supra-threshold event. This is well-replicated.

6. **The Appelman/Wüst (2024, *Nature Communications* 15:17) muscle-biopsy work is the most important recent mechanistic study — and it explicitly separates "fatigue/reduced exercise capacity" from "PEM."** After a single maximal CPET, the 25 long COVID patients showed worsened mitochondrial (SDH) activity, focal muscle necrosis, increased amyloid-containing deposits, and immune infiltration. Crucially, the authors state the next-day PEM cannot be explained by the (slow) fiber-type shifts, and that the mechanistic trigger of PEM "remains elusive."

7. **PEM is shared across the PAIS family but unevenly measured.** It is well-documented in ME/CFS, long COVID, and Q-fever fatigue syndrome; in post-Ebola, post-Lyme/PTLDS, and post-mononucleosis, fatigue and exertion intolerance are documented but PEM is often not specifically measured — a recognized gap. In the Dubbo Infection Outcomes Study (Hickie et al., 2006, *BMJ* 333(7568):575; n=253, EBV/Ross River virus/Q fever), prolonged illness was evident in 12% at six months, of whom 11% met chronic fatigue syndrome criteria (9% at 12 months).

## Details

### A. What taxonomies / subtypes of PEM exist in the literature?

The honest answer to your priority question #1 is: **other researchers observe the building blocks of your two patterns, but no one has assembled them into your specific two-type model.** The field's actual subtyping efforts run along different axes:

- **Symptom-domain subtyping (the only formal published PEM taxonomy).** Tuzzolino, Jason & Furst (2026, *Journal of Health Psychology*, doi:10.1177/13591053261420598; online ahead of print 28 Feb 2026) analyzed 2,068 ME/CFS patients on the DePaul Symptom Questionnaire and defined four subtypes by physical vs. mental PEM severity: "both severe" (1,535 patients), "physical only," "mental only," "neither severe." The "both" group had the highest symptom burden; even the "neither" group was significantly more disabled than controls. This is a symptom-quality taxonomy, not a temporal/trigger taxonomy.

- **Severity-gradient differences in threshold.** van Campen, Rowe & Visser (2020, *Healthcare* 8(3):192) ran 2-day CPET in 82 female ME/CFS patients (mild/moderate/severe by International Consensus Criteria) and found the severe group had the largest day-2 peak-workload decline (mild-vs-severe difference significant, p=0.019), the first demonstration that disease severity worsens exercise capacity. Very severe/bedridden patients have triggering thresholds so low that orthostatic stress (even sitting) and sensory stimuli (light, sound) act as PEM triggers, and they are routinely excluded from tilt testing because they cannot tolerate it. This maps onto your "severity-dependent threshold" sub-question: as severity increases, the supra-threshold "shock" can be something as small as sitting upright.

- **Onset-timing characterization.** Multiple patient-driven instruments (Holtzman, Cotler & Jason, 2019, *Diagnostics* 9(1):26; Chu et al., 2018) explicitly catalog that PEM onset "can be immediate or delayed by hours, days, or longer," establishing immediate and delayed onset as recognized, coexisting features — but they do not link these to two distinct trigger mechanisms.

- **Cluster analyses in long COVID** repeatedly find symptom-burden clusters and multi-system phenotypes (e.g., neurological/multisystemic, psychiatric/neurological, physical/respiratory), with PEM loading heavily in the highest-burden cluster — but again these are symptom-co-occurrence clusters, not PEM-dynamic clusters.

**Verdict on taxonomy:** There is no published taxonomy that separates acute-shock vs. cumulative-push PEM. Your model is a novel framing relative to the formal literature, even though its components are individually attested.

### B. Clinical characterization: acute (shock) vs. cumulative (push-crash) patterns

This is where your hypothesis finds its strongest independent support, primarily from **Stussman et al. (2020, *Frontiers in Neurology* 11:1025)**, the NIH intramural qualitative study of 43 ME/CFS patients. The study did not set out to test your model, but its Theme 4 is essentially a description of it:

- **Acute/single-bout pattern (CPET-induced):** ">three-quarters of participants reporting the onset of symptoms following CPET said they began immediately or within several hours." For some, symptoms began *while still on the bike*. CPET PEM was more immediate, more severe ("multiplied by a factor of five"), and longer-lasting; one patient had not returned to baseline four months later. This is precisely your "shock-induced/acute-deviation episode — a single discrete supra-threshold stressor."

- **Cumulative/daily pattern:** "For daily PEM, most participants perceived a delayed onset of symptoms, with nearly half reporting symptoms beginning 12–48 h after exertion." Patients emphasized "compounding" — when they overexerted while already in PEM, "it's not just an add-on, it's a multiplier… an exponential effect." This is your "push-crash… cumulative-load trajectory."

**Chu et al. (2018, *PLoS One* 13(6):e0197811, n=150)** independently supports the cumulative-threshold concept: the PEM threshold "depends on both the baseline state of individuals… [and] how much total activity they engaged in this week so far." Within the ~42% whose onset "varied," the most common earliest onset was immediate; 11% had a consistent ≥24h delay; 84% endured PEM ≥24h. The key point is that **immediate and delayed onset occur in the same individuals depending on context** — which argues against the two patterns being two separate diseases and for them being two manifestations of one dynamic system.

**"Rolling PEM" / "cumulative PEM" / "compounded PEM"** is your push-crash pattern under its clinical name. Critically, this terminology appears **only in clinical/patient/advocacy sources** (Workwell Foundation's "PEM Time Course" document; Bateman Horne Center crash materials; patient communities), **not in peer-reviewed literature**. It is defined as incomplete recovery stacking across episodes until baseline shifts downward — a gradual decline rather than a discrete crash. The CDC clinical guidance and ME/CFS clinical literature explicitly warn about "push-and-crash" / "boom-bust" cycles when patients overdo it on good days.

A note of scientific caution: the ME/CFS Skeptic / mecfsscience.org analyses argue that the simple "boom-bust" cycle (large day-to-day activity swings) is NOT well-supported by actigraphy data, and that the real abnormality is subtler than dramatic activity oscillations. So the cumulative pattern, while clinically intuitive, has weaker objective-data backing than the acute single-bout pattern (which the 2-day CPET objectively demonstrates).

### C. Mechanistic evidence, organized by mechanism, with mapping to acute vs. cumulative triggers

This is the most important section for your purposes, and the central finding is: **no mechanistic study maps distinct biology onto acute vs. cumulative triggers.** Most mechanisms are studied using a single acute exertion (1-day or 2-day CPET). Below, each mechanism is assessed for which trigger pattern it best fits.

**1. Impaired aerobic energy metabolism / mitochondrial dysfunction (strongest evidence; best fits the ACUTE supra-threshold model).**
- The 2-day CPET literature is the field's most robust objective finding: ME/CFS patients fail to reproduce VO₂ and workload at the ventilatory/anaerobic threshold on day 2 (Snell/Stevens/VanNess/Davenport 2013; Keller 2014; Nelson 2019; meta-analyzed by Lim et al., with workload@VT showing the largest patient–control divergence on test 2). After PEM induction, the anaerobic threshold *lowers* — anaerobic metabolism begins at a lower workload (Vermeulen; Lien et al. 2019, *Physiological Reports*, showing impaired lactate clearance and elevated arterial lactate per watt that worsened on day 2).
- This maps cleanly onto an **acute threshold-crossing ("shock") mechanism**: cross the (individual, dynamic) anaerobic threshold → trigger a bioenergetic crisis. The threshold is per-patient and measurable, and Workwell's heart-rate-based pacing (keep HR below the V/AT; ~85% of patients have chronotropic incompetence, so use resting HR + ~15 bpm) is explicitly built on this acute-threshold logic.

**2. Skeletal muscle pathology (Appelman/Wüst — the pivotal recent work; ambiguous on acute vs. cumulative).**
- Appelman, Charlton, Wüst et al. (2024, *Nature Communications* 15:17, doi:10.1038/s41467-023-44432-3), 25 long COVID patients + 21 controls, induced PEM with a single maximal CPET and took vastus lateralis biopsies before and one day after. Findings during PEM: reduced succinate dehydrogenase (SDH) activity post-exertion (p=0.0083), reduced OXPHOS capacity, a shift toward highly-fatigable glycolytic type IIx fibers, focal muscle necrosis (large necrotic areas in 36% of patients post-exercise), increased amyloid-containing deposits (p=0.008, both groups), and macrophage/T-cell infiltration. The authors summarize that "local and systemic metabolic disturbances, severe exercise-induced myopathy and tissue infiltration of amyloid-containing deposits in skeletal muscles of patients with long COVID worsen after induction of post-exertional malaise."
- Two critical interpretive points: (i) the authors **explicitly dissociate "fatigue/reduced exercise capacity" from "PEM"** — the fiber-type shift is too slow to explain next-day PEM, so reduced capacity and acute PEM are *distinct*; (ii) they found **no viral-persistence difference and amyloid deposits NOT inside capillaries**, so their data do NOT support the microclot-causes-hypoxia model. The mechanistic trigger of PEM "remains elusive."
- This study used a single acute bout, so it models the **acute trigger**; but the "vicious downward circle" the authors describe (exertion → muscle damage → reduced capacity → easier to crash) is a plausible biological substrate for the **cumulative push-crash**. The 2025 follow-up (Charlton et al., medRxiv preprint 2025.05.02.25326885, comparing 25 long COVID + 26 ME/CFS against a 60-day head-down bed-rest cohort) shows patients are "not simply deconditioned": bed rest causes atrophy, whereas patients show capillary rarefaction and a glycolytic shift instead. (The companion review is Charlton et al., 2025, *Trends in Endocrinology & Metabolism* 36(7):614–622 — note: not *Trends in Molecular Medicine*.)

**3. Autonomic dysfunction / sympathetic overactivation / HRV (fits BOTH; provides the threshold machinery).**
- Meta-analytic evidence shows resting sympathetic dominance (higher resting HR, elevated LF/HF), parasympathetic withdrawal (lower high-frequency HRV), lower max HR, and lower HR at anaerobic threshold (chronotropic incompetence). Post-exertion, vagal "braking" is impaired — HR stays elevated; in long COVID, HRV stayed depressed for a full 24h after exercise (vs. 3–6h recovery in controls).
- Autonomic dysfunction is attractive precisely because it can generate **both** patterns: an acute HR/sympathetic spike (your "shock") and a slow failure of overnight parasympathetic recovery that lets deficits accumulate across days (your "push-crash"). Systrom's invasive CPET work (Joseph et al., 2021, *Chest* 160(2):642–651) identifies "preload failure" (low cardiac filling pressures, impaired systemic oxygen extraction) in nearly all ME/CFS and long COVID patients, often with small-fiber neuropathy — a neurovascular substrate for exertion intolerance. Pyridostigmine (Mestinon) improved exercise output by addressing preload (Joseph et al., 2022, *Chest*).

**4. Immune activation / cytokines (time-course evidence best fits the DELAYED/cumulative recovery window).**
- Exercise-challenge transcriptomics (Cornell/Hanson group; Moneghetti et al., 2018, *Scientific Reports*) show the most dramatic ME/CFS-specific molecular changes occur during the 24h *recovery* window — exactly when delayed PEM emerges. ME/CFS immune cells fail to mount normal stress defenses after exertion and show prolonged, maladaptive cytokine regulation. A distinct post-exercise cytokine signature (CXCL10, IL-8, CCL4, TNF-β, ICAM-1) appears, and high-PEM patients (White et al., 2010) show opposite cytokine responses to low-PEM patients. Sex-dependent transcriptomic differences are reported (IL-12/NK-cell pathways in males; cell-stress/NF-κB in females).
- The delayed kinetics (peak molecular dysregulation at ~24h) map onto the **delayed-onset/cumulative** pattern better than the immediate shock pattern.

**5. Metabolomic / epigenetic recovery abnormalities (fits the cumulative/delayed window).**
- Urine metabolomics show anomalous recovery after maximal exertion in female ME/CFS; McGregor et al. (2019, *Diagnostics*) link PEM to hypermetabolism, hypoacetylation, and purine deregulation. Moreau's Montreal group uses a cuff-induced PEM challenge to derive microRNA signatures that subtype patients (11 miRNAs distinguishing ME/CFS with ~90% accuracy; Long COVID clustered into 6 subgroups). A small DNA-methylation study (5 patients, 2-day CPET) found early (0–24h), late (24–48h), and gradually-changing methylation fragments — a molecular hint of multiple temporal phases within a single exertion response.

**6. Neuroinflammation / vagus-brainstem (hypothesis-stage; trigger-agnostic).**
- Nakatomi et al. (2014, *J Nucl Med* 55(6):945–950) found elevated TSPO/microglial PET signal (45–199% higher) in 9 ME/CFS patients, correlating with symptom severity (small n; first-generation tracer). VanElzakker's vagus-nerve infection hypothesis (2013, *Medical Hypotheses* 81(3):414–423) proposes neuroinflammatory "sickness behavior" signaling via vagal afferents. Recent TSPO-PET in post-COVID shows elevated signal up to 2 years post-infection. These are mostly trigger-agnostic and small/contested.

**7. Endothelial dysfunction / microclots (contested; relevant to your infection-vs-exertion distinction).**
- Endothelial dysfunction (impaired flow-mediated dilation) is found in both ME/CFS and long COVID with no difference between them (McLaughlin/Sculthorpe et al., 2023, *Am J Med*). Fibrin-amyloid microclots (Kell, Laubscher & Pretorius, 2022, *Biochem J* 479(4):537–559) are hypothesized to block capillaries and impair oxygen delivery, but this is preprint-heavy and contested — and notably, the Appelman/Wüst biopsy data argue against microclots as the muscle PEM mechanism (amyloid deposits were extracellular, not intracapillary; no hypoxia markers).

**The integrative model.** A 2026 review (Jin et al., *Frontiers in Immunology*, doi:10.3389/fimmu.2026.1774310) proposes a "metabolism-immune-neuro" two-hit model: infection is the first hit (priming), exertion the second hit (trigger), with mitochondrial bioenergetic failure → DAMP release → NLRP3/NF-κB immune activation → neuroinflammation/interoceptive (insular cortex) dysregulation forming a self-amplifying loop. It also notes that TSPO (inflammation) and FDG-PET (metabolic) abnormalities can be independent, hinting at inflammation-driven vs. metabolic-defect-driven subtypes. This framework naturally accommodates both your patterns as one mechanism operating at different load scales, rather than two mechanisms. (Caveat: narrative/integrative review, heavily hedged, partly built on animal models.)

### D. Honest verdict on the two-type hypothesis

**The evidence most parsimoniously supports ONE core mechanism with a dynamic, load-dependent threshold — which produces your two patterns as two operating regimes, not two diseases.** Reasoning:

- **For two distinct types:** Stussman (2020) shows a real phenomenological dissociation — acute CPET PEM (immediate, severe, long) genuinely differs from daily cumulative PEM (delayed, compounding). The molecular time-course data (immediate metabolic/anaerobic crisis vs. delayed immune/metabolomic recovery dysregulation) hint that an acute supra-threshold bout and a slow cumulative load might recruit partially different downstream cascades.

- **Against two distinct types (the stronger position):** Chu (2018) shows immediate and delayed PEM occur in the same patients depending on baseline and weekly cumulative load — i.e., the same person flips between your "type 1" and "type 2" as a function of their current position relative to a moving threshold. The Energy Envelope Theory (Jason et al., 2008; O'Connor et al., 2017) models both a single large overstep and sustained small oversteps as the same event ("exceeding the envelope"), and its empirical anchor (overexertion correlates with fatigue *days later*) is itself a cumulative-delay finding. No mechanistic study has found two separable biological mechanisms.

- **Could there be MORE than two?** Yes — and the literature's actual subtypes (physical vs. mental PEM; severity gradients; orthostatic/sensory vs. physical/cognitive/emotional triggers; long COVID symptom clusters) suggest PEM heterogeneity is multidimensional. Your acute/cumulative axis is one plausible dimension, but trigger-type (physical/cognitive/emotional/orthostatic/sensory), symptom-domain (physical/mental), and severity are independently attested axes.

**Best-supported synthesis:** PEM is most likely **one mechanism — impaired/maladaptive recovery from exertion rooted in bioenergetic-autonomic-immune dysfunction — governed by an individual threshold that is dynamic (it falls after a crash, falls with cumulative load, and falls with severity).** Your "shock" pattern is a single discrete crossing of that threshold; your "push-crash" pattern is the threshold being repeatedly nudged downward by incomplete overnight recovery until a crash becomes unavoidable. This unifying interpretation fully accounts for both patterns without positing two mechanisms — but it is an inference, not a directly tested result.

### E. Your infection-driven vs. exertion-driven crash distinction

You separately distinguish "infection-driven floor-crashes" from "exertion-driven sags." This maps onto the **two-hit model** (infection = priming first hit; exertion = triggering second hit), and patient surveys do list infections/exposures as relapse triggers separate from exertion. However, **no study formally contrasts the physiology of a new-infection relapse vs. an exertion-induced PEM episode head-to-head** — this is a clean, unaddressed gap that your framing usefully highlights.

## Recommendations

**For framing your hypothesis (immediate):**
1. Reframe from "two mechanisms" to "one dynamic-threshold mechanism with two operating regimes (acute supra-threshold crossing vs. cumulative threshold-erosion)." This is more defensible against the existing evidence and still preserves the clinically useful acute-vs-cumulative distinction.
2. Adopt the existing vocabulary to connect to the literature: "immediate vs. delayed-onset PEM" (peer-reviewed) and "rolling/cumulative/compounded PEM" (clinical) for your push-crash type; cite Stussman 2020 and Chu 2018 as your anchors.

**For evidence-gathering / testing the model (next steps):**
3. The decisive missing study is a **wearable + ecological momentary assessment (EMA) time-series design** that records per-patient activity/HR relative to the individual anaerobic threshold and distinguishes (a) single supra-threshold spikes from (b) sustained sub-threshold cumulative load, then measures PEM onset latency, peak, and duration for each. Tools already exist (Visible/Visible Plus; PRO-Diary actigraphy; Bateman Horne Center's "Hours of Upright Activity"). No published study has cleanly separated threshold-crossing from dose-accumulation as predictors of PEM — this is the highest-value experiment.
4. To test whether acute vs. cumulative triggers recruit different biology, pair the Appelman/Wüst biopsy/metabolomics approach with TWO provocation arms: a single maximal CPET (acute) vs. a multi-day sub-maximal cumulative-load protocol (cumulative), with sampling across the 0–72h window.

**Benchmarks that would change the conclusion:**
5. If a time-series study shows PEM onset/severity is predicted by **threshold-crossing independent of cumulative dose** in some episodes and by **cumulative dose independent of any single threshold crossing** in others, that would upgrade the verdict from "one dynamic mechanism" toward "two genuine mechanisms."
6. If biopsy/omics provocation studies show **divergent molecular signatures** for acute vs. cumulative protocols, that would be direct mechanistic support for your two-type model. Convergent signatures would confirm the single-mechanism view.

## Caveats

- **Evidence-strength tiers.** *Well-established:* the 2-day CPET reproducibility decline; PEM as the cardinal, cross-PAIS symptom; delayed-onset (12–72h) as characteristic. *Emerging/preliminary:* the Appelman/Wüst muscle pathology (one peer-reviewed cohort + a preprint follow-up); immune/metabolomic recovery-window dysregulation; Systrom's preload failure. *Hypothesis/theory:* neuroinflammation/vagus, microclots, the integrative two-hit model, and — importantly — your acute-vs-cumulative distinction itself, which is not a tested construct.
- **The "cumulative/rolling PEM" concept is clinically robust but scientifically under-validated**; actigraphy critiques (mecfsscience.org) caution that simple boom-bust activity patterns are not clearly visible in objective data.
- **Much mechanistic work is post-2020 and COVID-driven, with small samples** (Nakatomi n=9; Appelman n=25; methylation n=5) and some preprint-stage; replication is pending.
- **PAIS generalization is uneven.** PEM is well-characterized in ME/CFS and long COVID, adequately in Q-fever fatigue syndrome, but poorly measured in post-Ebola, post-Lyme/PTLDS, and post-mono — so the presumption of shared PEM mechanisms across all PAIS is reasonable but not yet empirically established for several of them.
- **A field disagreement to flag:** the NIH deep-phenotyping study (Walitt et al., 2024, *Nature Communications* 15:907) controversially emphasized altered "effort preference" and central mechanisms, which many patients and researchers criticized as downplaying peripheral/biological PEM; this tension matters when interpreting central vs. peripheral mechanistic claims.
