# PAIS, PEM, and our data — a literature scan

*A targeted review answering three questions for the gevoelsscore project: (1) what symptoms people with post-acute infection syndromes commonly report, (2) what post-exertional malaise is and how it can be recognised or anticipated, and (3) whether the patterns in our own data — the two kinds of "down day" and the delayed cost of activity — are echoed in the broader research.*

> **Scope.** This is a reading of the published literature, not medical advice. Where a source is a single-author review or a preprint, that is flagged. The mapping to our data in §3 stays deliberately light, as requested — it points out where the literature agrees with us, not where our n-of-1 numbers are "proven."

---

## 1. Common symptoms people with PAIS report

The useful framing is **post-acute infection syndrome (PAIS)** as an umbrella: long-lasting illness that follows an infection which doesn't properly resolve. Choutka, Jansari, Hornig and Iwasaki's 2022 *Nature Medicine* review made the central observation that PAISs triggered by very different pathogens — SARS-CoV-2 (Long COVID), *Borrelia* (post-treatment Lyme), Epstein–Barr, Ebola, Q fever, Ross River virus — converge on a strikingly similar symptom profile, and that this profile overlaps heavily with ME/CFS. That convergence is the reason it makes sense to read across all these conditions when interpreting our data, rather than treating Long COVID as its own island.

A **core set of symptoms recurs across essentially every PAIS, regardless of the triggering pathogen**:

- pathological fatigue (not relieved by rest)
- cognitive dysfunction ("brain fog" — impaired memory and attention)
- unrefreshing sleep
- pain, especially myalgia
- **headache**

A 2025 review of the core features and diversity of PAIS (Mariani, *Frontiers in Neurology*) tabulates symptom frequencies side by side for Long COVID, ME/CFS and post-treatment Lyme. The five symptoms above are marked **"very frequent" (>50%) in all three conditions** — they are the shared spine of the illness. (Worth flagging for us specifically: **headache sits in that top tier across all three PAIS**, not as a peripheral complaint.)

Around that core sits a **large, variable set of "diverse" symptoms**, present in many patients but at differing rates:

- post-exertional malaise (very frequent in Long COVID and ME/CFS)
- orthostatic intolerance / POTS and other dysautonomia (palpitations, temperature dysregulation, "hot and cold spells")
- shortness of breath, chest symptoms
- gastrointestinal symptoms (nausea, altered bowel habit, bloating, reflux)
- sensory hypersensitivity (to light, sound, smell, foods, medicines)
- paresthesia, tinnitus, vertigo/ataxia, blurred vision
- muscle weakness and joint pain
- mood and emotional symptoms — increased irritability/emotionality, secondary anxiety and depression
- recurrent infections, mild fevers, flu-like spells

The defining feature of this second layer is **heterogeneity**: which diverse symptoms a given person gets, and how severe they are, varies enormously between individuals and over time. Both reviews stress that PAIS typically run a **relapsing–remitting course** — symptoms flare and settle in waves rather than holding steady. (This matters directly for §3.)

For **Long COVID specifically**, the patient-led characterisation study by Davis et al. (2021, *eClinicalMedicine*) catalogued **205 distinct symptoms** across ten organ systems in an international cohort, and found that the symptoms most central to the illness were fatigue, post-exertional malaise and cognitive dysfunction ("brain fog"). The US CDC's current Long COVID symptom list and Stichting Long COVID's patient-facing summary both group the common complaints the same way: neurological (fatigue, brain fog, headache), cardiorespiratory (palpitations, breathlessness, chest pain), and a broad "diverse" tail (muscle pain, GI, sleep). Notably, Davis et al. found that **people still experiencing PEM at six months carried a substantially higher total symptom burden** than those who never had it or whose PEM had resolved — i.e. PEM tracks with how sick and how multi-symptom someone is.

The Dutch research landscape fits this picture. Stichting Long COVID funds and points to work in the Post-COVID Netwerk Nederland / ZonMw programme on mechanisms — autoantibodies, neuro-inflammation, and (Brent Appelman's group at Amsterdam UMC) nerve-cell and muscle damage explaining why fatigue, pain and concentration problems persist. The Amsterdam UMC muscle work feeds directly into the PEM section below.

**Takeaway for Q1.** There is a stable, pathogen-independent core — fatigue, cognitive dysfunction, unrefreshing sleep, pain, and headache — wrapped in a highly individual cloud of dysautonomic, GI, sensory and mood symptoms, running a relapsing course. Headache being a *core* PAIS symptom (not a footnote) is consistent with the outsized role it plays in our own data.

## 2. Post-exertional malaise: what it is, how to recognise it, and what may precede it

### Definition

Post-exertional malaise (PEM) — also called **post-exertional symptom exacerbation (PESE)** in the physiotherapy literature, or post-exertional neuroimmune exhaustion (PENE) — is the **worsening of existing symptoms and/or appearance of new ones after physical, cognitive, emotional or orthostatic exertion that would have been tolerated before the illness**. It is the hallmark, near-defining symptom of ME/CFS (it is a required criterion in the 2015 US Institute of Medicine / SEID definition and the Canadian Consensus Criteria), and the majority of people with Long COVID experience it too.

Crucially, PEM is **not** ordinary tiredness or deconditioning. Three features distinguish it:

1. **Delayed onset.** The crash usually does not arrive during or immediately after activity — it lands **roughly 12–48 hours later** (many clinical sources widen this to 24–72 hours, and occasionally longer). This delay is precisely why patients and clinicians struggle to connect cause and effect.
2. **Disproportion.** The triggering exertion can be trivial relative to the size of the response — a short walk, a phone call, a stressful conversation.
3. **Prolonged, slow recovery.** Recovery takes days to weeks (sometimes longer), far beyond the hours a healthy person needs. Patients describe it as a "crash," "relapse," or "setback."

The triggers explicitly include **cognitive and emotional load and orthostatic stress, not just physical activity** — an important point, because activity logging that only captures "exercise" will miss a large share of real triggers.

### Objective signifiers

PEM has measurable physiological correlates, which is what separates it from subjective fatigue:

- **Two-day cardiopulmonary exercise test (2-day CPET).** The signature finding is that people with ME/CFS / Long COVID cannot reproduce their performance on a second test 24 hours later — output at the ventilatory threshold drops on day two, whereas healthy (even deconditioned) people reproduce their results. This is the closest thing to an objective test for PEM and is used specifically to **distinguish PEM from simple deconditioning**.
- **Muscle pathology after PEM.** Appelman et al. (2024, *Nature Communications*, Amsterdam UMC) showed that in Long COVID patients, muscle biopsies taken *after* an exertion challenge show worsened abnormalities — local metabolic changes, mitochondrial dysfunction, tissue damage — i.e. exertion produces real, measurable muscle injury, not just a feeling.
- **Autonomic / HRV changes.** PEM and the broader illness are tied to dysautonomia — an imbalance toward sympathetic "fight or flight" tone and reduced parasympathetic (vagal) activity, reflected in **low heart-rate variability (HRV)**.

### Leading indicators — can a PEM episode be anticipated?

This is the most actionable and least settled part. Two strands of evidence:

**Physiological thresholds (the stronger strand).** A 2025 wearable study (Ruijgt et al., medRxiv preprint — *not yet peer-reviewed*) continuously measured HR and HRV in 127 Long COVID patients vs. 21 controls, alongside a CPET to find each person's first ventilatory threshold (VT1). Key findings: HRV was persistently lower in patients across daily activity and sleep; and **HRV stayed depressed for ~24 hours after exercise at or above VT1 in patients but not controls**. The authors propose **VT1 can be read practically as a "PEM threshold"** — exertion that pushes heart rate past it tends to be followed by autonomic disturbance. This is the mechanistic basis for **heart-rate-monitor pacing**: patients (and the Workwell Foundation protocol) set an alarm at an individualised HR ceiling and slow down before crossing it. Morning HRV is sometimes framed as a daily "battery meter" for the day's capacity.

**Symptom prodromes (the weaker strand).** A retrospective analysis of 197 ME/CFS patients (Géraud et al., 2021, *J. Clin. Med.*) tested whether **new or atypical symptoms precede a crash**. They found a prodrome in only ~14% of patients, most commonly **mood/affective changes (≈37% of those)** — but, interestingly, patients who *did* get a warning signal had **significantly lower PEM intensity**, suggesting that noticing and acting on early signs may blunt the crash. So a reliable, universal "PEM is coming" symptom does not exist; warning signs are individual and present in a minority — which argues for *personal* pattern detection over a fixed rule.

### Management context

The consensus management approach is **pacing / staying within an "energy envelope"** — a daily budget of physical, cognitive and emotional energy — to avoid the boom-bust cycle. Evidence (e.g. the 2023 scoping review of pacing in ME/CFS, and structured-pacing cohorts in post-COVID syndrome) indicates pacing **reduces the frequency and severity of PEM** and is associated with better function, whereas **graded exercise therapy is contraindicated** where PEM is present because pushing through reliably worsens it. The goal of pacing is explicitly to *minimise* PEM, not eliminate it.

**Takeaway for Q2.** PEM is a delayed (12–48h, up to ~72h), disproportionate, slow-to-recover worsening triggered by physical *or* cognitive/emotional/orthostatic load; it has objective signatures (2-day CPET, post-exertion muscle pathology, suppressed HRV); and the most promising leading indicator is a **personalised heart-rate / HRV threshold** rather than a universal warning symptom.

## 3. Do our data findings show up in the broader research?

Three of our headline findings map onto the literature; here is where they land, kept deliberately brief.

**The two kinds of "down day" — PEM sag vs. infection-driven floor-crash — is a recognised distinction.** Our data separated an everyday ~3 sag (headache-led, the delayed cost of activity) from ≤2 floor-crashes (infection/fever, acute illness, none preceded by logged overexertion). The PAIS literature explicitly describes the illness as **relapsing–remitting**, and the 2025 core-features review names **two separate flare triggers**: *new infections by other pathogens*, and *excessive cognitive or physical effort*. In other words, the literature already treats "a crash because you did too much" and "a crash because you caught something / a relapse fired" as **distinct mechanisms that produce dips in the same person** — exactly the split we found. Our specific observation that the deep ≤2 days cluster with infection rather than with prior overexertion is consistent with that framing; the PEM-type cost shows up as the shallower, headache-led sag instead. (Caveat: the literature makes this split at the group/mechanistic level; we are inferring it from one person's self-report, and "infection" days in our log are self-identified.)

**The 48–72h delayed cost of activity matches the canonical PEM time course.** Our lagged analysis put the cost of activity at **t+2 / t+3 days** (≈48–72h later), not same-day. That sits squarely inside the textbook PEM window of 12–48h with a recognised tail to ~72h. The *shape* we found — activity rides with a good day, the bill arrives two to three days later — is the literature's description of delayed PEM, reproduced in a single time series. Our finding that the cost is a modest ~1-point nudge rather than a full crash, together with **zero windows of three consecutive activity days**, is also consistent with the pacing literature: someone already staying within their envelope keeps PEM small. In effect our data may be showing a *well-paced* PEM signal.

**Headache as the master variable is consistent with headache being a core PAIS symptom.** Headache is marked "very frequent (>50%)" across Long COVID, ME/CFS and Lyme in the cross-PAIS table, and is a commonly listed PEM symptom. So a single dominant, score-driving symptom that happens to be headache is unsurprising — though *which* core symptom dominates is individual (the reviews note the symptom mix is highly person-specific), so headache being *our* lead signal is a personal signature, not a universal law.

**Where the literature can't validate us (and that's fine).** The objective PEM tools — 2-day CPET, post-exertion muscle biopsy, continuous HRV vs. an individual VT1 threshold — are exactly what we *don't* have; our instrument is a daily 1–6 self-rating. That's a strength for coverage and a limit for proof. The literature supports the *patterns* we see (two flare mechanisms, delayed cost, headache centrality); it cannot confirm our specific coefficients, which remain n-of-1 and correlational. The natural bridge, if we ever want it, is the wearable layer already in the v2 plan: Garmin HRV against a personal threshold is precisely the leading-indicator the PEM research points to.

## 4. Infection-like floor-crashes: real infection, or endogenous flare?

A working hypothesis from this project is that the infection-like symptoms in our deep ≤2 crashes — feverishness, sore throat, cough, runny nose — are **more often an overreaction of the system than a genuine new infection caught from outside**. The literature substantially supports a version of this, and in doing so it complicates the clean two-mechanism split of §3.

**The diagnostic criteria already classify these as symptoms of the illness, not new infections.** The Canadian Consensus Criteria list "recurrent flu-like symptoms," "recurrent sore throat," "tender lymph nodes," and "general malaise (flu-like feelings of being ill and feverish)" as *immune manifestations of the chronic condition itself*. Two phrasings are decisive. Feverishness is defined as "with or without low grade fever" — feeling feverish without a measurable temperature is an expected feature, not an anomaly. And these symptoms' "activation **in response to exertion** is abnormal" — i.e. the criteria explicitly recognise that flu-like symptoms can be **exertion-triggered (a PEM phenomenon), not pathogen-triggered**.

**The mechanism is "sickness behaviour" running aberrantly.** Sickness behaviour is the evolutionarily conserved brain programme — fever, aches, sore-throat feeling, malaise, withdrawal — that pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β, TNF-α, IL-6) switch on during a real infection to enforce rest. In ME/CFS and Long COVID this programme appears to fire chronically or disproportionately, driven by immune dysregulation and neuroinflammation. Critically, the neuroimmunology literature finds **cytokines need not be detectable in the blood to produce sickness symptoms** — they are sensed via the vagus nerve and the response is generated centrally. That is, fairly precisely, "an overreaction of the system rather than a real infection," and it dovetails with the interoception framing in §1–2: the brain mis-reading and over-amplifying internal signals — a false alarm.

**The fever and the upper-airway symptoms have their own non-infectious explanations.** The subjective-fever component maps onto **thermoregulatory dysautonomia** — hypothalamic/autonomic misfiring producing feverishness, sweats and hot/cold spells without elevated temperature. The congestion/runny-nose/throat component maps onto **mast cell activation** (estimated in ~27% of Long COVID), which produces cold- or allergy-like symptoms that mimic the onset of an infection without one.

**The complication — latent virus reactivation.** EBV and HHV-6 reactivate at elevated rates in Long COVID and ME/CFS, often triggered by stress or exertion, and EBV reactivation correlates with fatigue, cognitive symptoms and persistent cough. This is a genuine middle ground: *real* viral activity, but **internally reactivated by dysregulation rather than a fresh external infection**. So the sharp question "real infection or not?" is better posed as **external new pathogen vs. internally-driven immune/sickness flare** — and the hypothesis is mostly right about the latter dominating. (Caveat: immune dysfunction in PAIS can also genuinely raise susceptibility to real infections, and symptoms alone can't separate the two — hence "more often than not," not "always.")

**What this means for our model.** It partially dissolves the clean two-mechanism split of §3. If flu-like floor-crashes are often exertion- or stress-triggered immune flares, then "infection crash" and "PEM crash" are not fully independent mechanisms — a severe flu-like crash can *be* a form of PEM/immune flare rather than a separate external cause. In our data, 6 of 26 crash episodes were labelled "infection" and none was preceded by logged overexertion; some of those may be endogenous flares reasonably labelled as infection because they *felt* fluey. The better mental model is a **spectrum of immune/sickness-response flares with different triggers** (external pathogen, reactivated latent virus, exertion- or stress-driven cytokine flare, mast-cell activation), not two clean boxes. This is testable with the v2 wearable layer: a true external infection tends to show a *sustained* resting-HR elevation and a measurable multi-day fever, whereas an endogenous sickness-behaviour flare would more likely show the autonomic/HRV disturbance without a sustained real fever. The same signal that could anticipate PEM (§2) could therefore also help *classify* a crash after the fact.

**Caution.** None of this is grounds for dismissing real infections — anything with a sustained high fever still warrants normal care. The value here is recognising the *pattern* across many episodes, not self-diagnosing any single one.
